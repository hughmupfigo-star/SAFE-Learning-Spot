Page 01 / 04 · The Paper Trail

The documents that prove you exist.

A handful of documents quietly run your whole financial life — and knowing what they are, what they're for, and how to get hold of your own is the first piece of real independence.

Three documents do most of the heavy lifting in the UK: your birth certificate (proof of who you are), your National Insurance (NI) number (your personal tax ID, which usually arrives by post just before your 16th birthday), and a form of photo ID (a passport, or a provisional driving licence from 15 years and 9 months). Almost everything official — a bank account, a first job, a tax record — sits on top of these.

Here's the empowering bit: these are yours. You're entitled to your own birth certificate — anyone can order a copy from the General Register Office on gov.uk for a small fee. If you've lost your NI number, you can recover it through HMRC. Knowing this means no one can use ‘I've got your documents’ as a way to control you.

If someone is withholding your documents

Sometimes a parent or guardian keeps a young person's documents from them as a way to keep control — and if that's happening to you, it's worth knowing it has a name: it's a form of financial control, and it isn't okay. You have routes that don't depend on them: order your own birth certificate, retrieve your NI number from HMRC, and talk it through, free and in confidence, with Citizens Advice or Childline (0800 1111). You're not stuck.

A quick honesty note: some documents do still need a parent's involvement under 16 (a first passport, for example), and that's the law, not a loophole. Where that's the case, a trusted adult — a relative, teacher, or mentor — can often help. The goal isn't to go around everyone; it's to know what's genuinely yours to handle.

Your documents are your right, not a privilege someone grants you. Knowing that is where financial confidence starts.

Page 02 / 04 · Digital Security

Lock the digital doors.

In 2026 your money lives behind your email, your phone, and your passwords far more than behind any filing cabinet. Securing those is a genuine life skill — and it protects you from strangers and, sometimes, from people closer to home.

Your email is the master key: reset a password almost anywhere and the link lands in your inbox. So that's where security starts. Use a strong, unique password you've told no one, turn on two-factor authentication (2FA) — the code-from-an-app or text step that stops anyone logging in with just your password — and make sure the recovery phone number and backup email are yours, not someone else's from years ago.

Then treat your phone and accounts the same way: a PIN or fingerprint lock, 2FA on anything with money attached, and an awareness of who else might know your passwords. None of this is paranoid; it's the exact hygiene every adult is told to use. Doing it at sixteen just means you're ahead.

Privacy isn't the same as secrecy

Wanting your own private email and secure accounts doesn't mean you're hiding something dodgy — it's the same boundary every adult has. You're allowed a space that's yours. If someone in your life insists on having all your passwords and monitoring everything, that's worth noticing; healthy support doesn't require surveillance. Keeping your own digital doors locked is a normal, healthy boundary, not a betrayal.

Your email is the master key to everything else. Protect it like it's the front door — because it basically is.

Page 03 / 04 · Knowing the Law

What you can actually do at your age.

A lot of the confusion around teens and money comes from not knowing the real rules. Knowing them — honestly, including the limits — is what lets you act with confidence instead of guessing.

In the UK there's no single ‘become an adult early’ button like the ‘emancipation’ you might've seen in American films. Instead, rights arrive in stages: you can usually open certain bank accounts and do casual paid work in your teens, you get more financial independence around 16, and full adult contract rights at 18. The law deliberately limits what under-18s can sign up to — and, importantly, that's there to protect you from being held to deals you don't fully understand, not to keep you down.

That's why so many apps and banks say ‘18+’. It's not them deciding you're not capable; it's that a contract with a minor is hard to enforce, so they avoid it. Understanding this flips your frustration into strategy: instead of trying to dodge the rules, you learn the legitimate routes that already exist for someone your age.

Get the real picture, free

Because the details depend on exactly how old you are and where you live in the UK, the smartest move isn't to trust a random video — it's to get tailored, free, confidential guidance. Citizens Advice, and services like The Mix (for under-25s), will talk you through your actual rights without judgement. Knowing precisely where you stand is its own kind of power.

The rules that limit what you can sign aren't there to hold you back — they're there so no one can trap a teenager in a contract. Know them, and use them.
