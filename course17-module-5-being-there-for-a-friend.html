Page 01 / 04 · Spotting It

Noticing when a friend is sinking.

If you need help right now

Call or text 988 (Suicide & Crisis Lifeline) · text HOME to 741741 (Crisis Text Line) · call 911 if you’re in immediate danger. Free, confidential, 24/7.

When school support is cut, young people end up being each other's front line. You don't need training to notice a friend slipping — just attention and the willingness to ask.

The signs in a friend look a lot like your own amber lights: pulling away from the group, going quiet or unusually flat, giving up on things they used to care about, big changes in sleep, eating, or mood, darker jokes or posts, or just a gut sense that something's off. You don't have to be sure. ‘You've seemed a bit different lately — are you okay?’ is always a fair thing to ask.

Take darker comments seriously, even when wrapped in a joke. If a friend says something like ‘everyone would be better off without me’ or ‘I just want it to stop,’ that's not a moment to laugh along or let slide — it's a moment to gently get real: ‘That sounds heavy. Are you actually okay? I'm here.’ Caring enough to ask directly is never the wrong move.

Asking is not ‘putting ideas in their head’

A common fear is that asking a friend directly — even ‘are you thinking about hurting yourself?’ — might plant the idea. It doesn't. Research is clear: asking directly and kindly tends to bring relief, not harm. It tells them they're not alone and gives them permission to be honest. The dangerous thing is silence, not the question.

You don't need a diagnosis to check on a friend. ‘You've seemed off — are you okay?’ is enough, and it can mean everything.

Page 02 / 04 · What to Say

You don’t need the right words.

People freeze up helping a friend because they're scared of saying the wrong thing. Good news: being there is mostly about listening, not fixing — and ‘I'm here’ beats any clever speech.

The most helpful things are simple: ‘That sounds really hard.’ ‘I'm really glad you told me.’ ‘You're not alone in this.’ ‘I'm here.’ Then mostly listen. You don't have to solve their problem, give advice, or have answers — in fact, rushing to fix it often makes people feel unheard. Letting them be heard, without judging or minimising, is the gift.

Try to avoid the well-meant lines that accidentally shut people down: ‘just think positive,’ ‘others have it worse,’ ‘you'll be fine,’ ‘it's all in your head.’ They're meant kindly and tend to land as ‘your feelings aren't valid.’ When in doubt, swap advice for curiosity: ‘Do you want to talk about it?’ ‘What's been the hardest part?’ ‘How can I help?’

Listen more than you talk

Your job as a friend isn't to be a therapist — it's to be a warm, steady presence who listens and helps them reach real support. You can't and shouldn't carry their whole weight, and you don't have to know what to do. Just being someone who hears them without flinching is doing a great deal. The next page is the most important part: not carrying it alone.

You can't say the perfect thing, because there isn't one. ‘That sounds really hard, and I'm here’ is already the right thing.

Page 03 / 04 · Not Alone

Be the bridge, not the whole rescue.

Supporting a friend matters enormously — and it has limits you must respect, for their safety and yours. You are a bridge to help, not the help itself. That distinction protects you both.

If a friend is in real danger — talking about ending their life, with a plan, or you believe they're not safe — that is bigger than friendship can hold, and the caring move is to get an adult or a crisis line involved now, even if they asked you to keep it secret. Telling a trusted adult, calling or texting 988 together, or texting HOME to 741741 is not betrayal — it's how you actually help someone you love stay alive. A secret is not worth more than a friend.

For the everyday weight, gently point them toward real support too: ‘Have you got someone older you can talk to?’ ‘Want to look up a free helpline together?’ You can walk beside them — sit with them while they text 988, help them find a counselor — without becoming their only lifeline. Helping them reach help is often the most useful thing you'll do.

Protect your own oxygen, too

Carrying a friend's pain can quietly drain you, especially if you're struggling yourself. You're allowed limits — ‘I love you and I want to help, and I also need us to get someone else in on this.’ Look after your own mind while you support theirs; check in with your own trusted adult, and use this course's tools on yourself. You can't pour from an empty cup, and you don't have to.

If a friend isn't safe, telling someone is not breaking their trust — it's keeping them here. A secret is never worth a life.
