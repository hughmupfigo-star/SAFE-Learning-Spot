Page 01 / 06 · The Frame

Why vampire is the right word.

The word sounds dramatic. It sounds like an exaggeration, a hyperbole reserved for people you want to villainise. But the word is exact — because what's happening is exact.

An energy vampire is not a bad person. Many of them are warm, funny, generous-seeming. Some of them genuinely love you. The dynamic is not about their character. It is about what their presence does to your reserves.

You can walk away from a kind person and feel emptied. You can walk away from a difficult person and feel sharpened. The label has nothing to do with how the person presents. It has everything to do with the energetic transaction underneath.

Three things vampires are not

They are not always cruel. Most are pleasant. The cruelty is in the imbalance, not the manner.

They are not always aware. Many have no idea they're doing it. The pattern is so old, so woven into how they relate, that they experience it as normal connection. Their unawareness does not make the drain less real.

They are not always strangers. The most efficient vampires are family, partners, oldest friends — because the access is established and the script is rehearsed. You stopped noticing the cost years ago.

A person can love you and still feed on you. These are not opposites. Untangling that is most of the work.
Scenario · Sit with this

Someone in your life is unmistakably loving — and you also dread their phone calls.

You feel guilty for the dread. You tell yourself you should be grateful. But the dread persists, and after every call you feel slightly worse about yourself than before.

Page 02 / 06 · The Archetypes

The four archetypes.

Vampires come in patterns. Once you can name the pattern, you can stop confusing it with personality and start treating it as information. These four show up most often.

Archetype 01

The Crisis Generator

Life around them is always a five-alarm fire. Every week brings a new emergency that requires your immediate attention, your strategic thinking, your emotional labour. The crises rotate, but the structure never does — and the crises somehow never resolve in a way that makes them less likely next time.

How they feel
  • Your phone buzzes and your stomach drops before you've read the message
  • You've rehearsed advice you've already given them three times
  • When things are calm in their life, you feel suspicious, not relieved
Archetype 02

The Emotional Auditor

They monitor your tone, your delays, your perceived warmth. Every interaction comes with a quiet evaluation — were you attentive enough, present enough, performative enough in your affection. The exchange isn't relational; it's transactional, but disguised as closeness.

How they feel
  • You catch yourself drafting messages multiple times to get the tone right
  • You apologise for small things you haven't actually done wrong
  • You feel scored rather than seen
Archetype 03

The Subtle Diminisher

Compliments arrive with knives in them. Your wins are met with a small note of caution, a warning, a "be careful you don't get ahead of yourself." Your losses are met with something that almost sounds like satisfaction. Spend too much time with them and your own life begins to feel smaller — not by accusation, by atmosphere.

How they feel
  • You hide good news from them by instinct
  • You leave conversations questioning things you were certain about an hour earlier
  • You feel embarrassed by ambitions you used to speak about freely
Archetype 04

The Gravity Well

Their unhappiness is the centre around which the relationship orbits. Every conversation routes back to their suffering, their disappointments, their unfairnesses. They are not necessarily complaining — they are processing, endlessly, with you as the unpaid container. The processing never produces change. The container never empties.

How they feel
  • You leave conversations heavy without knowing what you discussed
  • You realise you haven't said anything about your own life in three meetings
  • You feel responsible for their mood in ways you didn't agree to
A person can fit more than one archetype. A relationship can shift between them. The names are not labels for people — they are names for what is being done to you.
Scenario · Sit with this

You read the archetypes and a specific person comes to mind for every one.

Not just one person — four different people, each fitting a different pattern with uncomfortable precision. You feel a strange mixture of vindication and grief.

Page 03 / 06 · The Match

How they found you.

Vampires do not select randomly. They are drawn to specific signals — and the signals are usually wounds you didn't know you were broadcasting.

This is not your fault. Nobody chooses what their nervous system advertises. But understanding what made you legible to them is the only way to stop being legible to the next one.

The signals that draw them

The over-explainer. If you were raised in a household where your emotions had to be justified — where you couldn't simply feel something, you had to defend feeling it — you learned to over-explain yourself. Vampires read this instantly. It tells them your boundaries are negotiable, because you've already trained yourself to negotiate them.

The compulsive fixer. If love was conditional on usefulness — if you became valued because you solved things, smoothed things, anticipated needs — then someone constantly producing problems is, to you, an opportunity to be loved. Crisis generators recognise this in you within minutes.

The chronic apologiser. If you grew up walking on eggshells, you developed pre-emptive apology as a survival skill. To an emotional auditor, this reads as "this person will accept blame even when none is owed." Which is exactly the system they need.

The under-asserted. If your "no" was punished as a child — sulked at, argued with, treated as betrayal — you learned to soften every refusal into a maybe. Diminishers and gravity wells thrive in this softness. There's no clean edge to push against, so they keep pushing.

The wound is not the problem. The wound is the door. Until you can see the door, you cannot decide who walks through it.
Scenario · Sit with this

You recognise yourself in at least one of the four signals.

There's a temptation to either blame the version of you that learned that pattern, or to blame the parent / caregiver / partner who installed it. Both temptations feel righteous. Both keep you in the wound.

Page 04 / 06 · Real Time

The drain, while it's happening.

One of the strangest things about energy extraction is that you usually cannot identify it while it's underway. You only notice afterwards — sometimes hours, sometimes days — and by then the language to describe what happened is gone.

This is not because you are slow. It is because extraction interferes with the exact cognitive systems you would need to detect it. The freeze response, the fog, the loss of language — these are not failures of will. They are predictable responses to a specific kind of pressure.

The four states of being drained

State one — the fog. Your thinking softens. Things you knew clearly an hour ago feel uncertain. You can't quite remember why you were upset, or whether you had a point, or what you wanted to say. The fog is the first sign. It is also the most easily mistaken for being "in a bad mood."

State two — the over-functioning. You begin doing more than the situation requires. You apologise. You smooth. You laugh at things that aren't funny. You manage their mood instead of registering your own. This is the body trying to end the discomfort by being more accommodating, on the unconscious theory that accommodation will make it stop.

State three — the freeze. Speech becomes effortful. The thing you meant to say evaporates the moment you open your mouth. You agree to things you don't agree with. You forget the limits you set. Afterwards, you'll wonder why you didn't just say no — but in the moment, "no" was simply not accessible.

State four — the residue. The conversation ends. They leave or hang up. And then a quiet sickness settles in — somewhere between shame and exhaustion. You replay it. You catch up to what was actually happening. You find the words you couldn't reach. This delay is the most reliable diagnostic you have.

If clarity arrives only after they leave the room, the room is the problem — not your mind.
Scenario · Sit with this

You're mid-conversation with someone and you feel the fog descending.

Your thoughts are getting slower. You catch yourself apologising for something you didn't do. You feel yourself becoming smaller in the chair. You have maybe thirty seconds before the freeze takes over.

Page 05 / 06 · The Quiet Move

Seeing them, without needing them to know they've been seen.

There is a strong urge, once you can name the pattern, to confront the person doing it. To make them understand. To extract an apology, an acknowledgement, a moment of recognition.

This urge is understandable. It is also, almost always, a trap.

Vampires are unusually skilled at managing accusations. They have a lifetime of practice deflecting, reframing, and turning the conversation around so that you end up being the one who apologises. The confrontation is rarely the move that frees you. The confrontation is usually the move that costs you the last of your reserves trying to be understood.

What sovereignty looks like instead

You can see them without telling them you see them. The recognition is for you. It is the internal shift that allows you to stop performing, stop over-functioning, stop apologising pre-emptively. Their behaviour does not need to change for your relationship to change — because the relationship lives inside your responses to it.

You can love them and reduce your exposure. Recognition does not require excommunication. You can take fewer calls. You can shorten visits. You can stop volunteering information that will be used against you later. None of this requires an announcement.

You can let the relationship show you what it's made of. When you stop over-giving, the dynamic adjusts itself. Some relationships will quietly improve as you stop oversupplying — the person, no longer fed automatically, will either step up or step back. Others will not survive your sovereignty. That information is itself the gift.

You can keep the door closed without slamming it. The fantasy of the final confrontation — the email, the speech, the dramatic exit — is almost always more about your own catharsis than about the relationship. Catharsis is real. It is also expensive. There is usually a quieter way.

You don't owe anyone a tour of how you arrived at your new boundaries. The boundary is the message.
Scenario · Sit with this

You've recognised a long-term friend as a Subtle Diminisher.

You want them to know. You want them to apologise for years of small undermining. The desire to be acknowledged feels almost unbearable.

Practice this week

Withhold one piece of information you would normally have over-shared. Notice what happens — in them, and more importantly, in you.
