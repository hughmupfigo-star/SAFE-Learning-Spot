Page 01 / 04 · The Switch-On

Turning 18, on purpose.

For most people, turning 18 financially just sort of happens. For you, it can be a plan you execute — flipping on full control the moment you're entitled to it.

At 18 the limits from Module 1 lift: you can open a full adult bank account, sign contracts in your own name, and manage your money with complete independence. The difference between a smooth transition and a messy one is simply whether you saw it coming. A short checklist, ready in advance, turns a vague milestone into a clean switch-on.

A sensible day-one list: open your own adult account (or upgrade your youth one), make sure your wages or income are going into an account only you control, update your address and details everywhere they matter, and register to vote — which, as the next page explains, quietly helps more than you'd think.

If you've shared control, this is when you reclaim it

If, while you were under 18, money came through a joint or parent-operated account, turning 18 is the natural, no-drama moment to move things into accounts that are solely yours. It doesn't have to be a confrontation — it's just the normal next step of becoming independent. If that transition feels unsafe or complicated, Citizens Advice can talk you through doing it calmly and properly.

Turning 18 financially can be something that happens to you, or something you do on purpose. Have the checklist ready.

Page 02 / 04 · Credit, Carefully

Building credit without the trap.

‘Credit’ is one of the most useful and most dangerous tools in adult money. Built slowly and on purpose, it opens doors; rushed or misused, it's a trap. The good news: building it safely is genuinely simple.

Your ‘credit history’ is just a track record of how reliably you handle borrowing — and at 18 you start with a blank page, which is normal. The safe way to build it is boring and effective: register to vote (lenders use the electoral roll to confirm you're real), and consider one starter credit option used gently — for example, paying for something small and paying it off in full, on time, every single time. On time and in full is the entire secret.

The trap is the opposite: high-cost borrowing dressed up as easy and instant. Payday loans, some ‘buy now, pay later’ schemes, and anything promising cash with no checks tend to carry brutal costs or quietly train bad habits. The rule of thumb: if borrowing is being pushed at you as effortless and urgent, slow right down.

Credit is a tool, not free money

The mental shift that protects you for life: a credit card or loan isn't extra money you have — it's money you're borrowing and must pay back, usually with interest. Treat it as a tool for building a record and handling timing, not as a way to afford things you can't. Used that way, credit is a quiet superpower. Misunderstood, it's how people get stuck. Start with the right model in your head.

Pay it off on time and in full, every time. That one habit is 90% of good credit — and it never stops being true.

Page 03 / 04 · If Home Isn't Safe

A calm plan for the worst case.

Most of this course is about thriving. This last page is about safety — because every young person deserves a plan for the rare, hard situation where home stops being somewhere they can stay.

If you're ever told to leave, or it becomes unsafe to stay, knowing a few things in advance turns panic into action. Keep your key documents (or knowledge of how to get copies) and access to your own account secure and reachable. Know that as a young person you have rights, and there are people whose actual job is to help — you are not on your own, even if it feels that way.

Write the lifelines down somewhere you can reach them: in the UK, Shelter (housing and homelessness), Centrepoint (youth homelessness), Childline 0800 1111 (any time, for anyone under 19), and Citizens Advice (your rights and options). Local councils also have a legal duty to help young people who are homeless or at risk. Knowing these names before you ever need them is its own kind of safety.

Asking for help is strength, full stop

If any of this is close to home for you right now, please hear this clearly: needing help is not a weakness or a failure, and what's happening isn't your fault. The bravest, strongest thing — the genuinely sovereign thing — is to reach out to one of those services and let someone help you. They've supported many people your age through exactly this, and they can help you too.

Knowing who to call before you ever need them turns a crisis into a plan. That knowledge is something no one can take from you.
