Page 01 / 04 · The Gendered Pattern

The variable prevention won’t name.

Across almost every society and almost every form, the perpetration of serious violence is overwhelmingly concentrated among young males. Naming this is not blame; refusing to name it is a failure of analysis.

Young men are vastly over-represented as both perpetrators and victims of lethal violence worldwide. This is one of the most robust findings in the field — and yet much prevention focuses on teaching potential victims to stay safe rather than engaging the demographic doing most of the harming. This module flips the script: if you want to prevent violence, you have to engage boys and young men and the norms shaping them, not just protect everyone else from them.

The link runs directly into gender-based violence (GBV). Youth violence and violence against women and girls are not separate problems with separate causes; they share roots in the same systems of masculine status, control, and entitlement. A young man's violence toward a rival and his violence toward a partner are often expressions of the same underlying script about what being a man requires. Treating them as unrelated misses the common driver.

Biology is not the explanation

The over-representation of young males is not a simple story of testosterone or fixed nature; rates of male violence vary enormously across cultures and historical periods, which they could not do if biology were destiny. What varies is the norms — what a given society teaches boys that manhood demands. That variation is the hopeful part: norms that were taught can be untaught, which is exactly where gender-transformative work intervenes.

If a single variable predicted most of a disease, no epidemiologist would refuse to study it. Prevention that won't engage young men and masculinity is prevention with its eyes closed.

Page 02 / 04 · Masculinities & Honour

The scripts that make harm feel mandatory.

To change the pattern you have to understand the script. In many contexts, dominant masculinity ties a young man's worth to toughness, dominance, sexual conquest, and a readiness to answer disrespect with force — making violence not a deviation but a performance of normal manhood.

‘Hyper-masculinity’ describes an exaggerated version of these expectations: status earned through dominance, vulnerability treated as weakness, and honour defended through violence. Where a patriarchal honour code prevails, an insult or a challenge isn't a minor social friction — it is a threat to a young man's standing that the code demands he answer, often violently, or lose his place in the masculine hierarchy. The violence is, in the actor's world, the rule-following thing to do.

These codes vary across cultures, but the structure recurs: manhood as a precarious status that must be continually proven and can be lost, with violence as the proof of last resort. This is why ‘honour’ contexts, gang reputation systems, and online masculine subcultures can look so different yet share a grammar. Understanding the specific script in a given context — what counts as disrespect, what restores honour — is the prerequisite for changing it.

Precarious manhood as a driver

A useful research lens is ‘precarious manhood’: the finding that manhood is widely seen as hard-won and easily lost, generating anxiety that can be discharged through dominance and aggression. The intervention implication is precise — if violence is a way of proving a manhood that feels under threat, then offering boys other, secure routes to status, respect, and identity removes the need for the proof. You change the scoreboard, not just the player.

Where manhood is a status you can lose at any insult, violence becomes a man's way of paying a debt the culture invented. Cancel the debt and the payment stops making sense.

Page 03 / 04 · Gender-Transformative Work

Changing the norms, not just the behaviour.

The frontier of prevention is ‘gender-transformative’ programming: approaches that actively engage boys and young men to question and dismantle the very norms of masculinity that script violence, rather than merely managing its symptoms.

Gender-transformative programmes — exemplified by approaches such as Promundo's ‘Program H’ and allied work — go beyond telling young men not to be violent. They create structured spaces to critically examine masculinity itself: where their ideas of manhood came from, what those ideas cost them and others, and what alternative, non-violent versions of strength and respect look like. The aim is to shift the norm, so that non-violence becomes the masculine thing, not the emasculating thing.

The evidence base, while still developing, is encouraging: well-designed gender-transformative work has been associated with reduced support for violence, including partner violence, and healthier relationship norms among young men. Critically, these programmes treat boys and young men as allies and agents in change rather than as problems to be contained — a stance that both respects them and tends to produce better engagement than shame-based messaging.

Why ‘don't be violent’ isn't enough

Simple prohibition fails because it leaves the underlying status system intact: if manhood still requires dominance, you've asked a young man to surrender his standing with nothing offered in return. Transformative work succeeds by replacing the script — offering secure, respected identities that don't run on aggression. It changes what manhood means, which is the only durable way to change what manhood does.

You cannot subtract violence from a definition of manhood that requires it. You have to offer a definition that doesn't — and let young men help write it.
