Page 01 / 06 · The Architecture

Energy is not a metaphor.

Before anything else in this course can land, the word "energy" needs to be rescued from the two industries that have made it nearly useless. The wellness industry treats energy as a mystical substance — something that flows through chakras, can be cleansed with sage, and responds to crystals placed on specific meridians. The sceptic industry treats energy as a word used by people who cannot think clearly. Neither is helpful. Neither is what this course means when it uses the word.

Energy, in this course, refers to four measurable, felt, finite resources that your body produces and distributes every day. They are not mystical. They are not imaginary. They are the actual material of your daily life — the substance you spend in every conversation, every decision, every interaction, every hour of your waking existence. When this course says someone is "harvesting your energy," it means something specific, observable, and felt. It means they are drawing on one or more of these four resources without your conscious agreement.

The four channels

Attention.

The first and most easily extracted resource. Your attention is not infinite — it is produced in a limited quantity each day and it is spent every time you focus on something. When someone captures your attention without adding to your life, they are drawing from this channel. When you leave an interaction feeling mentally scattered, you have been depleted through attention. The depletion is real. It is not a feeling. It is a measurable reduction in your capacity to focus on what matters to you for the remainder of the day.

Nervous system capacity.

The second channel, and the one most people cannot name. Your nervous system has a daily budget for activation — for being alert, responsive, on-guard. Every interaction that requires you to manage someone's mood, anticipate their reaction, or calibrate your behaviour to their emotional weather draws from this channel. When you feel exhausted after a conversation that was "perfectly fine" on the surface, the exhaustion is not imaginary. Your nervous system budget has been spent, and the spending was invisible because it happened below the level of conscious thought.

Emotional bandwidth.

The third channel. Your capacity to feel, process, and hold emotions — your own and other people's — is finite. It replenishes, but it replenishes slowly, and the replenishment requires conditions (rest, solitude, safety) that many adults do not regularly have. When someone deposits their emotional distress into you regularly, without invitation, they are drawing from a channel that you need for your own emotional processing. The cost is not that you heard their feelings. The cost is that you now have less capacity to feel your own.

Sexual and creative presence.

The fourth channel, and the one this course will spend the most time with. Your sexual energy and your creative energy are not separate systems — they draw from the same well. The life force that produces desire, imagination, creative output, physical vitality, and the felt sense of being alive in your own body is a single resource. When this resource is being extracted — through unwanted sexual attention, through relationships that treat your body as a commodity, through cultural programming that converts your sexuality into a performance for consumption — the cost is not only sexual. It is creative, physical, and existential. The depletion reaches into every part of your life.

These four channels are not metaphors for something else. They are the actual substance of what you have available each day. When this course talks about energy sovereignty, it means the governance of these four resources — who has access to them, under what conditions, and whether the access was chosen or extracted.

You have been told that energy is either mystical nonsense or a vague feeling. It is neither. It is four finite resources that your body produces daily, that other people can draw from, and that you have the right to govern. The governance is the sovereignty.

The conversation was normal. Nobody was rude. You laughed several times. Yet you are now lying on the sofa unable to think clearly, unable to start the evening you had planned, feeling as though something has been taken from you that you cannot name.

Page 02 / 06 · The Felt Sense

How energy actually moves through you.

If the four channels are the architecture, the felt sense is the instrument. Your body already knows the difference between expansion and depletion — between being with someone who contributes to your energy and being with someone who draws from it. The body has always known. What it has not had, until now, is language precise enough to convert the knowing into a diagnostic you can use in real time.

Energy does not move through you abstractly. It moves through specific physical pathways that produce specific sensations. The sensations have been there your whole life. You have been feeling them after every interaction, every encounter, every hour spent with another person. What you have not been doing is reading them as information. This page teaches you to read them.

The felt sense of expansion

When you are with someone who is contributing to your energy rather than extracting it, the body produces a recognisable set of responses. The breathing deepens without effort. The shoulders drop. The chest opens slightly — not dramatically, just a degree or two of physical space that was not there before. You find yourself thinking clearly, sometimes more clearly than you think alone. Ideas arrive. The felt sense is of having been given something, though nothing material has been exchanged.

After an expansive interaction, you have more energy than you arrived with — not dramatically, but measurably. You can think. You can start things. You have momentum. The conversation did not cost you. It generated something, and the something is available to you for hours after the interaction has ended. This is what chosen energy exchange produces. Both parties leave richer than they arrived.

The felt sense of depletion

When someone is drawing from your energy without contributing to it, the body produces a different set of responses. The breathing becomes shallow. The shoulders rise. There is a tightening across the chest or the stomach — subtle, often not noticed until after the interaction is over. You may find your thoughts becoming circular, scattered, difficult to land. The felt sense is of something having been removed, though you cannot always identify what was taken or when the taking occurred.

After a depleting interaction, you have less energy than you arrived with. The deficit shows up as brain fog, as physical tiredness that is not proportional to what you actually did, as the inability to start things you had been looking forward to. The depletion is not imaginary. It is the felt signature of one or more of the four channels having been drawn from without your active participation in the exchange.

The critical distinction

Expansion and depletion are not about whether you liked the person. You can enjoy an interaction and still be depleted by it. You can find someone mildly tedious and still be expanded by the exchange. The diagnostic is not your opinion of the interaction. The diagnostic is your body's state after the interaction. The opinion can be wrong — the interaction was fun, therefore it must have been good for you. The body is rarely wrong. The body has no investment in the social narrative of what should have been energising. It simply reports what occurred.

Learning to read the body after interactions — consistently, without overriding its signals with social interpretation — is the foundational skill of this course. Everything that follows depends on your ability to feel the difference between expansion and depletion in real time, and to trust what you feel over what you think you should feel.

Your body has been filing reports after every human interaction you have ever had. You have not been reading them. The reports are accurate. The reports are still available. The reading begins now.

You leave a conversation with someone you genuinely like, and notice that your body is registering depletion — shallow breath, scattered thoughts, a vague tiredness.

Your mind says: that was a good conversation, I like this person, I enjoyed myself. Your body says: something was taken. The two signals are in conflict.

Page 03 / 06 · The Two Directions

Chosen exchange versus extraction.

Energy exchange between people is not inherently harmful. It is the basic currency of all human interaction — every conversation, every touch, every shared silence involves an exchange of energy through one or more of the four channels. The question is not whether energy is being exchanged. The question is whether the exchange was chosen or extracted.

This distinction is the structural spine of the entire course. Chosen exchange is what healthy relationships run on. Both parties are aware, at some level, that energy is moving between them. Both parties have consented to the exchange, even if the consent is implicit rather than declared. Both parties leave the interaction with something — not always equal, not always balanced, but something. The exchange is mutual, even when it is asymmetric.

Extraction is what happens when energy moves in one direction without the other party's conscious participation. The taking is often invisible — neither party may be fully aware of what is occurring. The person extracting is frequently unconscious of the extraction. The person being extracted from is frequently unable to name what has been taken. The invisibility is the mechanism. If the extraction were visible, it would be refused.

Chosen exchange
  • You chose to give your attention, and it was received with care
  • You held someone's emotional distress because you had the capacity and they asked — implicitly or explicitly
  • The interaction cost you something and you would choose to pay it again
  • Both parties left with something, though not necessarily the same thing
  • The exchange has a natural end point — it does not continue extracting after the interaction is over
  • Your body, afterwards, registers tiredness that is proportional to what you gave
Extraction
  • Your attention was captured before you chose to give it — through crisis, performance, or manipulation of your empathy
  • Someone's emotional distress was deposited in you without invitation, often disguised as conversation
  • The interaction cost you something and you did not consent to the spending
  • One party left lighter; the other left heavier. The direction was consistent
  • You find yourself still processing the interaction hours later — the taking continues after the encounter has ended
  • Your body, afterwards, registers a tiredness disproportionate to anything that visibly occurred

Read both columns slowly. The left column costs you and feels clean. The right column costs you and feels residual. The residual quality — the sense that the interaction is still taking from you hours later, that your processing capacity has been occupied by something you did not choose to carry — is the felt signature of extraction. It is not the same as the clean tiredness of a chosen exchange. It is the tiredness of having been drawn from without consent.

Why this distinction matters for the rest of the course

The entire course operates on this distinction. Module Two examines the specific tactics of extraction. Module Three examines how extraction became normalised, particularly in the domain of sexuality. Module Four reclaims what sacred sexuality actually means when separated from both the wellness industry and the morality industry. Module Five treats the body as territory. Module Six restores the depleted reserves. All of this work depends on your capacity, right now, to feel the difference between chosen exchange and extraction — in real time, in your own body, with the accuracy that only your nervous system can provide.

You have been taught that generosity means giving without counting. The truth reversal: generosity is giving from choice. Extraction is having it taken before you chose. The difference between these two is the difference between sovereignty and depletion.
Scenario · Sit with this

A friend calls, upset, and talks for forty-five minutes about a recurring crisis you have heard about many times before.

You care about them. The distress is real. But you notice, midway through the call, that your breathing has become shallow, your thoughts are scattering, and your evening plans have already been quietly abandoned. The call was not requested — they called, you answered, and the extraction began before you had time to choose whether to give.

Page 04 / 06 · The Mechanism

Why some people deplete you and others expand you.

The explanation is not personality. It is not vibes. It is not that some people are toxic and others are safe. The explanation is structural — and the structure, once seen, demystifies the entire experience of feeling inexplicably drained by certain people and inexplicably energised by others.

Three mechanisms determine whether an interaction expands or depletes you. All three operate below conscious awareness. All three are felt in the body before they are understood by the mind. Learning to read these mechanisms in real time is the practical skill this module exists to develop.

Mechanism 01

Directionality of attention.

In an expanding interaction, attention flows in both directions. You are attending to them and they are attending to you. The flow is not always equal — sometimes you are listening more, sometimes they are — but over the course of the interaction, both parties have received attention and both parties have given it. The flow, across time, is mutual.

In a depleting interaction, attention flows in one direction. You are attending to them. They are receiving your attention without reciprocating. The flow may be disguised — they may appear to be asking you questions, but the questions are calibrated to redirect the conversation back to themselves. Or they may not bother with the disguise at all, simply occupying the entire conversation with their own material. The unidirectional flow of attention is the simplest and most common mechanism of depletion. It is also the easiest to detect, once you know to look for it.

Mechanism 02

Nervous system regulation transfer.

Some people use you, unconsciously, to regulate their own nervous system. Their anxiety, when deposited in you through conversation, becomes more manageable for them — because it is no longer only inside them, it is now distributed between two nervous systems. The transfer is rarely visible. It shows up as you, after the interaction, carrying a low-grade anxiety that was not yours before the conversation began.

This is not a mystical process. It is co-regulation operating in one direction. Co-regulation is the natural process by which two nervous systems influence each other. In a healthy interaction, co-regulation is mutual — both systems stabilise. In a depleting interaction, one system stabilises by transferring its dysregulation to the other. You leave calmer for them and more agitated for you. The transfer was the purpose of the interaction, even though neither party named it.

Mechanism 03

Identity positioning.

Some interactions deplete you not through what is taken but through the position you are placed in. The person across from you — through tone, assumption, framing — positions you as the audience, the helper, the support act, the less interesting one. You find yourself, without having chosen it, playing a role that requires the suppression of parts of yourself. The suppression costs energy. The role costs energy. The slow disappearance of your own presence, in order to maintain theirs, is a form of extraction as real as any other.

Expanding interactions produce the opposite. The person across from you, through the quality of their attention, positions you as fully present — interesting, worth hearing, permitted to take up space. You do not have to perform a smaller version of yourself. The fullness is welcomed. The welcoming, felt in the body, is the expansion. It is not that they gave you energy. It is that they did not require you to spend any on self-suppression.

You are not depleted by bad people and expanded by good people. You are depleted by specific mechanisms — unidirectional attention, regulation transfer, identity positioning — that operate regardless of the person's character. The mechanism is what you learn to read. The character is irrelevant to the body's report.
Scenario · Sit with this

You spend an hour with someone you deeply respect and leave feeling smaller, quieter, and oddly flattened.

They are kind. They are impressive. They are not, in any obvious way, doing anything wrong. And yet you are depleted, and the depletion has the specific texture of having been positioned as the audience for the duration of the interaction.

Page 05 / 06 · The Territory

Your body is not a resource. It is a territory.

This is the framing that will carry through the entire course, and it is the one that most directly challenges the programming most adults have received about their bodies, their energy, and their sexuality. The programming says: your body is a resource — to be offered, shared, accessed, appreciated, consumed. The truth reversal says: your body is a territory — to be governed, defended, opened selectively, and never entered without your active, conscious, ongoing consent.

The difference between these two framings is not semantic. It is structural. A resource exists to be used. A resource that is not being used is wasted. A resource that is being hoarded is selfish. A resource has value only in proportion to how much of it has been made available. This is the framing most of us have been given about our bodies — particularly about our sexual energy, our emotional capacity, and our physical presence. The framing says: share, give, open, make available. The framing does not say: to whom, on what terms, with what consequences.

A territory is different. A territory has borders. It has an interior that is not visible from outside. It has entry points that can be opened or closed. A territory is not diminished by being ungoverned — it is diminished by being governed by someone else. A territory that is well-governed is not selfish. It is sovereign. The sovereignty is not about withholding. It is about choosing — from a position of clarity, authority, and full ownership — what enters, what leaves, and under what conditions.

The truth reversal

You have been told that your openness is your value. The reversal: your governance is your value. You have been told that your availability is generosity. The reversal: your selectivity is sovereignty. You have been told that your body is a gift to be given. The reversal: your body is a territory to be inhabited. The gift framing serves the people who want access. The territory framing serves you.

What the territory framing changes

It changes how you think about boundaries. In the resource framing, a boundary is a wall — something defensive, something that blocks. In the territory framing, a boundary is a border — something that defines, something that marks where governance begins and ends. Borders are not hostile. They are the structural requirement of any territory that intends to remain self-governing. Without borders, the territory is not shared. It is undefended.

It changes how you think about depletion. In the resource framing, depletion means you have been too generous — you gave too much, you should have held back. In the territory framing, depletion means your borders were crossed without your governance — someone entered a channel without your consent, and the cost is being paid in your reserves. The problem is not your generosity. The problem is the absence of governance over who enters and when.

It changes how you think about your sexuality. This is the change the rest of the course will develop. In the resource framing, your sexuality is something to be managed — given wisely, withheld strategically, deployed for maximum effect. In the territory framing, your sexuality is a region of your own body that you govern absolutely. It is not given or withheld. It is opened or closed, from the inside, by the only person with legitimate authority to do so — which is you.

Your body has been treated as a resource for long enough that the resource framing feels natural. It is not natural. It is programming. The territory framing will feel strange at first, because sovereignty always feels strange to someone who has been governed by others for a long time. The strangeness is the reclamation beginning.

The recognition: you have been operating as a resource. The resistance: the territory framing sounds selfish, withholding, cold. The grief: for the years in which your body was open to access you did not consciously choose.
