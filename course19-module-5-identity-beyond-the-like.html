Page 01 / 04 · The Highlight Reel

Everyone’s best bits, all at once.

Scrolling can quietly make you feel worse about your own life, and there’s a precise reason why: you’re comparing your ordinary, behind-the-scenes reality to everyone else’s carefully chosen highlights.

People post their best moments — the holiday, the achievement, the good-hair day — usually filtered, framed, and chosen from dozens of attempts. What you almost never see is the boredom, the insecurity, the arguments, the ordinary Tuesday. So your feed becomes a stream of everyone’s peak moments stacked together, which no single real life could ever match. It is a comparison rigged to make you feel behind.

This is the comparison trap, and knowing the mechanism takes a lot of its sting out. When you feel that pang of ‘everyone’s doing better than me,’ remember you’re seeing edited outsides versus your own unedited inside. The people whose lives look flawless online are, behind the scenes, having the same ordinary, mixed human experience as you.

An unfair fight

You wouldn’t judge your whole personality against a film’s best ten seconds, yet that’s effectively what comparing yourself to a feed does. Naming it as unfair — your full reality against their showreel — lets you feel the pang without believing it. The feeling is real; the comparison it’s based on is not.

You’re comparing your whole messy inside to everyone else’s edited outside. No wonder it hurts — the fight was rigged.

Page 02 / 04 · Worth Not Counted in Likes

A number is a terrible mirror.

Likes, followers, and views give a small hit of feel-good — and that hit can quietly train you to chase them, until your mood rises and falls with a count controlled by strangers and an algorithm.

There’s real brain chemistry here: an unpredictable bit of approval (sometimes a post does well, sometimes it flops) is the same variable-reward pattern from Module 3, now aimed at your self-esteem. It can train you to post for the count, to check obsessively, and to feel a genuine dip when the numbers are low. The trouble is that a like is feedback on a post at a moment, not a verdict on you as a person — but it’s easy to feel it as the second.

When your sense of worth tracks those numbers, you’ve effectively handed the dial that controls how you feel about yourself to strangers, a recommendation algorithm, and the time of day you posted. That’s a precarious place to keep your self-esteem. The work is to take the dial back — to let likes be mildly nice information, not the measure of your value.

Who’s holding the dial?

A practical reframe: decide, in advance, that your worth isn’t a metric. Post things because you want to, not to farm approval; notice the urge to check the count and let it pass; and remind yourself that the most valuable things about you — kindness, curiosity, the way you treat people — are precisely the things no platform can count. The uncountable parts are the real ones.

If your worth goes up and down with a like count, you’ve handed the dial to strangers. Take it back.

Page 03 / 04 · Who Are You Offline?

Build a self the app can’t touch.

The strongest protection against everything in this module isn’t a setting or a rule — it’s having a life and identity that exist solidly off the feed. The more of you that lives offline, the less any platform can shake.

Think of your identity like a building. If it’s built mostly on online validation — likes, followers, the image you project — it’s built on sand that shifts with every algorithm change and every quiet day. If it’s built on real things — skills you’re developing, interests you genuinely love, people who know the actual you, things you’re proud of that no one online ever sees — it’s built on rock. Same person, completely different stability.

And here’s your unfair advantage: you can build that rock now, before you’re online, when nothing is competing for the hours. Pick up the hobby, get good at the thing, deepen the friendships, spend time in the physical world. Every bit of real-world identity you build is a part of you the internet will never be able to define or destabilise later.

The off-switch test

A simple check: if every app vanished tomorrow, who would you still be? What would you still do, love, and be good at? The bigger and clearer that answer, the safer you are online — because your sense of self isn’t plugged into a system someone else controls. The goal was never to fear the internet; it’s to be whole enough that the internet can’t define you.

The goal isn’t to fear the internet. It’s to be someone whole enough that the internet can’t define you.
