Module 03 · Slide 01

The Attention Economy

For most of human history, the scarce resource in information environments was information itself — it was difficult to produce, expensive to distribute, and limited in supply. The digital revolution eliminated that scarcity. Information is now effectively infinite. What is scarce is attention — the finite capacity of human minds to engage with the available content.

This shift produced a new economic model: the attention economy. In this model, the product is not information but the directed attention of human beings, which is then sold to advertisers. Every platform, every outlet, every feed competing for your attention is operating inside this model — and the model has a specific and consistent effect on what gets produced. Content that captures and holds attention is rewarded. Content that informs without stimulating is not.

Central observation
When your attention is the product being sold, the media environment is not designed to inform you. It is designed to capture you — and the emotions most reliable for capture are fear, outrage, and the tribal intensity of us-versus-them.
Engagement as the metric that replaced truth
In the attention economy, the primary metric of content success is engagement — clicks, shares, time-on-page, comments. Accurate but undramatic information scores poorly on engagement metrics. Emotionally charged, identity-activating, conflict-centred content scores well. Over time, the selection pressure of these metrics does not produce a media environment designed around truth. It produces one designed around the reliable stimulation of the emotional states that generate engagement. Truth is incidental to this process, not its goal.
The business model of outrage
Outrage is the attention economy's highest-value product. An outraged person clicks, shares, returns, comments, and recruits others to the content. An informed but calm person does none of these things at comparable rates. This creates a systematic economic incentive to produce content that generates outrage — about political opponents, cultural change, threatening groups, institutional failures. The outrage is not incidental to media economics. It is, in many cases, the actual product being manufactured and sold.
The cost of free
The perception that free media — free social media, free news websites, free search — is a public good obscures what is actually being exchanged. The user is not the customer of these services. The user is the product: their attention, their behavioural data, their psychological profile — all are extracted and sold. "Free" media that is funded by advertising has a structural incentive to produce whatever content most reliably captures attention for the longest periods, regardless of whether that content serves the interests of the people consuming it.

Module 03 · Slide 02

Who Pays for the Story

Every piece of journalism, every media outlet, every platform exists within an economic structure that shapes what it can and cannot say. Understanding that structure is not a conspiracy theory. It is the most basic form of media literacy — the same kind of analysis applied routinely to any other industry whose products affect public life.

Following the money in media does not require assuming bad intent. Economic structures shape editorial decisions through incentive, culture, and the gradual selection of people and content that fit the model — not primarily through direct editorial interference. The result is a systematic pattern of coverage that serves the interests of whoever funds the media, without any individual journalist needing to be corrupt, and without any editor needing to issue explicit instructions.

Central observation
You do not need to know who owns a media outlet to understand how ownership shapes its coverage. You need to understand whose interests are structurally aligned with its economic survival — and ask what coverage those interests would discourage.
Advertising dependency
A media outlet dependent on advertising revenue from a particular industry sector will consistently produce coverage that does not seriously threaten that sector's interests — not because editors receive calls from advertisers, but because journalists who produce threatening coverage find their stories deprioritised, their beats reassigned, and their career advancement slowed. The selection is gradual and largely invisible. The pattern it produces is reliable: advertising-dependent media covers the industries that fund it with consistent softness, without any individual instance of corruption being provable.
Ownership and editorial alignment
Media ownership concentrates — in most major markets, a small number of corporations and wealthy individuals own the majority of significant media outlets. Owners do not need to issue editorial directives for ownership to shape coverage. Editors who consistently produce coverage misaligned with ownership interests are replaced. Journalists learn what kinds of stories advance their careers and what kinds do not. The ideological alignment between media output and ownership interest is produced by selection and cultural transmission, not by instruction. It is more reliable as a result.
The funding of the free
Social media platforms are funded by advertising and data extraction. Their economic model requires maximising time-on-platform, which requires maximising emotional engagement, which rewards content that generates fear, outrage, and tribal intensity. This is not incidental to the funding model — it is produced by it. Understanding who pays for the free social media environment you use tells you what that environment is actually optimised to produce, and why the content you encounter there has the emotional texture it does.
I used to think media bias was about individual journalists having opinions. Then I looked at who owned the outlets and who advertised in them, and I realised individual opinion was the smallest part of it. The shape of the coverage was built into the economics long before anyone wrote a word.
— workshop participant, former editor

Module 03 · Slide 03

Reading Who Benefits

Cui bono — who benefits? — is one of the oldest tools of critical analysis, used in law, philosophy, and investigative journalism. Applied to media, it asks: whose interests are served by this story being told this way, with this framing, at this time? It is not a question that assumes a hidden hand behind every story. It is a structural question about the alignment between content and interest.

The answer is rarely a single identifiable actor. Usually it is a pattern: stories that consistently serve certain interests over time, framings that consistently make certain questions unaskable, sources that consistently represent certain perspectives as expert while excluding others. The pattern, not the individual instance, is what the question reveals.

Central observation
Asking who benefits from a story does not require assuming anyone intended the benefit. Structural interests shape coverage without conspiracies. The question is analytic, not accusatory — and it should be applied to stories you agree with as readily as to stories you distrust.
The timing question
When a story appears matters as much as what it says. A damaging story about a political candidate published the day before an election serves different interests than the same story published six months earlier. A story about government corruption published during a period of social unrest that the government wants to distract from serves different interests than the same story in a stable period. The timing of stories is an editorial decision that rarely gets analysed — but it is often where the most revealing alignment between content and interest can be found.
The framing question
The same event can be framed to serve very different interests depending on what is foregrounded and what is backgrounded. A labour strike framed as "workers demanding better conditions" serves worker interests. The same strike framed as "disruption to commuters" serves management interests. Neither framing is necessarily false — both may accurately describe aspects of the situation. But the choice of which aspect to centre is an editorial decision with consistent patterns that reveal whose interests the outlet structurally serves.
The source question
Journalism depends on sources — people with knowledge, access, or authority who provide information and perspective. The consistent pattern of which sources are treated as credible and which are treated as fringe, which voices are presented as expert and which as partisan, reveals the editorial framework that determines whose interests get represented as objective reality and whose interests get represented as special pleading. Government ministers are "officials." Trade union representatives are "union leaders." The language of sourcing encodes a hierarchy of credibility that consistently advantages certain interests over others.

Module 03 · Slide 04

What Is Being Hidden

The most significant editorial decisions in any media environment are not the ones about what to say — they are the ones about what not to cover. Omission is the most powerful and least visible tool of narrative management, because you cannot fact-check what is not there. You cannot challenge a perspective that is never presented. You cannot ask about a question that the coverage never raises.

This is not primarily about active concealment — about stories being suppressed or journalists being silenced, though these things do occur. It is about the structural tendency of any media system to cover some things extensively and others barely at all, based on the economic, political, and cultural interests that shape what counts as news.

Central observation
The map is not the territory — and the media map of the world leaves out more than it includes. What is consistently absent from the map is not random. The pattern of absence tells you whose interests the map is drawn to serve.
The gap analysis
Media literacy applied to omission requires developing the habit of asking: what is not here? Which perspectives have not been sought? Which consequences have not been examined? Which affected groups have not been asked for comment? Which alternative explanations have not been considered? This gap analysis is more demanding than fact-checking present claims, because it requires knowing enough about a topic to notice what is missing — which in turn requires consuming media across a wider range than most people do.
The scale inversion
Media coverage is often inversely related to significance. Events that are visually dramatic, emotionally immediate, and involve identifiable individuals attract enormous coverage. Events that are structurally significant but visually undramatic, affect large populations diffusely, or require sustained attention to understand attract little. The result is a systematic distortion of public understanding: people are very well informed about vivid, emotionally engaging events and very poorly informed about the structural conditions that shape their lives in more consequential ways.
The perspective deficit
Most mainstream media in any country systematically underrepresents the perspectives of people without institutional access — the poor, the marginalised, the geographically peripheral, the culturally non-dominant. Their experiences are covered when they become subjects of stories about social problems. They are rarely consulted as sources of analysis, expertise, or perspective on the conditions that shape their lives. The perspective deficit means that public understanding of social reality is systematically filtered through the experience of people who are not representative of most people's lives.
The practice
Regularly ask: who is not in this story? What would this situation look like from the perspective of the person least likely to be quoted as a source? What would a journalist need to know, and have access to, to cover what is not being covered here? These questions do not produce alternative facts — they produce awareness of the shape of what you are not being shown.

Module 03 · Slide 05

What You Are Being Made to Fear

Fear is the attention economy's premium product. A frightened audience is an engaged audience — they return for updates, they share alerts, they recruit others to the source of the warning. Fear also has a specific political utility: it makes people more receptive to authority, more tolerant of restrictions on civil liberties, more willing to accept simplistic explanations and punitive solutions, and less able to engage in the deliberative complexity that genuine democratic participation requires.

This does not mean all media fear is manufactured or illegitimate. Some things genuinely warrant fear. The question is not whether fear is ever appropriate — it is how to distinguish between fear that is proportionate to actual risk and fear that is being engineered to serve economic or political interests that have nothing to do with your safety.

Central observation
The fears that dominate media coverage are rarely the fears proportionate to actual risk. They are the fears that are economically and politically most useful — which is a different criterion entirely.
Risk distortion
Psychological research consistently shows that people dramatically overestimate the risks of dramatic, vivid, memorable events — plane crashes, terrorist attacks, stranger violence — and dramatically underestimate the risks of mundane, statistical, diffuse events — car accidents, preventable disease, structural poverty. Media coverage amplifies this distortion: dramatic events receive extensive, emotionally vivid coverage; statistical risks receive occasional, low-engagement coverage. The result is a public whose fear is systematically misdirected away from the risks most likely to actually harm them.
Who benefits from your fear
Every manufactured fear serves someone's interests. Fear of crime benefits politicians who campaign on law and order, private security companies, and media outlets that attract frightened audiences. Fear of immigration benefits nationalist political movements and the media ecosystems that sustain them. Fear of economic collapse benefits financial institutions that can be positioned as necessary stabilisers. Asking who benefits from the specific fear being produced — not from protecting you from the threat, but from your being afraid of it — is one of the most clarifying questions in media literacy.
The chronically frightened citizen
A person in a sustained state of fear is less capable of the reflective, long-term, complexity-tolerant thinking that genuine civic participation requires — and more dependent on the sources of information and authority that appear to manage the threat. Chronic media-induced fear produces a population that is politically anxious, cognitively narrowed, and more susceptible to simple explanations and authoritarian solutions. This outcome serves certain political and economic interests so reliably that it is worth asking whether it is, in some contexts, an intended feature rather than an accidental byproduct.

Module 03 · Slide 06

Source Literacy

Media literacy depends substantially on source literacy — the capacity to evaluate not just what a source is saying but what kind of source it is, what interests it serves, what its track record looks like, and what its methodology for arriving at claims actually is. Most people evaluate sources primarily on whether they agree with the source's conclusions. This is the opposite of useful.

A source that tells you what you already believe is the source you least need to scrutinise — you will do that automatically. The sources that require the most active evaluation are the ones whose conclusions you find most convincing: the ones aligned with your existing views, your tribal identity, your emotional needs. Those are the sources where your scrutiny is lowest and where the influence is therefore highest.

Central observation
The question is not "do I trust this source?" Trust is a feeling. The question is "what is this source's funding, methodology, track record of accuracy, and conflicts of interest?" Those are facts — and they apply regardless of whether you happen to agree with the source's current conclusions.
The four source questions
Four questions applied to any source begin to make its reliability and interests visible. First: who funds this source, and whose interests does that funding align with? Second: what is its methodology — how does it gather and verify information, and is that methodology described and auditable? Third: what is its track record — has it been accurate in the past, and how does it handle corrections when it is wrong? Fourth: what conflicts of interest might shape its conclusions — does it have financial, political, or reputational stakes in particular outcomes? None of these questions produces a verdict. Together they produce a profile that allows more calibrated trust.
Primary sources versus secondary reporting
Most media consumption involves secondary reporting — journalists describing what other sources said, summarising research they read, paraphrasing officials they spoke to. Each layer of secondary reporting introduces selection and interpretation. A claim that becomes widely known through secondary reporting may bear little resemblance to the primary source it originated from — a research paper may have been misread, a quote taken out of context, a nuanced finding simplified into a headline. Tracing claims to primary sources — the original research, the actual document, the full quote — is one of the most reliably informative media literacy practices available.
The credibility transfer problem
Credibility transfers between adjacent claims in ways that can be deliberately exploited. A source with a strong track record on one kind of claim is treated as reliable on adjacent claims where its track record is weaker or its conflicts of interest are greater. A respected scientist commenting on policy. A credible journalist reporting outside their area of expertise. A reliable outlet publishing opinion alongside news. The credibility earned in one domain does not automatically extend to others — but it regularly does in how audiences receive the content. This is why partisan and commercial actors invest in building credibility in non-contested areas before using it in contested ones.

Module 03 · Slide 07

The Scroll as Participation

Scrolling through a social media feed feels passive. It is not. Every moment you spend on the platform, every post you pause on, every link you click, every reaction you leave — all of this is data that trains the algorithm to show you more of what kept you engaged. The scroll is not neutral information consumption. It is active participation in shaping what you see next, in a system designed to show you what keeps you most engaged for the longest time.

This means that the information environment you inhabit is not the product of journalism or editorial decisions alone. It is substantially a product of your own past emotional responses, recorded and processed by systems designed to maximise engagement rather than to inform. You are, in a specific and literal sense, co-authoring the propaganda environment you consume.

Central observation
The algorithm does not show you the world. It shows you a model of you — built from your past emotional responses — reflected back at you. The longer you scroll, the more the model tightens, and the further the reflection moves from anything outside your existing responses.
The filter bubble as self-construction
Filter bubbles — information environments curated to show you content aligned with your existing views — are not primarily the product of deliberate manipulation by platform operators. They are substantially the product of your own engagement patterns, amplified by algorithms optimised for retention. Content that confirms your existing views generates more engagement than content that challenges them. The algorithm learns this and produces more confirming content. Over time the bubble tightens, not because someone is censoring alternatives, but because your own responses have selected them out.
Emotional engagement as algorithm training
Every emotional response you have to content — anger, fear, joy, disgust, tribal pride — trains the algorithm to show you more content that generates that response. This creates a feedback loop between your emotional states and your information environment: the more outraged you become at the content you see, the more outrage-generating content the algorithm serves you; the more frightened you become, the more fear-producing content arrives. Your emotional life and your information diet are locked in a mutually escalating relationship that the platform profits from.
The attention cost of awareness
Simply knowing that algorithms shape your feed does not break the feedback loop. The emotional responses that train the algorithm happen faster than conscious awareness — you feel the outrage before you notice you are being shown outrage-generating content. The loop operates at a level below the cognitive engagement that media literacy training addresses. This is why the most effective interventions are structural — changing the conditions of consumption (time limits, deliberate diversification of sources, removing apps from phone) — rather than purely cognitive (knowing how it works but continuing to scroll).

Module 03 · Slide 08

Practical Media Literacy

Media literacy is not primarily an intellectual achievement. It is a set of habits — daily practices applied consistently to the actual information environment you inhabit. The gap between understanding how media works and actually changing how you relate to it is significant, and it is not closed by knowledge alone. It requires the deliberate construction of new practices and the gradual modification of existing ones.

None of the practices below produce certainty or perfect information. They produce something more modest and more durable: a slightly more accurate picture of the territory behind the map, and a slightly less automatic relationship to the emotional states that media environments are designed to produce.

Central observation
The goal of practical media literacy is not to consume less media or to find the one reliable source. It is to consume media with more of yourself present in the act — noticing what is happening to you as you encounter it, rather than being carried along by it.
The three questions before sharing
Before sharing any piece of content, three questions interrupt the automatic propagation loop. First: have I read beyond the headline? (Most sharing occurs without reading the full article, which is the primary mechanism by which misleading headlines spread true stories.) Second: do I know what kind of source this is, and what its funding and methodology look like? Third: am I sharing because this is accurate and important, or because it confirms something I already believed and generated a feeling I wanted to pass on? The third question is the hardest and the most useful.
Deliberate source diversification
Reading only sources that confirm your existing views produces information that feels like knowledge but functions like propaganda — it produces high confidence in a narrow picture. Deliberate source diversification means regularly reading the strongest versions of perspectives that challenge your own — not fringe or bad-faith sources, but the most credible outlets representing different frameworks. The goal is not balance for its own sake but to maintain enough contact with alternative perspectives that your existing views remain genuinely held rather than reflexively performed.
The emotion-as-signal practice
Treating your emotional response to media content as data about the content rather than simply experiencing it is one of the most valuable practices available. When you notice strong fear, outrage, or tribal pride arriving in response to a story, pause and ask: what in this content produced this feeling, and what does that feeling make me want to do? This is not about suppressing the emotion — it is about using it as a signal that the propagandistic mechanics may be running, and that slowing down to look at the architecture of what you are experiencing is warranted.

Module 03 complete.

You have examined the attention economy and what it is actually optimised to produce, how to follow the money in media, how to read who benefits from a story, how to analyse what is being hidden through omission, how fear is manufactured and who profits from it, how to evaluate sources rather than just trust or distrust them, how the scroll is not passive consumption but active algorithm training, and the practical habits that build genuine media literacy over time.

What you carry forward
  1. 01The attention economy optimises for engagement, not information. The metric is time on platform and emotional activation; truth is irrelevant to the optimisation target.
  2. 02Following the money means asking: who funds this outlet, who advertises in it, and what narratives align with those interests? Coverage and funding are not independent variables.
  3. 03The most revealing part of a story is often what it does not say. Omission is an editorial choice that reflects the narrative frame more honestly than the content that appears.
  4. 04Fear is manufactured as a product. It is reliable, highly engaging, and drives consumption. The threat does not need to be proportionate to the coverage for the manufacture to succeed.
  5. 05Source evaluation is a skill, not a list. It requires examining a source’s track record, funding, errors, and incentive structure — applied consistently, including to sources you trust and stories that confirm what you already believe.
  6. 06The scroll is not passive consumption — it is active algorithm training. Every pause and engagement teaches the system what to amplify next.
Practice this week

Before reading any news story this week, identify three things first: who published it, who funds the outlet, and what narrative frame is established in the first two paragraphs. Do this once per day for seven days — not as a research exercise but as a reading habit. The frame is visible before the facts if you look first.

Monthly: pick one topic you feel well-informed about. Find two or three sources whose funding and ownership differ from your usual outlets. Spend thirty minutes reading only those sources on that topic. Note what they see that yours does not, and what they miss that yours covers. The gaps between sources are the curriculum.
