Page 01 / 04 · From Punishment to Prevention

Violence as a disease, not a crime.

The single biggest shift this course asks you to make is from a punitive question — ‘who do we punish?’ — to an epidemiological one: ‘what conditions produce this, and how do we prevent it?’

For most of modern history, societies have treated violence as a moral failing to be punished after the fact. The public-health paradigm reframes it as a preventable health problem with identifiable risk factors, transmission patterns, and points of intervention — much as we approach cholera or smoking. This isn't ‘soft on harm’; it's a different theory of where harm comes from and how to stop it before it happens.

The evidence for this shift is substantial. The World Health Organization's landmark World Report on Violence and Health (Krug et al., 2002) established violence as a leading global public-health issue and argued it is ‘preventable, not inevitable.’ Programmes built on this logic — like the Cure Violence model, which treats violence as a contagious process and deploys trained ‘interrupters’ to halt transmission — have produced measurable reductions in shootings in some settings. The frame changes what counts as an intervention.

Why ‘contagion’ is more than a metaphor

Violence behaves epidemiologically: exposure to it is one of the strongest predictors of perpetrating it. A young person who witnesses violence, is victimised by it, or is embedded in a network where it is normalised is measurably more likely to enact it — the pathogen spreads through exposure, not through some fixed individual defect. Seeing violence as transmissible reveals that interrupting exposure, treating trauma, and shifting norms are not side projects; they are the core of prevention.

‘Why do kids commit crimes?’ is a question about individuals. ‘What manufactures youth violence?’ is a question about systems — and only the second one can be engineered.

Page 02 / 04 · The Ecological Model

Four levels, one system.

The WHO's ecological model is the backbone of this field: it insists that violence is never explained at one level alone, but emerges from risk factors nested across the individual, the relationship, the community, and society.

Introduced in the World Report on Violence and Health (Krug et al., 2002), the model maps risk across four nested levels. Individual: personal history, trauma, biology, age. Relationship: family, peers, partners — the close ties that shape behaviour. Community: schools, neighbourhoods, workplaces, and the conditions of those settings. Societal: the broad forces — economic inequality, cultural norms about masculinity and violence, weak governance, policy. The crucial claim is that these interact; a risk at one level amplifies risks at others.

This is what makes the model so useful for design. A purely individual lens (therapy, punishment) ignores the societal conditions that keep producing new cases. A purely societal lens (policy) ignores the relational and individual trauma that policy alone won't heal. Effective prevention is multi-level by necessity — it works the relationship and community levels while also changing the societal conditions that load the dice.

Why single-level explanations fail

Most public debate fixates on one level — usually the individual (‘bad choices’) or occasionally the societal (‘it's all poverty’). Both are incomplete. Two young people with identical individual risk can diverge entirely depending on relational support and community conditions; identical neighbourhoods produce different outcomes depending on family and individual factors. The model's discipline is to refuse the single story and ask, every time: what is happening at all four levels at once?

Violence is not located in a person or in a society. It is produced in the interaction between levels — which is also where it can be interrupted.

Page 03 / 04 · The Neurobiology of Trauma

How adversity rewires the threat response.

At the individual level, the most important mechanism is trauma. Chronic adversity in childhood physically shapes the developing brain's threat-detection systems — which is why ‘just make better choices’ misunderstands what's happening.

The Adverse Childhood Experiences (ACEs) research (Felitti, Anda and colleagues, from 1998 onward) demonstrated a strong dose-response relationship between cumulative childhood adversity — abuse, neglect, household dysfunction — and a wide range of later harms, including violence. The mechanism is partly neurobiological: chronic developmental stress tunes the brain's threat-response systems (the amygdala and stress-hormone axis) toward hypervigilance, so that ambiguous situations are read as dangerous and met with fight-or-flight.

For an adolescent — whose prefrontal cortex, the seat of impulse control and consequence-weighing, is still years from maturity — a threat-primed brain is a recipe for reactive aggression. This is neither an excuse nor a destiny; brains remain plastic and trauma is treatable. But it reframes ‘violent youth’ as, very often, ‘traumatised youth whose nervous systems are doing exactly what chronic danger trained them to do.’ Prevention then includes preventing the adversity in the first place.

From ‘what's wrong with you’ to ‘what happened to you’

This reframing — central to trauma-informed practice — captures the shift. ‘What's wrong with you?’ assumes a defective individual. ‘What happened to you?’ assumes a normal nervous system responding to abnormal conditions. The second question is not only more humane; it is more accurate and more actionable, because ‘what happened’ points at preventable exposures and treatable injuries rather than fixed character.

A threat-primed adolescent brain is not making free choices in a vacuum. It is running software that chronic danger installed — and software can be rewritten.
