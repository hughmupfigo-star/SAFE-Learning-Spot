Sea Moss: The Ocean's Gift

Of all the foods celebrated in the alkaline healing tradition, sea moss — the red algae Chondrus crispus — holds a special place. It contains an extraordinary density of the minerals the human body needs, sourced directly from the sea.

Sea moss is said to contain 92 of the 102 minerals found in the human body — including iodine, potassium, calcium, iron, magnesium, phosphorus, zinc, and selenium. It is a natural source of iodine that supports thyroid function and metabolism. Its natural mucilaginous quality — the gel it produces when soaked — soothes and coats the gut lining, acting as a prebiotic that feeds beneficial gut bacteria. It supports the respiratory system, the immune system, skin health, and joint flexibility.

Sea moss gel — made by blending soaked sea moss with spring water — can be added to smoothies, teas, porridge, and soups. It has no strong flavour, making it one of the most versatile healing foods available. As a fast-breaker after extended fasting, it is ideal: gentle on the gut, immediately mineralising, and deeply supportive of the renewed gut lining.

Burdock Root: The Blood Purifier

Burdock root has been used as medicine across African, Asian, and European healing traditions for centuries. In the alkaline tradition it is among the most important herbs: a deep cleanser of the blood, the liver, and the lymphatic system.

Burdock root contains inulin — a prebiotic fibre that feeds beneficial gut bacteria — as well as arctigenin, a compound with documented anti-inflammatory and anti-tumour properties. It is rich in antioxidants including quercetin, luteolin, and phenolic acids. It stimulates bile production, supporting fat digestion and liver detoxification. It has a mild diuretic effect, supporting kidney function and the elimination of waste through the urinary system.

The lymphatic system — the body's drainage and immune network — is a primary focus of the alkaline healing tradition. Unlike the blood, the lymphatic system has no pump; it moves through muscular contraction and breathing. Burdock root is one of the herbs most consistently recommended for lymphatic stimulation and clearing — making it particularly valuable for skin conditions like eczema, psoriasis, and acne, which the tradition views as the body eliminating through the skin what the lymph has failed to clear.

Bladderwrack: Sea Vegetable Medicine

Bladderwrack is a brown seaweed that grows along Atlantic coastlines — and in the alkaline tradition it is paired with sea moss as one of nature's most complete mineral and thyroid-supportive combinations.

Bladderwrack is rich in iodine, fucoidan, and fucoxanthin. Fucoidan — a sulphated polysaccharide unique to sea vegetables — is one of the most researched natural compounds in current nutritional science, with documented anti-inflammatory, antiviral, anticoagulant, and anti-tumour properties. Fucoxanthin is a carotenoid with potent antioxidant effects and emerging research supporting its role in metabolism and weight management.

Together, sea moss and bladderwrack provide a spectrum of minerals and bioactive compounds from the sea — the same mineral-rich environment that life on earth originated from. The tradition holds that sea vegetables bring the ocean's mineral intelligence directly into the body. Science is increasingly mapping what that intelligence contains: compounds that support immunity, reduce inflammation, protect against viral replication, and nourish the thyroid and adrenal systems that govern energy and stress response.

Sarsaparilla: The Hormone Harmoniser

Sarsaparilla root — used across Central American, Caribbean, and African healing traditions — is one of the alkaline tradition's most valued herbs for blood purification, hormonal balance, and inflammatory conditions.

Sarsaparilla contains plant sterols — steroidal saponins including sarsasapogenin and smilagenin — that interact with the endocrine system. These plant compounds are structurally similar to human steroid hormones and are believed to support the body's own hormone production, helping to restore balance in states of hormonal disruption. It has historically been used for conditions associated with hormonal imbalance including skin disorders, low libido, fatigue, and menopausal symptoms.

Sarsaparilla also binds to endotoxins in the gut — bacterial lipopolysaccharides that, when absorbed into the bloodstream, drive systemic inflammation — and helps carry them out of the body before absorption. This endotoxin-binding property makes it particularly relevant in conditions of gut permeability where internal toxins are contributing to inflammation, skin conditions, and hormonal disruption. It is both a blood purifier and a hormonal support in one root.

Elderberry and Elderflower: Nature's Antiviral

Elder — both the berry and the flower — is one of the most thoroughly researched medicinal plants available. What healing traditions across Europe and Africa have known for thousands of years, clinical trials have now confirmed.

Elderberries are exceptionally rich in anthocyanins — the dark purple pigments that give them their colour and are among the most potent antioxidant and anti-inflammatory compounds in the plant kingdom. Multiple randomised controlled trials have found that elderberry extract significantly reduces the duration and severity of influenza and upper respiratory tract infections. The mechanism includes direct antiviral activity — compounds in elderberry have been shown to prevent viral particles from binding to and entering host cells — as well as immune modulation.

Elderflower supports the respiratory system through a different route: it is diaphoretic — it promotes sweating, helping the body release fever — and has expectorant properties, supporting the clearing of mucus from the airways. As a tea during illness, it supports the body's own fever response rather than suppressing it. Both berry and flower demonstrate the elder plant's complete orientation toward respiratory and immune health.

Turmeric: The Golden Root

Turmeric has been central to Ayurvedic and African healing traditions for thousands of years. Its active compound — curcumin — is now among the most extensively researched natural anti-inflammatory compounds in science.

Curcumin works by inhibiting NF-kB — a molecule that travels into cell nuclei and switches on inflammation genes. This is the same pathway targeted by many pharmaceutical anti-inflammatory drugs. But curcumin modulates this pathway rather than blocking it entirely, producing anti-inflammatory effects without the side effects — gut damage, immune suppression, cardiovascular risk — associated with long-term NSAID use.

Curcumin's bioavailability is significantly enhanced by two things: black pepper (piperine increases absorption by up to 2,000%) and a fat source, since curcumin is fat-soluble. The traditional preparation — turmeric cooked with pepper in oil — was arrived at through generations of practical wisdom, not laboratory analysis. Research has also identified curcumin's neuroprotective properties: it crosses the blood-brain barrier, reduces neuroinflammation, and is being actively researched for applications in Alzheimer's and depression.

Dandelion: The Misunderstood Weed

The most dismissed plant in the garden is one of the most medicinally complete. Every part of the dandelion — root, leaf, and flower — is medicine. It grows freely, costs nothing, and most people pull it out of the ground and throw it away.

Dandelion leaves contain more calcium per gram than milk, more iron per gram than spinach, and significant amounts of potassium, vitamin K, vitamin A, and vitamin C. They are bitter — and that bitterness is medicine: bitter compounds stimulate the digestive system, trigger bile production in the liver, and prime the gut for optimal nutrient absorption. The leaf has a gentle diuretic effect that supports kidney function and helps the body clear excess fluid without depleting potassium the way pharmaceutical diuretics do.

The dandelion root is a liver tonic and prebiotic — rich in inulin, it feeds beneficial gut bacteria. It is one of the most reliable herbs for supporting liver detoxification pathways and is used across multiple healing traditions for conditions associated with liver congestion: sluggish digestion, skin conditions, hormonal excess, and chronic fatigue. The flower provides antioxidants and polyphenols. A plant scorned as a weed contains an entire pharmacy.

The Pharmacy Is in the Garden

The herbs in this module are not the whole library — they are a doorway. Behind them is an entire tradition of plant medicine that covers nearly every condition the human body encounters. The pharmacy was always in the earth.

Garlic: one of nature's most potent antimicrobial and antiviral compounds. Raw garlic produces allicin — a sulphur compound that kills bacteria, fungi, and viruses with a potency that has been compared to pharmaceutical antibiotics in laboratory conditions. Ginger: a powerful anti-inflammatory and digestive support, effective for nausea, joint pain, and gut motility. Avocado: one of the densest sources of healthy fat and glutathione — the body's master antioxidant, essential for liver detoxification. Dark leafy greens: chlorophyll-rich blood builders that oxygenate the body at a cellular level. Figs and dates: among the most mineral-dense fruits available — figs contain more calcium than milk per 100g.

The principle that runs through all of it is the same: nature provided what the body needs, in the form the body can receive it, long before laboratories existed. Learning to read the garden — to know which plant supports which system — is the restoration of an ancient literacy that was never supposed to be lost.
