Page 01 / 04 · Naming It

You can’t steady what you can’t name.

If you need help right now

Call or text 988 (Suicide & Crisis Lifeline) · text HOME to 741741 (Crisis Text Line) · call 911 if you’re in immediate danger. Free, confidential, 24/7.

Feelings move through you like weather — and the simple act of naming the weather (‘this is anxiety,’ ‘this is grief’) genuinely calms your brain down. It's one of the most studied tools there is.

When a feeling is just a big formless storm, it runs you. The moment you put a word to it — ‘I'm anxious,’ ‘I'm overwhelmed,’ ‘I'm lonely’ — something shifts. Researchers call it ‘name it to tame it’: labeling an emotion actually lowers its intensity in the brain. You're not getting rid of the feeling; you're getting a handle on it.

Lots of us were never taught more than ‘fine,’ ‘good,’ ‘stressed,’ and ‘tired.’ But there's a difference between anxious and overwhelmed, between sad and numb, between angry and hurt. The more precisely you can name what's moving through you, the less it controls you — and the easier it is to know what you actually need.

Weather, not climate

Here's the hopeful part: weather passes. The worst feeling you'll have this week is a storm front, not your permanent climate. Inside it, it feels endless — that's what intense emotion does, it lies about how long it'll last. Naming it (‘this is a hard wave, and waves move’) reminds you that you're watching weather, not stuck in it forever.

Name the weather and it stops being you. ‘I am anxious’ becomes ‘I notice anxiety’ — and that small gap is where your power is.

Page 02 / 04 · Your Early Warning Signs

Learn your own amber lights.

Long before a really bad day arrives, your mind and body usually flash warning signs. Learning your personal amber lights lets you act early — when small tools still work easily.

Everyone has a pattern that shows up before things get heavy. For some it's sleep slipping — staying up too late, or not wanting to get out of bed. For others it's pulling away from friends, snapping at people, living on your phone, skipping meals or eating to numb out, or that low buzz of dread before school. None of these are character flaws; they're dashboard lights telling you the engine needs attention.

The skill is to know yours specifically. When you can say ‘okay, I'm cancelling on everyone and not sleeping — that's my pattern,’ you catch the slide early. Acting at amber (a tough afternoon) is so much easier than acting at red (a crisis week). Most of this course is about getting good at amber.

Tracking, gently

You don't need an app or a mood journal unless you want one. Just start noticing, over the next while, what tends to show up first when you're heading downhill. A mental list of three or four of your own warning signs is genuinely powerful — it turns ‘suddenly everything's terrible’ into ‘ah, I know this pattern, and I know what helps.’

By the time it's a red light, the easy fixes are harder. Catch yourself at amber, and a walk or a text can change the whole day.

Page 03 / 04 · Hard Day vs. Needs Help

When a rough patch needs backup.

Not every hard day needs a professional — and some absolutely do. Knowing roughly where the line is means you neither panic over normal lows nor ignore something that genuinely needs more support.

Hard days, even hard weeks, are part of being human — a breakup, exam stress, a grief, a low stretch. Those usually respond to time, rest, support, and the kind of tools in this course. What's different — and worth taking to a professional — is when low mood or anxiety sticks around most days for weeks, when it stops you doing normal things (school, friends, getting up), when nothing brings any pleasure, or when you're using substances to cope.

And some things skip the queue entirely. If you're thinking about hurting yourself or ending your life, having thoughts of not wanting to be here, or you don't feel safe — that's not a ‘wait and see.’ That's a reach out now: 988, text HOME to 741741, a trusted adult, or 911. There is no bar you have to clear to deserve that call. Thinking about it at all is enough.

You won't get it ‘wrong’

People worry they'll over-react or under-react. Here's the relief: you can't really get this wrong by reaching out. If you call a line or tell an adult and it turns out you ‘just’ needed to talk, that's a completely fine outcome — that's what they're for. Reaching out a little early always beats a little late. When unsure, treat it as worth support.

If you're ever asking ‘is this bad enough to get help?’ — that question itself is usually a good enough reason to reach out.
