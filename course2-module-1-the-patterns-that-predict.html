Page 01 / 07 · The Window

Why the first three months tell you almost everything.

There is a window at the beginning of every relationship — roughly the first ninety days — in which two truths are simultaneously available. The person is showing you who they want to be, and the person is showing you who they are. Both signals are present. Most people read only one of them.

This course is structured around the second signal. Not because the first one doesn't matter — the version of someone they are reaching toward tells you something real about their aspirations and self-concept. But the second signal is what predicts the next three years. The structural truth, peeking through the performance, in moments the person didn't know you were paying attention.

Why ninety days, specifically

The first three months of a new relationship are governed by a particular neurochemistry — heightened dopamine, suppressed critical assessment, and a felt sense that this person is unlike anyone you've ever met. The chemistry is real. It is also lying to you about specific things. Not about whether you like them. About whether they are who they appear to be.

The honeymoon window is, paradoxically, both the time when you are least equipped to evaluate someone, and the time when their information is most freely available. They have not yet learned which of your reactions to manage. You have not yet decided what to overlook. The mask is on, but it is still being adjusted. The cracks are visible to anyone watching for them.

By month four or five, two things have happened. The performance has stabilised — they now know which version of themselves to deploy with you, and they deploy it consistently. And you have begun, without noticing, to revise their inconvenient behaviours into more flattering interpretations. The information was clearer in week three. By month five, it has been quietly rewritten.

The first ninety days are not the time when the relationship is being built. They are the time when the relationship is being honestly disclosed. Most people are too busy enjoying the chemistry to read the disclosure.

The connection is intense, the conversations are deep, the chemistry is real. There is also one small thing they did last week — a tone, a comment, a moment — that didn't sit right with you. You've already explained it away three times.

Page 02 / 07 · The Diagnostic

Incident versus pattern.

This is the conceptual move that separates everyone who recognises red flags too late from everyone who recognises them in time. The skill is not memorising a list of bad behaviours. The skill is learning to distinguish between an incident and a pattern.

An incident is a single behaviour. Everyone has incidents. The kindest person you know has been rude to a waiter once. The most reliable person you know has missed a deadline once. A single bad night is information about a moment — it is not information about the person. Patterns are different. A pattern is the same structural truth showing up in different costumes.

Most people, scanning a new partner for red flags, are looking for incidents — and then they apply the right and reasonable filter of "but everyone has a bad day." This is correct. It is also why they miss the patterns. The pattern is not the loud incident. The pattern is the small repeated thing that shows up in five different situations and was never an emergency in any single one.

Incident

"They snapped at me when they were stressed about work."

"They were distant on Tuesday — they'd had a hard day."

"They forgot the thing I'd told them. They had a lot on."

"They said something thoughtless about my friend, but they apologised."

Pattern

"They snap at me whenever they are stressed, and the stress is increasingly often."

"They become distant whenever something is asked of them, and I never feel safe asking."

"They forget the things that matter to me, and remember in detail the things that matter to them."

"They say thoughtless things about people I love, and the apologies follow a script that never produces change."

How to spot a pattern in real time

Ask whether you can name three examples. If a behaviour has only happened once, it is an incident. Note it, file it, do not react to it. If you can name three examples of the same structural truth in different situations, you are looking at a pattern. The threshold is not arbitrary — three examples is enough to rule out coincidence and stress.

Ask whether the behaviour appears in moments they didn't expect to be watched. The patterns that predict are the ones that surface when the person thinks the scene is unimportant — how they speak to staff in restaurants, how they handle being told no by a stranger, how they respond when something small inconveniences them. These moments are the unguarded data. The performance is off because the audience seems to not be there.

Ask whether your explanations are becoming creative. The first time you explain something away, the explanation is usually simple — they had a long day. By the fourth time, the explanations are becoming increasingly intricate, increasingly accommodating, increasingly removed from what actually happened. The complexity of your explanations is itself the data. Simple behaviour requires simple interpretation. Complex interpretation suggests you are working hard to maintain a story.

A pattern is what an incident becomes when nobody named it the first three times. By the fifth incident, the pattern is the relationship, and the costume is just the day's weather.
Scenario · Sit with this

You realise that in the last six weeks, you have three separate examples of the same small structural thing.

Each one, taken alone, was explainable. Stress, a bad day, a misunderstanding. But laid next to each other, they have the same shape — and you don't quite know what to do with the recognition.

Page 03 / 07 · The Four

The four patterns that predict.

If you only had four diagnostic questions to ask about a new partner — four windows to look through in the first three months — these would be the ones. They are not the most dramatic. They are not the most discussed. They are the ones that, when read accurately, predict the rest.

Pattern 01

How they treat people who can do nothing for them.

The waiter. The cleaner. The Uber driver. The customer service representative on the phone. The stranger who asks for directions. These people occupy a specific position in the social hierarchy of a new relationship — they are visible, they are not part of the performance, and they cannot reward or punish the person you are dating. How your partner treats them is the most accurate disclosure of who they are when no calculation is involved.

What to watch for
  • The shift in tone when speaking to staff versus to you
  • Whether they say please and thank you when nobody is watching
  • How they react when service is slow, imperfect, or genuinely poor
  • What they say about the person after they've left the table
  • Whether their warmth has a hierarchy you can map
Pattern 02

How they handle being told no.

A no, even a small one, is a refusal of access — and how someone receives that refusal in the first three months is one of the most direct previews of how they will respond to your no's for the next decade. The no does not need to be dramatic. It can be a no to a request for sex on a particular night, a no to a suggestion, a no to an invitation, a no to a way they wanted to be perceived.

What to watch for
  • Whether the energy shifts noticeably when the no is delivered
  • Whether they sulk, withdraw, or punish through silence in the hours after
  • Whether they negotiate the no, find loopholes, or try again with different framing
  • Whether they retaliate later with a small unrelated coldness
  • Whether they receive the no with grace, even when disappointed
Pattern 03

How they speak about their exes.

This is the most discussed pattern and the most misread. The common wisdom — "watch out if they only have terrible things to say about every ex" — is correct but incomplete. The deeper diagnostic is not whether the exes are villainised, but the consistency of the role the ex plays in every story. If every previous relationship ended because the other person was crazy, unstable, cheating, controlling — and no version of the breakup features anything your partner did or learned from — then you are not hearing about the exes. You are hearing about your partner's relationship to accountability.

What to watch for
  • Whether they can name one specific thing they would do differently in a previous relationship
  • Whether they can describe an ex with some genuine fondness, even after the ending
  • Whether the breakup narratives all feature them as the rational party and the ex as the unstable one
  • Whether they describe an ex in language that, repurposed, could be applied to you
  • How they speak about the person whose role you are currently filling
Pattern 04

How they recover from small ruptures with you.

In the first three months, there will be small ruptures — a miscommunication, a disappointment, a moment where one of you was tired and the other felt it. These are inevitable. What matters is not whether the ruptures happen, but the architecture of repair. The way someone repairs a small rupture in week eight is the way they will repair the larger ones in year three.

What to watch for
  • Whether they can acknowledge their part in a misunderstanding without minimising or maximising it
  • Whether the apology, when it comes, is for the impact or for getting caught
  • Whether they re-escalate the conflict by bringing up old material in the middle of repair
  • Whether they expect you to do the emotional work of getting back to good
  • Whether they avoid the rupture entirely by performing as if it never happened
A relationship is not predicted by what happens on the best days. It is predicted by what happens on the small ordinary days when nothing is at stake. Most people only ever look at the highlight reel.

You read the four patterns and one of them lands harder than the others.

There's a specific pattern where you can already name three examples from the relationship you're currently in, or from the one before. You weren't reading abstractly — you were reading a description.

Page 04 / 07 · The Override

What you ignored last time.

If you have been in dysfunctional relationships before — and if you are reading this course, the odds are very high that you have — you did not miss the early signals. You saw them. You felt them. And then, through a specific internal process, you decided to proceed anyway. Understanding that process is more important than the signals themselves.

This is the harder honesty of the entire course, and it is the only one that protects you from the next relationship looking exactly like the last. The red flags were available. The override was the work. Not the override of someone else's behaviour — the override of your own knowing.

This is not about blame. The override was almost always intelligent — it was the operation of a specific learned strategy that has kept you in relationships your whole adult life. The strategy was useful once. Naming it now is the only way to stop running it in the future.

The four overrides

The "but they're not always like that" override. The pattern is real, you see it, and then the kind moments — and there are always kind moments, especially early on — produce a recalibration. The good version becomes the "real" them. The difficult version becomes "stress" or "their wounding" or "a moment." You have just made the structural truth into an exception, and the exception into the truth. This is the most common override.

The "I'm being too picky" override. You notice the pattern and immediately convert it into a fault in yourself — your standards are too high, your previous relationship made you paranoid, you're projecting, you're sabotaging something good. The override comes from a deep training, usually installed in childhood, that your perceptions are not to be trusted. The pattern is true. The voice telling you you're being picky is what your last relationship sounded like, still speaking inside your head.

The "I can help them grow" override. You see the pattern and reframe it as a project — your love, your patience, your insight will help them become the person they almost are. This is the most romantic override and the most destructive. The pattern is not a development gap. It is a structure. People do change, but they change because they decide to and do the work. They do not change because you saw their potential. The relationship in which one person is constantly midwifing the other's growth is not a relationship. It is a long, exhausting service contract.

The "what if this is the last chance" override. Often the quietest one and the most powerful. You override the pattern because you do not believe better is available — for your age, your circumstances, your particular constellation of needs. The fear of being alone overrides the data about who you would be alone with. This override often hides under more dignified justifications, but it is usually the engine. Loneliness is a real cost. So is the wrong relationship. The mistake is treating them as the same currency.

Every dysfunctional relationship you have been in was preceded by a moment in which you saw what was coming and made a decision not to see it. Recovering that moment is the actual work. The pattern was the symptom.
Scenario · Sit with this

You think back to your last relationship and remember a specific moment in the first two months where you knew something.

It might have been an incident, a comment, a tone, a feeling. Whatever it was, you saw it — and then you watched yourself decide not to have seen it. The memory is uncomfortable because it confirms that the information was always there.

Page 05 / 07 · Pre-cognitive Signal

The body knows in week two.

Before your mind has language for what is wrong, your body has data. The chest tightness before a phone call you should be excited to take. The script you find yourself rehearsing before seeing them. The flicker of relief when they cancel — a flicker so small you almost miss it. These are not nothing. These are the earliest, most reliable signals you have, and they almost always arrive before week four.

The challenge is that you have probably been trained to ignore these signals — or worse, to interpret them as your problem. You were told you have attachment issues. You were told you sabotage good things. You were told your nervous system is broken from previous relationships. Some of these things may be partly true. None of them justifies overriding a body that is reporting a pattern your mind has not yet caught up to.

The five pre-cognitive signals

The chest tightness

Before specific kinds of conversations. Before they get home. After they leave a voice note you haven't listened to yet. The tightness is not anxiety in a vague sense — it is specifically directed. The body is reporting that something in the relationship is producing low-grade dread, even when nothing observable is wrong.

The script-running

You catch yourself, on the way to see them, rehearsing what you'll say. Practising how you'll deliver a piece of news. Anticipating their possible reactions and pre-formulating your responses to each. Healthy relationships do not require this level of advance preparation. The script-running is the body trying to manage a system it has already identified as unpredictable.

The relief at their absence

When they cancel, you feel a small flash of relief before the disappointment arrives. When you have a few days alone, your shoulders drop in a way they don't drop around them. You don't have to like every minute together — but the body's relief at the absence is one of the most reliable signals there is. It is almost impossible to fake, and almost impossible to override.

The friend-warning impulse

You find yourself, in conversation with friends, downplaying things you've already noticed. You skip the detail that would worry them. You edit yourself in a particular direction. The editing is the data. Healthy relationships are not stories you have to manage when telling them to people who love you. If you are protecting your partner from your closest friends' perception of them, your closest friends are seeing something you've already decided to ignore.

The aftermath fog

After certain interactions — not all, but a recognisable subset — you find yourself unable to think clearly for an hour or two afterwards. You replay the conversation trying to figure out what happened. You arrive at no clear conclusion. You feel slightly off but cannot articulate why. The fog is the body processing information it could not process in the moment. The fog is not your weakness. It is your evidence.

The body had the answer before you asked the question. Every relationship that hurt you was reported by your body in the first month. The work is learning to read the report before the bill arrives.
Scenario · Sit with this

You read the five signals and recognise at least three of them in a current or recent relationship.

The recognition arrives with an uncomfortable clarity. The body has been reporting for weeks. You have been overriding the reports. You can name now, precisely, what you were doing.

Page 06 / 07 · The Protocol

The ninety-day verification protocol.

Everything in this module has been preparing you for one practical structure — a way to use the first three months of any new relationship as the disclosure window they actually are. Not as a paranoid background check. As a deliberate practice of paying attention to what is already in front of you.

The protocol is not a list of demands. It does not require you to interrogate the person you are dating or to administer tests. It is, instead, a structure for what to watch for, what to remember, and what to test for yourself — quietly, on your own, without the other person ever needing to know there is a protocol at all. The protocol is for you. It is your sovereignty in action, before you have anything to lose.

Step 01

Keep a private record from week one.

Open a note, on paper or on your phone, that nobody else sees. Date the entries. When something small happens that you would otherwise explain away, write down exactly what occurred, exactly how you felt, and exactly what you told yourself about it. The record is not for confrontation later — it is for you, to prevent the rewriting that month four will perform on the memories of month one. Most people, by month five, no longer remember what month two was actually like. The note prevents that.

Step 02

Watch the four patterns deliberately.

In the first ninety days, you will have multiple natural opportunities to observe each of the four patterns from Page Three. You don't have to manufacture them. Restaurants happen. Service workers exist. You will tell them no about something. Conversations about exes will arise. Small ruptures will occur. When these moments come, pay deliberate attention — not as a test of them, but as data for you. Mentally note what you see. Add it to the record.

Step 03

Check the body once a week.

Once a week — Sunday evening is often a good time — ask yourself: what has my body been telling me this week? Where has it been tight? When did it relax? What did I feel before, during, and after each meaningful interaction with this person? The weekly check creates a longitudinal record of what your nervous system has been registering, which is information that single-moment introspection rarely captures.

Step 04

Tell one trusted friend the unedited version.

Find one person — not a chorus, one — and tell them, every two or three weeks, what is actually happening. The full version. The good and the small unease. The chemistry and the moment that didn't sit right. Tell them not because they will give you advice, but because the act of telling someone the unedited version prevents you from privately editing it into something more palatable. Healthy relationships survive being described accurately to people who love you. The ones that don't survive that telling were never going to survive year three.

Step 05

At day ninety, read the record before deciding anything.

On day ninety — or close to it — sit with the record. Read every entry. Look for patterns. Look for repeated overrides. Look for the moments where you noticed something and then watched yourself decide not to notice it. You are not deciding the future of the relationship in this reading. You are deciding what you actually know. The decision about the future comes from the knowing — and the knowing is what the record protects.

The protocol is not paranoia. It is the discipline of staying conscious during the months when the chemistry is doing its best work on your discernment. Nobody else will do this for you. The notebook is enough.
Scenario · Sit with this

You are about to start a new relationship — or you are six weeks into one. The protocol feels, in your gut, like too much.

There's a pull to skip it. Trust the connection. Stop overthinking. Just enjoy it. The pull feels reasonable. It also feels familiar.

Practice this week

If you are in a new relationship — or about to be — begin the record this week. One entry. No pressure. Just start the practice of keeping a private account that month-four-you will not be able to overwrite. If you are not in a new relationship, go back to the last one. Identify which override you used, and which signal you ignored. The recognition is the inoculation against repeating it.
