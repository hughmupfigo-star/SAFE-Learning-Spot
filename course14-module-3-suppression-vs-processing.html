Suppression is not the same as calm.

The most consequential and most consistently misunderstood distinction in the entire regulation framework is this: suppressing an emotion is not the same as being in a genuinely settled state. From the outside — and often even from the inside — they can appear identical. Physiologically, they are opposite in almost every relevant way.

A genuinely settled nervous system is one in which the emotion has completed its cycle: the physiological activation that accompanied it has risen, peaked, and discharged; the information it carried has been registered; and the system has returned, naturally and without effort, to baseline. The emotion is not absent — it can be recalled, thought about, even felt again — but it no longer has the same grip on the system. It has been processed.

A suppressed nervous system is something entirely different. The emotion has been interrupted mid-cycle — pushed down, covered over, or simply refused entry to conscious experience before it could complete. The physiological activation remains. The information was never registered. The system is not at rest. It is actively engaged in the effort of maintaining the suppression — an effort that is invisible to the person doing it and to those around them, but that consumes exactly the cognitive, emotional, and physical resources that genuine rest would otherwise restore.

Suppression is not rest. It is work. Chronic suppression is chronic work — the compounding cost of a system that has never been allowed to complete its cycles, maintaining ever-larger amounts of incomplete activation at the cost of the energy, the connection capacity, and the cognitive function that could otherwise be available.

The cultural pressure toward suppression is enormous and often invisible. "Stay professional." "Don't be so sensitive." "Get over it." "You're overreacting." These instructions, delivered consistently enough, do not teach emotional regulation. They teach emotional suppression — and the two could not be more different in their long-term effects.

The physiology of emotions.

Understanding what emotions actually are — physiologically, not conceptually — changes the entire way you approach working with them. Most people relate to emotions as though they are states of mind: psychological events that happen in the head. They are, in fact, primarily physiological events that happen in the body and travel upward to consciousness.

An emotion begins as a physiological cascade: a change in the autonomic nervous system's state that produces measurable changes in heart rate, breath pattern, blood flow, muscle tone, and hormonal release. This cascade is initiated before conscious awareness — before you "know" you are feeling the emotion, the body is already producing it. What we experience as the emotion — the label, the feeling, the sense of "I am angry" or "I am afraid" — is the brain's interpretation of the physiological cascade after the fact.

Research by Jill Bolte Taylor suggests that the average duration of the neurochemical cascade associated with a basic emotion, if allowed to complete uninterrupted, is approximately ninety seconds. Ninety seconds from the triggering event to the natural discharge of the physiological activation. What keeps emotions alive for hours, days, or decades is not the emotion itself but the thoughts about the emotion — the stories, the ruminations, the resistance — that re-trigger the cascade continuously, preventing it from completing.

The 90-second rule
If you can observe an emotion as a physiological event — noticing the body's response without immediately building a narrative around it — it will typically complete its physiological cycle within ninety seconds. The extension of emotional experience beyond ninety seconds is almost always the result of thought re-triggering the cascade, not the continuation of the original event.

This is not a licence to dismiss difficult emotions as temporary biochemistry. It is a reframe that makes it possible to work with them differently — to meet the physiological event with presence rather than resistance, to allow the wave to complete rather than fighting it.

What genuine processing involves.

Processing is not catharsis. It is not crying it out, expressing the emotion loudly, or narrating the feeling at length. It is not revisiting the event that produced the emotion in elaborate detail. It is not journaling, though journaling can sometimes create the conditions for processing to occur. Genuine processing is a specific physiological event — the completion of the emotional cascade — and it requires two things: presence and non-resistance.

Presence means being in contact with the emotion as it is occurring in the body — maintaining awareness of the physical sensations, the location in the body, the quality and texture of the experience — rather than moving away from it into thought, analysis, or narrative. Not describing the emotion. Not interpreting it. Not situating it in the story of what caused it. Simply being with what is happening, in the body, right now.

Non-resistance means allowing the physiological activation to complete its cycle without interrupting it. Not fighting the tears if tears want to come. Not suppressing the trembling if the body wants to tremble. Not cutting off the anger mid-expression to check whether it is appropriate. The body's completion of the emotional cycle — the shaking that discharges trauma, the sobbing that moves grief, the trembling that releases fear — is not a loss of control. It is the system completing what it started.

What gets in the way

Intellectualisation: moving into analysis before the physiological event has completed. The thought about the emotion replaces the emotion itself. Common in very intelligent people — the analysis becomes the escape. Catastrophising: the emotion triggers a narrative about what it means about the future, about oneself, about what will happen, and the narrative overwhelms the original feeling. Shame about the emotion: the secondary emotion — shame about feeling what is felt — interrupts the primary emotion's cycle and adds its own activation to the system. Premature resolution: reaching for meaning, lesson, or silver lining before the emotion has been allowed to complete.

Genuine processing does not require understanding the emotion. It requires being willing to feel it. The understanding — when it comes — tends to arrive naturally after the completion, as a quiet clarity, not as a replacement for the completion.

The completion cycle.

Peter Levine's somatic experiencing model introduces a concept that is among the most practically useful in the entire regulation literature: the completion cycle. Activation, in any organism, is designed to complete — to move from mobilisation through expression through discharge back to rest. When this cycle is interrupted, the incomplete activation is stored in the body as unfinished business. The regulation work, in significant part, is the work of allowing stored incomplete cycles to finally complete.

Levine observed that animals in the wild, after surviving a predator encounter, reliably exhibit a characteristic trembling and shaking behaviour before returning to their normal activities. This is not a sign of trauma. It is the completion of the stress cycle — the physiological discharge of the activation that the survival response produced. The animal does not carry the encounter forward. The cycle completed. The system reset.

Human beings, uniquely among animals, have the capacity to interrupt this completion process — through the prefrontal cortex's ability to suppress and override the body's impulses. We tremble and hold it in. We cry and stop ourselves. We feel the rage and manage it into professional presentation. These suppressions serve real social functions. Their cumulative cost — the accumulation of incomplete cycles — is the body burden that underlies much of what is described as chronic stress, unexplained physical symptoms, emotional numbness, and the persistent sense of being stuck.

Allowing completion

Allowing the completion cycle does not require recreating or revisiting traumatic events. The body carries the incomplete activation regardless of whether the mind is engaged with the original experience. Physical movement, vigorous exercise, shaking (spontaneous or deliberate), deep sobbing, even significant yawning — all of these can facilitate the discharge of stored activation without requiring the narrative engagement with the original event. The body can complete what it started, if given the conditions and the permission to do so.

Processing versus rumination.

The distinction between processing and rumination is the most practically important in this module — because many people are convinced they are doing the former when they are doing the latter, and the outcomes are as different as growth and erosion.

Processing is body-centred, present-timed, and finite. The physiological activation is felt, the cycle completes, and the activation discharges. Afterwards: a characteristic quality of relief, spaciousness, or quiet — not the absence of the feeling, but a change in the feeling's quality. The event that prompted the emotion feels different afterwards: further away, less charged, more integrated. Sometimes the processing produces clarity — an understanding of what the emotion was carrying — but this clarity is a byproduct of the completion, not a prerequisite for it.

Rumination is thought-centred, repetitive, and does not complete. The same event, the same feeling, the same narrative cycles through again and again — each cycle re-triggering the physiological activation, each cycle adding to the accumulated charge rather than discharging it. After rumination, the emotion has as much or more intensity than before — and the person is exhausted, having used significant resources without moving anything forward.

The diagnostic question

If you have been "processing" something for weeks with no reduction in emotional charge — if the conversation in your head is as fresh and activated today as it was the first day — you are almost certainly ruminating rather than processing. The question to ask is: am I moving toward the feeling, or am I moving around it? Processing requires contact with the physiological event. Rumination perpetually approaches it through the narrative, which maintains the activation without allowing the discharge.

The intervention for rumination is not more thinking. It is less thinking, combined with more body-directed attention. Ground the body, reduce the narrative, bring attention back to the physical sensation of the emotion itself. Let the feeling be felt rather than thought about. That shift — from the narrative level to the somatic level — is the shift from rumination to processing.

What suppressed emotion actually costs.

The cost of suppression is not primarily psychological — it is physiological, and the research on it is consistent enough to be considered established. An emotion that is not processed does not disappear. It is stored in the body as incomplete physiological activation, and it compounds.

The research: suppressing emotional expression does not reduce the physiological arousal associated with the emotion. It maintains the arousal while reducing the person's awareness of it. The body is still activated. The person has simply stopped registering the activation consciously. The result is the worst of both worlds: the ongoing physiological cost, without the signal that would motivate addressing it.

Long-term chronic suppression is associated in the literature with: elevated inflammatory markers (chronic activation of the stress response system produces systemic inflammation, which is implicated in a wide range of serious illnesses), immune suppression, disrupted sleep and HPA axis dysregulation, elevated cardiovascular risk, and the functional impairment of interoceptive awareness. The body is not a passive container for suppressed emotion. It is an active system that the suppression is continuously costing.

The relational cost

The suppressed person cannot be fully present in relationship. The ongoing effort of suppression uses exactly the attentional resources that genuine connection requires. The suppressed person often describes their relationships as superficial or characterised by a persistent sense of not being fully known — because the parts of them that are being suppressed are also the parts that, if expressed, would allow genuine intimacy. The suppression of vulnerability is the suppression of closeness.

What you suppress, you carry. The question is not whether you will pay the cost — you are already paying it. The question is whether the suppression is protecting you from something genuinely more costly, or whether it has become a habit so automatic and so normalised that it is no longer serving any purpose at all. For many people doing this work, it is the latter.

Creating the conditions for processing.

Genuine processing does not happen on demand. It requires conditions — physiological, environmental, and relational conditions that most people rarely have simultaneously. Part of the regulation work is building the consistent capacity to create those conditions.

Safety in the body

The nervous system will not open to emotional processing while it is in a high state of activation. The body needs to be within the window of tolerance for processing to occur. This means that the regulation tools in Module 4 — breath, movement, grounding — are not separate from the processing work. They are its prerequisite. They create the physiological conditions in which the nervous system is safe enough to feel.

Absence of the narrative

Processing requires, at least initially, a reduction in narrative engagement with the emotion. The story — what caused it, what it means, who is responsible, what should have been different — maintains the activation rather than allowing its discharge. Temporarily setting the story down (not abandoning it — just setting it aside while the physiological event completes) creates the space in which the body can do what it needs to do.

Permission

Perhaps the most overlooked condition: genuine permission to feel what is felt without it meaning something about your competence, your stability, your worth, or your identity. Many people cannot process because the processing — the tears, the trembling, the anger, the grief — feels like evidence of something catastrophic about themselves. The internal permission to feel, without interpretation, is itself a form of internal parenting — and it is often the condition that is most missing.

Module 4 builds the practical toolkit for regulation — the specific physiological tools for working with the nervous system. Everything in Module 4 is also, in part, the work of creating the conditions described here: safety in the body, reduced narrative pressure, and the growing permission to be present to what is actually happening inside you.

Practice this week

This week: When you notice an uncomfortable emotion — frustration, sadness, anxiety, irritability — resist the first impulse to explain, analyse, or escape it. Give it thirty to sixty seconds of body-directed attention: where is it, what is its texture, what does it feel like physically? Do not narrate. Just feel.

You are not trying to process everything this week. You are practising the first move: meeting the physiological event before the narrative arrives. Notice what happens when you do. Notice also what the narrative does when you try to set it down — that resistance is itself important information.
