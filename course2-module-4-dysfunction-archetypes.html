Page 01 / 07 · The Frame

The archetypes are diagnostic, not verdicts.

Before we name the four dysfunction archetypes, a careful frame. These are patterns of relating, not categories of people. A person is not a controller in the way they are a brunette. They run controlling patterns, in specific moments, often emerging from specific wounds, and almost always in combination with other patterns. The work of this module is to give you names for the patterns. The work is not to convert another human being into a label.

There are three reasons archetypal thinking is useful, and three reasons it is dangerous. Both lists are worth holding before you read further. Used well, the archetypes are a vocabulary that lets you recognise structural moves in real time. Used poorly, they become a way to flatten a complex person into a verdict, write them off, and skip the actual work.

Why naming the archetypes helps

The patterns are real, and they recur. If you have been in two or three dysfunctional relationships across your adult life, the chances are high that there were recognisable shapes in common — not random misfortune, not bad luck, but structural patterns repeating with different faces. Naming the patterns lets you stop treating each new relationship's dysfunction as a surprise.

The names interrupt the spell. While you are inside a relationship, the partner's behaviour usually feels personal, specific, unique to your situation. Naming the archetype creates distance. When you can think "this is the keeping-score move" rather than "they are mad at me about the laundry," the move loses some of its power. The naming converts the move from atmosphere into mechanism.

The names create a shared diagnostic language with your one trusted friend. If you have been keeping the practice from previous modules — telling one person the unedited version — having shared vocabulary makes those conversations more useful. You no longer have to describe everything from scratch. "It's the abandoner thing again" carries the entire conversation in one phrase. The friend can recognise it, and you can recognise it together.

Why naming the archetypes can do harm

People are more than one pattern. Most actual partners — including the most dysfunctional ones — run more than one archetype, in different proportions, in different moments. The partner who keeps score also occasionally controls. The controller occasionally abandons. Treating the archetype as the whole person flattens them in ways that distort your assessment.

Labels can become permission to disengage from the actual work. Once you have decided your partner "is" the controller, you can stop engaging with them as a person and start interacting with them as a category. This is easier than the work of seeing them clearly, and it is a form of the same flattening they have been doing to you. Recognising the pattern is the beginning of understanding. The label, treated as identity, is often the end of it.

Naming the partner's archetype without naming your own role is half the diagnostic. The most important page in this module is Page Six — your matching architecture. Each archetype finds a specific wound in its partner. Naming the partner's pattern without examining your own participation in the system tends to produce another relationship with the same architecture, just with a different person. The full picture requires both halves.

The archetypes are a map, not the territory. Held loosely, they let you navigate. Held tightly, they replace the navigation with a story you tell yourself about who someone is, and stories like that are usually wrong by year three.
Scenario · Sit with this

Before reading the four archetypes, you already think you know which one your partner is.

You have a label in mind, ready to be confirmed by what comes next. Some part of you is looking forward to the validation of your existing diagnosis.

Page 02 / 07 · Archetype One

The Controller.

The controller's defining operation is the management of your behaviour. Not always crudely. Often with sophistication. The control may not feel like control while it is happening — it may feel like care, like protection, like reasonable expectations, like the natural way a serious relationship operates. Recognising the controller is mostly the work of noticing how much of your daily behaviour has slowly come under another person's regulation.

The wound it emerges from

Most controllers were raised in environments of chaos, unpredictability, or felt powerlessness — sometimes from a parent's emotional volatility, sometimes from neglect, sometimes from being made responsible for a household before they were old enough. Control is what they learned to do with anxiety. Their nervous systems calmed only when they could predict the environment, and adult relationships became another environment to manage. This origin is not an excuse. It is context.

The architecture

The controller does not usually arrive demanding control. They arrive offering structure — preferences that feel reasonable, suggestions that seem helpful, an ease with making decisions for both of you. Over months, the preferences become expectations. The expectations become rules. The rules become the air in the relationship. You stop noticing when you stopped choosing what to wear to dinner.

How it scales
Milder forms
  • Strong opinions about your friends, clothes, career choices
  • Subtle pressure to do things their way "because it makes sense"
  • Tracking of your time without naming it as tracking
  • Becoming the one who makes all the decisions about both of you
  • Reactivity when you make choices without consulting them
More severe forms
  • Required check-ins, location-sharing, account monitoring
  • Isolation from friends and family framed as relationship loyalty
  • Financial control: combined accounts they manage, "allowances"
  • Decisions about your body — what you eat, wear, how you present
  • Punishment for autonomy, often expressed as withdrawal or rage
What it costs the person on the receiving end

Living with sustained control produces a particular kind of erosion. You lose access to your own preferences because they have been overridden so often that your nervous system has stopped generating them. You stop making decisions because the decisions get countermanded. By month eighteen, when someone asks what you want, you genuinely do not know — and the not-knowing is the harm, more than any individual override.

Control rarely arrives as control. It arrives as preference, then expectation, then rule, then climate. By the time you can name it, you can no longer remember the version of yourself that lived before it.
Scenario · Sit with this

You realise that most decisions in your relationship — small and large — have been made by them or in deference to them for the past year.

No one moment created this. There was no demand. There was just a series of small accommodations that became the structure. You don't quite know what you actually prefer for dinner tonight because you've gotten used to letting them choose.

Page 03 / 07 · Archetype Two

The Abandoner.

The abandoner's defining operation is withdrawal as a relationship strategy. Not as a single dramatic exit — most abandoners never actually leave. The withdrawal is the climate. They are present, technically, while being unreachable. They are in the room, while having left the conversation. They are in the relationship, while having quietly stopped showing up to it.

The wound it emerges from

Most abandoners learned, early, that intimacy was unsafe. Often a caregiver was emotionally inconsistent — present and warm, then suddenly distant, with no understandable pattern. The child's nervous system learned that connection was the precursor to loss, and built a defence: do not be fully present, and the disappearance cannot hurt you. This defence works for survival. It also produces an adult who cannot tolerate the closeness their partner expected when they began the relationship.

The architecture

The abandoner is often the partner who began the relationship most enthusiastically. The early months may have been full presence — attention, warmth, depth, the feeling of being chosen. Then, somewhere around month three or four or six, the temperature begins to shift. The texts get shorter. The conversations get thinner. The plans get harder to schedule. Nothing has happened. Nothing is wrong, they assure you. They are just busy. They are just tired. They are just going through something. The withdrawal is always presented as situational, even when it has become structural.

How it scales
Milder forms
  • Periodic emotional unavailability with vague explanations
  • Slow response times that they describe as their natural pace
  • Difficulty discussing the relationship itself, even when invited
  • A felt distance even when physically present
  • The recurring need for "space" without specifying what would end the need
More severe forms
  • Multi-day silent treatments after minor conflicts
  • Sudden disappearances followed by re-emergence as if nothing happened
  • Refusal to discuss the relationship's state, even in crisis
  • Threats to leave deployed strategically, never followed through
  • Withholding affection, sex, or warmth as the primary regulation tool
Why this archetype is the hardest to confront

The controller is doing something visible. The abandoner is doing nothing visible. They are absent. They are quiet. They are not engaging. How do you confront an absence? The conversation you want to have about the withdrawal requires them to be present for the conversation, and presence is precisely what they are withholding. Most attempts to address the abandoning produce more abandoning, as the conversation itself becomes another thing to withdraw from. This is the structural reason this archetype tends to outlast the other three: you cannot push back against someone who is not, technically, doing anything.

The abandoner is the only archetype who can leave the relationship while staying in it. The version of them you fell in love with did not vanish. It simply moved into a room you are no longer permitted to enter.
Scenario · Sit with this

Your partner has been gradually withdrawing for months and the latest silent stretch has lasted four days.

You haven't fought. You're not sure what triggered it. When you ask if everything is okay, you get short answers and assurances that they're "just busy." You can feel yourself doing more work to restore the connection than they are.

Page 04 / 07 · Archetype Three

The Provider Who Keeps Score.

The score-keeper's defining operation is the conversion of generosity into leverage. They give. They give visibly, often dramatically, sometimes generously beyond what was asked for. And then, somewhere down the line, the giving is invoked — sometimes once, sometimes constantly, sometimes only at the strategic moment when leverage is needed. The generosity was real. It was also being recorded.

The wound it emerges from

Most score-keepers were raised in households where love was transactional — where care had to be earned, where worth was measured by contribution, where being valued required producing value. They learned, structurally, that relationships are exchanges, and the partner who keeps the better books holds the better position. They are not always aware of the accounting. Many score-keepers experience their own giving as straightforwardly generous — until the moment when leverage is needed, at which point the ledger emerges, fully detailed, sometimes years old.

The architecture

The giving phase can look genuinely beautiful. The score-keeper may pay for things you couldn't afford, fly in to help when you were sick, put career or money or time toward your wellbeing in ways no previous partner did. You may, for the first time, feel materially or practically supported. The giving is real. It is also being entered into a ledger that you have not been shown. The first time the ledger is invoked — usually in a moment of conflict, often subtly — the entire history of the relationship reorganises in your mind. The gifts you thought were gifts were, retroactively, investments.

How it scales
Milder forms
  • Mentioning past favours during current disagreements
  • Visible disappointment when reciprocity does not match their giving
  • An internal scoreboard you can sense even though it is unspoken
  • Performances of generosity that seem to require witnessing
  • A quiet sense that you are perpetually slightly indebted
More severe forms
  • Detailed financial accounting deployed during fights
  • Career or financial sacrifices invoked to demand specific behaviour
  • The expectation that you owe specific actions in return for past help
  • Threats to "take it all back" if you do not perform gratitude correctly
  • Strategic giving designed to deepen your dependence
What it costs the person on the receiving end

The score-keeper relationship produces a specific kind of accumulated debt that is felt in the body. You cannot receive cleanly anymore. Every gift carries a small dread of the future invoice. You stop allowing yourself to enjoy generosity, because the enjoyment has become evidence of obligation. You begin pre-emptively reciprocating, often in excess of what was given, to manage your sense of the running tally. The relationship turns you into a bookkeeper of an account you never agreed to open.

A gift carries no expectation. A loan carries one. The score-keeper presents loans as gifts, and the difference becomes visible only when the call is made. By then, the interest has been compounding silently for years.
Scenario · Sit with this

During a disagreement, your partner invokes something they did for you eight months ago.

It catches you off guard. The thing was real. They did do it, and you were grateful. You'd thought of it as part of the relationship — something they wanted to do because they loved you. The fact that it is now being deployed as leverage reframes the original act in a way that feels destabilising.

Page 05 / 07 · Archetype Four

The One Who Needs Saving.

The fourth archetype is the most romantic, the most disguised, and the most exhausting. The one who needs saving is not, on the surface, dysfunctional in the way the other three are. They are not controlling. They are not abandoning. They are not keeping score. They are, however, structurally arranging the relationship so that one person — you — is the helper, and the other person — them — is the helped. And the help is the relationship.

The wound it emerges from

Most "needs saving" partners were children who learned that attention came primarily when they were suffering. The crisis was the connection. The struggle was what produced their parent's softer presence, or the only time anyone asked how they were. As adults, they have unconsciously reproduced the environment that gave them what they needed — a partner whose love is most reliably accessed when they are in some form of crisis. The crisis is not the obstacle to the relationship. The crisis is the relationship.

The architecture

The needs-saving partner often arrives sympathetic, vulnerable, in some kind of ongoing difficulty — career struggles, mental health, family of origin issues, addiction, financial chaos. The difficulties are usually real. They are not invented. But the structural feature is this: when one difficulty resolves, another emerges, often within weeks. There is always something. Sustained periods of stability rarely occur, and when they do, the partner often seems somehow less engaged with you during them — as if the dynamic only knows how to operate around an active difficulty.

How it scales
Milder forms
  • Patterns of small recurring crises that always require your support
  • Difficulty taking advice that would resolve the recurring problems
  • The dynamic feels off during periods of ease, settles back during crisis
  • You are described as their saviour, their rock, the one who keeps them going
  • Your own difficulties are often minimised or quickly redirected back to theirs
More severe forms
  • Threats of self-harm or breakdown when you attempt to set limits
  • The active sabotage of solutions, often after asking for help to find them
  • Financial dependence that grows year over year despite your support
  • The relationship cannot survive your having needs of your own
  • You are responsible for their survival, framed as love
Why this archetype is uniquely exhausting

The other three archetypes give you, at minimum, the dignity of being recognised as a separate person. The controller engages with you, even controllingly. The abandoner withdraws from you, but the withdrawal acknowledges that you exist as someone to withdraw from. The score-keeper, even at their worst, sees you as a counterparty. The needs-saving partner often does not quite see you at all. You exist primarily as the helper, the witness, the container for their experience. Your inner life, your difficulties, your needs — these are visible to them only when they are not in crisis, which is rarely. The exhaustion is in being chronically present for someone who is structurally unable to be chronically present for you.

Helping someone you love through difficulty is one of the deepest acts of partnership. Being the permanent helper in a relationship that requires the other person to stay broken is a different thing entirely. The two look the same from year one. By year three, the difference is unmistakable.
Scenario · Sit with this

You realise that your relationship has been organised around your partner's recurring crises for most of its length.

The crises are real. They are not invented. But you cannot remember the last sustained period in which your partner was simply okay — not because life is hard, but because the dynamic itself seems to require that they not be.

Page 06 / 07 · The Matching Architecture

Your role in the system.

This is the most important page in the entire module, and the one most people would prefer to skip. Each archetype is one half of a system. The other half is the role that makes the archetype possible — the partner who reliably participates in a way that allows the dysfunctional pattern to continue. Without that participant, the controller cannot control, the abandoner cannot abandon usefully, the score-keeper has no debtor, and the needs-saving partner has no saviour.

Two careful things before we proceed. First — this page is not about blaming you for the relationship you have been in. Identifying your participation in the system is not the same as identifying yourself as the cause of the dysfunction. Your role is a real piece of the architecture, but the architecture has two roles, and naming one does not absolve the other. You can be precisely accurate about your participation and entirely clear that the dysfunction was not yours to fix alone.

Second — this is not the "you attract what you are" framing. That framing is lazy and often cruel. You did not attract dysfunction because you are dysfunctional. You participated in a system because you carried specific wounds that made you a structurally good fit for it. Wounds are not moral failures. They are the result of a history you did not author. The work is to recognise the wound, not to assign blame for having it.

The four matching architectures
Controller · matches with

The over-explainer

If you were raised in an environment where your feelings had to be justified, where your decisions required you to defend them, where having a position meant having to argue for it — you became an adult fluent in justification. The controller does not control by force. They control by requiring your reasoning, then dismissing it, then offering theirs as the more reasonable position. You participate by continuing to explain. Each explanation is a small invitation to override. The controller takes the invitation. By month eighteen, you have stopped having unjustified preferences, because every preference requires defending. The matching architecture is not that you are weak. It is that you were trained to debate your own desires.

Abandoner · matches with

The chronic restorer

If you were raised in an environment of inconsistent affection — where a parent's warmth was unreliable and you became the family's emotional repair worker — you became an adult who reflexively closes distance whenever it appears. The abandoner withdraws. You restore. They cool. You warm. They go silent. You initiate the conversation that brings them back. The system works because your restoration prevents them from ever having to face the consequences of their own withdrawal. The matching architecture is not that you are needy. It is that you were trained, very young, that closeness was your job to maintain.

Score-keeper · matches with

The debt-acceptor

If you were raised in an environment where love was transactional — where being good required earning it, where you carried a permanent low-grade sense of being not quite enough — you became an adult who accepts unspoken debts as part of how love operates. The score-keeper extends generosity. You absorb it as obligation, often before they have called it in. You pre-emptively over-give to manage a ledger they have not yet referenced. The system works because your acceptance of indebtedness keeps both of you operating in their preferred currency. The matching architecture is not that you are calculating. It is that you were taught that worth must be paid for.

Needs-saving partner · matches with

The compulsive rescuer

If you were raised in an environment that required you to manage someone else's wellbeing — a depressed parent, an addicted sibling, a chaotic household — you became an adult who reliably positions yourself as the helper. Your nervous system calmed when you were useful. Other people's emergencies were familiar territory. The needs-saving partner gives you a permanent supply of what your childhood trained you to do — manage someone else's chaos as a substitute for managing your own life. The system works because your rescuing instinct provides the structure their crisis requires. The matching architecture is not that you have a saviour complex. It is that you were given a job too young, and you became excellent at it.

What this recognition is for

The point of naming your matching architecture is not to assign you partial blame for the dysfunction. It is to identify the structural feature that, if it does not change, will reliably pull you into another version of the same system with a different person. The partner is replaceable. The architecture is what travels with you. Until the matching architecture is recognised, the next relationship will tend to have the same shape, because the same signal is being broadcast.

The recognition does two things. It frees you from the narrative that you have terrible luck or pick wrong — neither of which is accurate, both of which keep you helpless. And it gives you something to actually work on: the specific learned response that pulls you, reflexively, into your half of these dynamics. Working on the architecture is hard, slow, lifelong work. It is also the only intervention that changes which relationships become available to you in the future.

A final note that holds the whole module together. The presence of your matching architecture does not make the partner's behaviour acceptable. The controller is still controlling. The abandoner is still abandoning. The score-keeper is still keeping score. The needs-saving partner is still requiring you to remain in the helper role. Recognising your participation in the system is not the same as accepting responsibility for the system. Both parties are running their architectures. Both are responsible for the part they bring. Neither's recognition cancels the other's.

A dysfunctional relationship has two roles, and both are required for the dance. Naming your role is not surrendering to the idea that you caused the dance. It is recognising that the music will play again, with someone new, until you have changed the step you keep performing.
Scenario · Sit with this

You recognise yourself in one of the four matching architectures, clearly and specifically.

The recognition produces two simultaneous feelings — a clarity about why the same shape of relationship keeps finding you, and a slight defensiveness, as if naming your role is a way of letting the partner off the hook.

Module Complete
✓

You've completed Module Four.

You now have names for the four most recognisable dysfunction archetypes in adult relationships — and, more importantly, names for the four matching architectures in yourself. The combination is the diagnostic. Without your half, the recognition is incomplete. With it, you can finally see the system rather than blaming either of its participants.

What you carry forward
  1. 01The archetypes are patterns of relating, not verdicts on people. Held loosely, they are diagnostic. Held tightly, they replace the person with a category.
  2. 02The Controller manages your behaviour through preference, expectation, rule, and climate. Most controllers were raised in chaos and learned that control was how anxiety became manageable.
  3. 03The Abandoner is present while being unreachable. Withdrawal is the operation. You cannot confront an absence — which is the structural reason this archetype tends to outlast the others.
  4. 04The Score-Keeper converts generosity into leverage. The gifts were real. They were also being recorded. The ledger emerges in the moment leverage is needed.
  5. 05The One Who Needs Saving organises the relationship around their crises because crisis is how they learned love operates. There is always something. The crisis is the relationship.
  6. 06Each archetype matches a specific architecture in you: the over-explainer, the chronic restorer, the debt-acceptor, the compulsive rescuer. Recognising both halves is the diagnostic. Neither half's recognition cancels the other half's responsibility.
Practice this week

Identify the matching architecture in yourself with the most precision you can manage. Not the one you wish were true. The one that actually fits when you look at the relationships you've been in across your adult life. Write down, privately, the situations in which you most reliably run that architecture — the moments when you over-explain, restore, accept the debt, rescue. The naming itself is most of the work. Without acting on it. Without confronting anyone. Just the noticing, over the course of one week.

The next module — Your Role in Dysfunction — goes deeper into the matching architecture. Why these patterns were installed in you. How to work with them rather than against them. And how recognising your role becomes the precondition for choosing differently the next time.
