Page 01 / 04 · The Unglamorous Basics

Sleep, food, move, connect.

If you need help right now

Call or text 988 (Suicide & Crisis Lifeline) · text HOME to 741741 (Crisis Text Line) · call 911 if you’re in immediate danger. Free, confidential, 24/7.

The least exciting advice in mental health is also the most reliable. Sleep, food, movement, and connection are the floor everything else stands on — and when they slip, everything gets harder.

None of these is a cure, and all of them quietly matter more than they get credit for. Sleep is the big one — too little wrecks mood, focus, and emotional control, so protecting it (a consistent-ish bedtime, less screen at 2am) pays off fast. Food and water: being under-fed or dehydrated mimics and worsens anxiety and low mood, so eating something regularly is genuinely self-care. Movement, even a short walk, is one of the most evidence-backed mood lifters there is. Connection — a little real contact with people — buffers almost everything.

You don't have to overhaul your life or do them perfectly. When you're struggling, the basics are the first things to wobble and the highest-leverage things to nudge back. ‘I'm spiralling — have I slept, eaten, moved, or talked to anyone today?’ is a surprisingly powerful checklist. Often the answer points straight at the fix.

Be kind to the body that carries you

Think of these as charging your battery, not chores you're failing at. On a bad day, doing one of them — a glass of water, ten minutes outside, a text to a friend, an early night — is a real win, not a bare minimum. Lower the bar and celebrate the small stuff. Steadiness is built from tiny, repeated, kind acts toward yourself, not heroic overhauls.

When everything feels too big, ask: have I slept, eaten, moved, or connected today? The answer is often where to start.

Page 02 / 04 · Your Personal Plan

Write your own hard-day plan.

The worst moment is the wrong time to figure out what helps — your thinking brain is offline. So you build the plan now, while you're calm, and follow it later, when you're not.

A simple plan, kept in your notes app, might have four parts. My warning signs (the amber lights from Module 2). Things that help me (the grounding and soothing tools from Module 3 — the two or three that actually work for you). People I can reach (your trusted adult, a good friend, plus 988 and text HOME to 741741). One first step for a bad moment (‘text [name],’ ‘5-4-3-2-1,’ ‘go outside’). That's it — short enough to actually use.

The power of writing it down is that you don't have to think when you're flooded — you just follow your own instructions, like a fire drill. Calm-you is looking after future-struggling-you. It's one of the kindest, most practical things you can do for yourself, and it takes ten minutes.

If you're ever unsafe

Make one line of your plan crystal clear for the worst moments: if I'm thinking about hurting myself or don't feel safe, I will call or text 988, text HOME to 741741, tell [trusted person], or call 911. Having that decided in advance — so it's a step you follow, not a decision you make in the dark — can make all the difference. You deserve a plan that keeps you safe.

Decide your hard-day moves while you're calm. When the storm hits, you won't have to think — you'll just follow the plan you made with love.

Page 03 / 04 · Carrying Hope

This gets better — and you’re not stuck.

You've built a real toolkit. The last and most important thing to carry out of this course is hope — not the fragile kind, but the grounded kind that knows hard times move and people do get through.

Whatever you're carrying right now, it is not your permanent address. Young people who feel hopeless in one season very often look back, later, amazed at how much shifted — relationships, circumstances, their own minds. You are still growing, still changing, and the brain you have at your lowest is not the brain you'll have for life. Things that feel unbearable now have a real way of becoming bearable, then survivable, then sometimes even a source of strength.

You don't have to feel hopeful to act hopeful. On the days the hope isn't there, you can borrow it: from the tools that have worked before, from the people who care, from the simple fact that you're still here and still trying — which is, itself, a quiet kind of courage. Keep using the lifelines. Keep reaching for one person. Keep going. It matters more than you know.

You did a brave thing

Taking a course about your own mental health, when the world made support harder to find, is not a small thing — it's you refusing to be left to cope alone. That instinct, to learn to care for your mind and to reach for help, is exactly what carries people through. Be proud of it. And come back to these tools whenever you need them; they're yours now, for good.

You are not stuck, and you are not alone. Whatever this season is, it moves — and you've just learned how to move with it.
