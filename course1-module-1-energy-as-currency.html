Page 01 / 06 · The Principle

Your energy is finite.

Whether emotional, psychological, or spiritual — your capacity to give, care, and engage is a resource that depletes with use and requires recovery time.

This single recognition changes everything. Once you understand that your energy has a cost, every relationship becomes a transaction. Not every transaction is malicious — but every transaction has a price, and you need to decide whether the exchange is fair.

The Invisible Exchange

In every interaction there is an invisible movement of energy. Someone is either adding to your reserves or drawing from them. Some relationships are neutral. Many are quietly extractive.

The problem begins when you were taught that your energy should be limitless — especially in relationships. You absorbed the message that loving means giving until there's nothing left. That caring means sacrificing your own stores. That friendship means being available regardless of the cost to yourself.

This belief — that infinite giving is synonymous with love — is how people learned to mine you.
Scenario · What would you do?

It's Sunday evening. You've finally sat down with a book after a draining week.

Your phone lights up. A friend who only calls when they need something is ringing. You know this conversation will last at least an hour and will be entirely about their problems.

Page 02 / 06 · The Cost

When the reserves run dry.

When your energy is chronically depleted, everything suffers. Your immune system weakens. Your judgement becomes impaired. Your work loses its edge. Your patience evaporates.

But the deepest cost is not what depletion does to your body or your output. It is what depletion does to your discernment.

A depleted person cannot tell the difference between someone who loves them and someone who needs them. They cannot tell the difference between a relationship that nourishes and one that consumes. They lose the ability to register their own warning signals — the tight chest, the dread before a phone call, the way certain people's names make the room feel smaller.

The Slow Disappearance

This is the insidious nature of energy extraction. It doesn't happen in one dramatic event. It happens gradually, imperceptibly, until you realise you've become a shell that looks occupied but feels hollowed out.

You will not notice the day you stopped laughing at small things. You will not notice the day you started flinching at your own phone. You will only notice, eventually, that the person you used to be is no longer accessible to you — and you cannot quite remember when she left.

Learning to measure and protect your energy is not selfish. It is survival.
Scenario · What would you do?

You notice you've been exhausted for three weeks straight.

Sleep isn't fixing it. Weekends aren't fixing it. Looking back at your calendar, you realise you've had six emotional support conversations this week — all initiated by other people.

Page 03 / 06 · Recognition

Reading the exchange.

Once you accept that energy is currency, the next skill is learning to read the ledger — to feel, in real time, whether an interaction is adding to your reserves or drawing from them.

Most people cannot do this. They were trained to override the signal. They were taught that the discomfort they feel around certain people is their own failure — they should be more patient, more understanding, more generous. So they suppress the data their body is giving them, and they keep paying.

Three Signals to Trust

The body knows first. Before your mind has worked out what's happening, your shoulders have tightened. Your breathing has changed. There is a heaviness in your chest that wasn't there twenty minutes ago. Your body is not betraying you. It is reporting.

The aftermath tells the truth. After some people, you feel energised, clearer, more yourself. After others, you feel small, anxious, foggy. You may even feel ashamed without being able to identify what you did wrong. That feeling is the price tag.

The asymmetry compounds. Notice who reaches out when they need something and who reaches out simply because they were thinking of you. Notice who asks how you are and stays for the answer. Notice who remembers what you said last time.

You don't have to label someone a villain to recognise that the exchange is uneven. You only have to be honest about the cost.
Scenario · What would you do?

After every coffee with a particular colleague, you feel inexplicably drained for the rest of the day.

They've never done anything obviously wrong. They're "nice." But you can't focus afterwards, and you find yourself replaying the conversation in your head with a low, queasy feeling.

Page 04 / 06 · The First Decision

Deciding what the exchange is worth.

You will not be able to remove every extractive person from your life. You will not always have that luxury. But you can begin to make conscious decisions about what each exchange costs you — and what you are willing to pay.

This is the beginning of sovereignty. Not cutting everyone off. Not building a fortress. But moving from unconscious payment — giving energy without registering the cost — to conscious exchange — knowing what something will take from you, and deciding, on purpose, whether to spend.

A New Internal Question

Before every interaction that you suspect will be costly, ask yourself one question: "Am I choosing to pay this, or am I being mined?"

If you are choosing to pay — because the person matters, because the situation calls for it, because love sometimes asks for sacrifice — then pay with your whole chest and do not resent it.

If you are being mined — if the payment is automatic, expected, never reciprocated, and resented — then the exchange must change. Not all at once. Not with announcements or ultimatums. But the ledger has to be acknowledged, first to yourself.

Awareness is not the end of the work. Awareness is the beginning of every decision that follows.
Scenario · What would you do?

You've recognised that a long-standing friendship is extractive. What is the most sovereign first move?

This person isn't cruel. They're not even aware of what they're doing. But the exchange has been uneven for years, and you're finally able to see it clearly.

Page 05 / 06 · The Other Side

What exchange feels like when it's right.

The work of this module is not only about identifying extraction. It is equally about learning to recognise the opposite — exchanges that replenish rather than deplete.

These relationships exist. They may be fewer than you would like, and they may be quieter than the extractive ones, but they are present in most lives if you know what to look for. The problem is that people trained to over-give often cannot identify nourishment when it is offered. They distrust it. They find it bland compared to the intensity of extraction.

What Reciprocal Exchange Feels Like

You leave more yourself, not less. After a genuine exchange, you feel settled rather than scattered. Your thoughts are cleaner. There is a quality of being met — not just heard, but actually encountered by someone who was present for the whole of you, not just the parts that serve them.

The cost is acknowledged, even if not spoken. Both people are aware they are bringing something and receiving something. There is no performance of infinite availability, no pretence that giving costs nothing. The exchange is honest about its weight.

You are remembered between interactions. They bring back things you said last time. They notice changes in you. They are interested in your interior — not as material for their own processing, but as a genuine curiosity about who you are becoming.

The goal is not relationships free of cost. It is relationships where the cost is mutual, acknowledged, and worth it.
Scenario · What would you do?

After a long phone call with a close friend, you notice something unexpected — you feel lighter than before you picked up.

The conversation covered difficult things. You talked about real problems. But when it ended, your chest felt open rather than compressed, and you found yourself wanting to call them again soon.
