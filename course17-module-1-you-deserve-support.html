Page 01 / 04 · Where Things Stand

Your mind matters — budget cuts or not.

If you need help right now

Call or text 988 (Suicide & Crisis Lifeline) · text HOME to 741741 (Crisis Text Line) · call 911 if you’re in immediate danger. Free, confidential, 24/7.

In 2025, about $1 billion that paid for school counselors and social workers was cut across the US. If getting support feels harder than it should right now, you're not imagining it — and it isn't your fault.

Here's the honest picture, kept simple: after the 2022 Uvalde school shooting, Congress set aside about a billion dollars to put more mental health professionals in schools. In 2025 that funding was ended, and many districts had to lay counselors and social workers off. Fewer adults, longer waitlists, less time per student. That's a real change, and it's reasonable to feel let down by it.

But here's the part that matters most: a budget decision in Washington says nothing about whether your mind deserves care. It does. Your stress, your sadness, your anxiety, your worst nights — they're real and worth tending to, no matter what got defunded. This course exists to put practical tools in your hands and show you the help that still exists, for free.

What this course is — and isn't

This is a toolkit, not a treatment. The skills here are genuinely useful and used by professionals, and they can help you steady yourself day to day. But they don't replace a real human professional, and they're not meant to. Think of it like learning first aid: hugely worth having, and also not a reason to skip the doctor when you need one.

You'll see the crisis box at the top of every module. That's on purpose. If things ever reach the point where you're thinking about hurting yourself or you don't feel safe, please use it — those lines are free, confidential, and answered by people trained for exactly that moment.

A funding cut can take away a counselor. It cannot take away your right to care for your own mind.

Page 02 / 04 · The Help That Still Exists

Free help is one text away.

Even with school budgets cut, the biggest US support lines are still free, still confidential, and still there 24 hours a day — and most people have no idea how much they can do.

Save these right now — your notes app, the back of your hand, anywhere. 988: call or text the Suicide & Crisis Lifeline any time, for anything from ‘I'm in danger’ to ‘I'm just really struggling and need someone.’ Text HOME to 741741: the Crisis Text Line, if texting feels easier than talking. 911: for immediate physical danger. These cost nothing and don't go on any school record.

A myth worth busting: you do not have to be suicidal to use 988. People reach out when they're overwhelmed, panicking, grieving, can't stop crying, or just can't carry it alone tonight. The person who answers is trained to listen without judgement and help you find a next step. Using it early, before things hit rock bottom, is exactly what it's for.

More free US resources

For ongoing help rather than a crisis line: SAMHSA's National Helpline (1-800-662-4357) gives free, confidential referrals to local treatment, including low-cost and sliding-scale options. findtreatment.gov maps services near you. If you're LGBTQ+ and want someone who gets it, The Trevor Project is free 24/7: call 1-866-488-7386 or text START to 678-678. We'll build on this in Module 4.

You don't have to be at your lowest to deserve a lifeline. ‘I just can't carry this alone right now’ is reason enough.

Page 03 / 04 · You're Not Alone In This

A whole country of students feels this too.

When support gets cut, it's easy to feel singled out and isolated. But millions of young people are navigating the same gap right now — and that shared reality is actually a source of strength.

Mental health struggles among teens and young adults were common well before any of this — roughly a third of high-schoolers report persistent sadness, and many more wrestle with anxiety, pressure, and loneliness. You are not a rare broken exception; you're part of a very large, very normal group carrying heavy things. That's not depressing — it's the opposite. It means support, language, and community already exist for this.

It also means the people around you — friends, classmates, online communities, family — are far more likely to understand than you'd think. The instinct when we're low is to hide and assume no one gets it. Usually, more people than you'd guess have been exactly where you are, and would help if they knew.

Hiding vs. connecting

Struggling quietly and alone is the default for a lot of people, and it's the setting that makes everything harder. A big part of this course is gently turning that default around — toward naming what's happening, reaching one safe person, and letting support in. You don't have to broadcast anything to anyone. You just have to stop carrying all of it solo.

Isolation whispers that you're the only one. You're not — not even close. And that changes what's possible.
