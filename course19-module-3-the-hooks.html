Page 01 / 04 · Built to Hold You

Designed like a slot machine.

It isn’t a lack of willpower that keeps people scrolling for hours. These products are deliberately engineered to be hard to put down, using some of the same mechanics that make gambling compulsive.

The core trick is the variable reward. When you pull to refresh or scroll on, you don’t know what you’ll get — maybe something brilliant, maybe nothing. That uncertainty is exactly what makes a slot machine addictive: the brain keeps pulling the lever for the unpredictable hit. A feed is a slot machine you can carry in your pocket, for free, forever.

Two more design choices remove your natural stopping points. Infinite scroll means the page never ends, so there’s no ‘you’ve reached the bottom’ moment to prompt you to stop. Autoplay means the next video starts before you’ve decided to watch it, so leaving requires an active choice while staying requires nothing. The friction is all on the side of stopping.

No natural ending

Older media had built-in stops: a book ends, a TV episode finishes, a newspaper runs out of pages. Those endings gave you a moment to choose whether to continue. Feeds deliberately removed them. When you notice there’s no natural place to stop, that’s not your failing — it’s the design, and naming it helps you create your own stopping points.

A book ends. A feed never does — and that’s not an accident, it’s the design.

Page 02 / 04 · Notifications & FOMO

The little red dot wants something.

Notifications feel like helpful updates. Mostly they’re a tool to pull you back into the app — and they work by hijacking two powerful human instincts: curiosity and the fear of missing out.

That red badge, the buzz, the ‘someone tagged you’ — these are engineered interruptions designed to break whatever you’re doing and return you to the feed. They’re often deliberately vague (‘you have new activity’) because uncertainty makes you curious enough to check. Each check is another session, another chance to keep you scrolling. The notification isn’t really for you; it’s for the app.

FOMO — the fear of missing out — is the other lever. Streaks that ‘break’ if you don’t return daily, stories that vanish in 24 hours, the sense that everyone’s at the thing you’re not: these manufacture urgency so you feel you have to keep checking. Much of it is artificial scarcity, designed to make missing the app feel like a loss.

Manufactured urgency

The freeing realisation is that almost none of it is actually urgent. The world will not end if a streak breaks or you check in three hours late. Once you see notifications and FOMO as levers being pulled on you rather than real emergencies, you can turn most notifications off — and discover how little you actually miss.

Most notifications aren’t news you need. They’re a leash, dressed up as a favour.

Page 03 / 04 · Dark Patterns

Spot the trick, take the wheel.

‘Dark patterns’ are design choices made to trick you into doing what the company wants rather than what you want. The good news: a trick you can see can’t work on you — so naming them hands you back control.

Common examples: making an account easy to create but a nightmare to delete; burying the privacy settings while the ‘share everything’ option is one tap; pre-ticked boxes that opt you into things; ‘just one more’ prompts when you try to leave; guilt-tripping wording (‘No thanks, I don’t want to improve’). None of these are accidents — they’re engineered nudges that exploit the fact that most people take the path of least resistance.

Once you start spotting dark patterns, you see them everywhere, and they lose their grip. The instinct to cultivate is mild suspicion: when an app makes one choice suspiciously easy and another suspiciously hard, ask ‘who benefits from me doing the easy thing?’ Usually it’s them, and that’s your cue to slow down and choose deliberately.

Take back the controls

Recognition pairs with action. You can defang most of the hooks with a few settings: turn off non-essential notifications, set app time limits, keep your phone out of the bedroom at night, try greyscale mode (a grey screen is far less compelling), and remove the most compulsive apps from your home screen. None of this requires heroic willpower — it just removes the triggers before they reach you.

You can’t be manipulated by a trick you can see. Naming the hook is half of escaping it.
