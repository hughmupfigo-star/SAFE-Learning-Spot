What Ultra-Processed Food Actually Is

Not all processed food is equal. Cutting a vegetable is processing. Fermenting food is processing. What this module addresses is something distinct — ultra-processed food: industrial formulations made primarily from substances extracted from food or synthesised in laboratories.

The NOVA classification system, developed by nutritional researchers, distinguishes four categories of food based on the degree and purpose of processing. Ultra-processed foods — the fourth category — are not modified versions of real food. They are industrial products that use minimal whole food ingredients and instead combine refined starches, sugars, fats, flavourings, colourings, emulsifiers, and preservatives to produce something that mimics the experience of eating without delivering the substance of it.

Crisps, breakfast cereals, packaged breads, fizzy drinks, flavoured yogurts, instant noodles, processed meats, most fast food — these are ultra-processed. They now make up more than half of the calories consumed in many Western countries. Understanding what they are is the beginning of understanding what they do.

What Refined Sugar Does

Sugar is not just empty calories. In its refined form — stripped of the fibre, minerals, and water that accompany it in natural fruit — sugar is one of the most disruptive substances the modern diet delivers to the body.

When refined sugar enters the bloodstream, insulin spikes sharply to manage it. The liver, receiving more fructose than it can process, begins converting the excess to fat — contributing to fatty liver disease, the fastest-growing liver condition in the world. Chronically elevated insulin drives inflammation throughout the body, disrupts hormone balance, and over time produces insulin resistance — the metabolic root of Type 2 diabetes.

Refined sugar also feeds the harmful bacteria and yeasts in the gut — particularly Candida — at the expense of beneficial microbes. It depletes the body of B vitamins and minerals that are required to metabolise it but are absent from the refined product itself. It ages the skin through a process called glycation, where sugar molecules bind to collagen and break it down. And it is addictive — triggering dopamine responses similar in mechanism to other addictive substances.

Seed Oils and Silent Inflammation

The oils that dominate modern cooking and food production — sunflower, canola, corn, soybean — are a recent industrial invention. They are also one of the most significant dietary drivers of chronic inflammation in the modern world.

These oils are extremely high in omega-6 fatty acids. Omega-6 and omega-3 are both essential fats, but they have opposing effects: omega-6 is pro-inflammatory when in excess; omega-3 is anti-inflammatory. The body requires a balance — traditionally around 4:1 or lower. In the modern diet dominated by seed oils, the ratio has reached 20:1 or higher. The result is a body in a state of chronic, low-grade inflammation — the silent driver behind heart disease, arthritis, eczema, depression, and many cancers.

Seed oils are extracted using high heat and chemical solvents — processes that produce toxic by-products including aldehydes. They are found in almost every packaged food, every restaurant fryer, and most commercial cooking. Replacing them with cold-pressed olive oil, coconut oil, avocado oil, and the natural fats in seeds and nuts is one of the most significant anti-inflammatory dietary changes available.

04 / 09

Engineered to Addict

Junk food is not accidentally irresistible. Food scientists are paid to make it that way. The inability to stop eating it is not a moral failing — it is the product of deliberate engineering.

Food companies employ scientists to find what researchers call the "bliss point" — the precise combination of fat, sugar, and salt that maximises palatability and minimises the brain's ability to register fullness. This combination does not exist in nature. Natural foods contain fat, sugar, or salt — rarely all three in engineered ratios. When the bliss point is hit, the brain's dopamine reward system responds similarly to how it responds to addictive substances — releasing a hit of pleasure and priming the body to want more before the current portion is finished.

The result is a product designed not to satisfy but to perpetuate consumption. You finish the packet and want another. You eat until the packet is empty, not until you are nourished. The hunger this creates is not a natural hunger — it is a manufactured one. Recognising this mechanism is the first step to being free of it.

What Junk Food Does to the Gut

The gut microbiome — the vast community of bacteria, fungi, and other organisms living in your digestive tract — governs immunity, mental health, hormone balance, and more. Junk food is devastating to it.

A healthy gut microbiome requires diversity — hundreds of different microbial species living in balance. Refined sugar selectively feeds harmful bacteria and yeasts while starving beneficial ones. Emulsifiers — found in almost all packaged food — have been shown to break down the mucus layer that protects the gut lining, allowing bacteria to come into direct contact with gut wall cells and triggering inflammation. Artificial sweeteners disrupt the microbiome even without calories. Antibiotics in conventionally raised meat wipe out gut flora indiscriminately.

A depleted microbiome creates its own cravings — the harmful bacteria and yeasts that thrive on junk food produce chemical signals that influence the brain's food preferences, creating a cycle in which junk food depletes the microbiome and the depleted microbiome craves more junk food. Breaking this cycle requires consistent introduction of living, fermented, and fibre-rich whole food that feeds and rebuilds beneficial microbial communities.

06 / 09

Hormonal Disruption

The endocrine system — the body's hormonal communication network — is exquisitely sensitive to chemical inputs. Junk food delivers a constant stream of compounds that interfere with it in ways that medicine has been slow to acknowledge.

Refined sugar chronically elevates insulin and drives cortisol dysregulation — the stress hormone that, when chronically elevated, promotes fat storage particularly around the abdomen, disrupts sleep, suppresses immune function, and accelerates ageing. Processed food packaging — particularly plastics — leaches xenoestrogens: synthetic compounds that mimic oestrogen in the body. Chronic exposure to these compounds is associated with hormonal imbalance, early puberty in children, impaired fertility, and increased cancer risk.

Many artificial additives also function as endocrine disruptors — interfering with thyroid function, reproductive hormones, and the signalling systems that regulate appetite, mood, and metabolism. The body's hormonal intelligence is a precision system. Flooding it daily with industrial chemicals produces the diffuse, hard-to-diagnose symptoms that millions of people now carry as their normal: weight that won't shift, moods that won't stabilise, energy that won't return.

07 / 09

The Stakes Are Higher for Children

A child's gut microbiome, hormonal system, brain, and immune system are all in active development. Feeding them ultra-processed food during this window does not just affect today's health — it shapes the biological foundation they will carry for life.

The gut microbiome is largely established in the first three years of life. The foods that dominate those years determine which microbial communities take up long-term residence — and those communities influence immunity, behaviour, mood, and metabolism well into adulthood. A child raised on ultra-processed food develops a microbiome dominated by the organisms that thrive on it — and those organisms make it harder to enjoy or crave natural food later.

Children are also more vulnerable to chemical exposure than adults — their livers are less developed, their blood-brain barriers more permeable, and their bodies are building rapidly from whatever materials are available. Artificial colours and preservatives that produce modest effects in adults produce more pronounced effects in children's developing nervous systems. The food industry knows this — which is why it targets children so deliberately in its marketing.

08 / 09

Hunger vs. Craving

Real hunger can be satisfied by any real food. A manufactured craving wants one thing specifically — and it does not go away when you are full. Learning to tell the difference is one of the most liberating nutritional skills there is.

True nutritional hunger develops gradually, feels like an absence, and can be satisfied by a wide variety of foods — including simple ones. An apple, some nuts, a piece of fruit. When you are genuinely hungry, these satisfy. A craving for junk food is specific, urgent, and frequently present even when the body is full of calories. It is not the body asking for nourishment — it is the trained response of a dopamine system and a microbiome shaped by the very food being craved.

As the diet transitions toward whole, alkaline, natural food, the cravings for junk food diminish — often dramatically — within two to four weeks. The microbiome shifts. The dopamine system recalibrates. The taste for natural food returns — or in many cases develops for the first time. People who have eaten naturally for months frequently find that junk food no longer tastes like food at all. The body, given real nourishment, stops asking for the imitation.

Practice this week

The next time a food craving arrives, before acting on it, sit with it for two minutes and ask: is this a genuine hunger signal from the body, or is this a learned craving triggered by an external cue, a time of day, an emotion, or habit? You do not have to override the craving to do this practice. You only have to name what it is before you respond to it.

Monthly: check the seed oils in your kitchen. What oils are you currently cooking with? Replace one bottle of a refined seed oil with cold-pressed extra-virgin olive oil or coconut oil this month. This single swap, over time, reduces a significant source of chronic inflammatory load without requiring an overhaul of everything else.
