Page 01 / 06 · The Myth

You do not use 10% of your brain. You use all of it — but not all of yourself.

The "10% myth" is scientifically false but psychologically true. You are not limited by how much of your brain you use. You are limited by how much of your mind you can access.

The claim that humans use only 10% of their brains is one of the most persistent myths in popular culture. Neuroscience has thoroughly debunked it — brain imaging shows that virtually all brain regions are active across a normal day, and damage to any area produces measurable deficits. There is no dormant 90% waiting to be unlocked. The myth, in its literal form, is simply false.

And yet the intuition behind the myth points at something real. There is a widespread, persistent, cross-cultural sense that something in human beings operates below or beyond ordinary consciousness — that the surface of what we call "thinking" is not the whole story. This sense is not scientific illiteracy. It is the accurate perception of a genuine phenomenon: that the ordinary waking mind is a narrow band within a much larger spectrum of human awareness.

What Is Actually Untapped

What is untapped is not a hidden lobe of the brain. What is untapped is the depth of the mind itself — the subconscious processes that run constantly below awareness, the intuitive intelligence that operates faster than reason, the creative capacity that surfaces in dreams and flow states and moments of unexpected insight, and the deeper layers of consciousness that contemplative traditions have mapped for thousands of years. This is the uncharted territory. Not a percentage — a depth.

The mind is not 10% used and 90% empty. It is 100% active and largely inaccessible to the conscious self. The work is to change that ratio.
Scenario · Where does this path lead?

You are in a meeting where someone confidently states that humans only use 10% of their brain and that the rest is untapped potential.

The room nods. The claim feels encouraging. And it is wrong — but the underlying intuition it points to is not entirely wrong.

Page 02 / 06 · The Surface

Ordinary thinking is a small fraction of what is happening in your mind.

The conscious mind processes roughly 40–50 bits of information per second. Your nervous system processes approximately 11 million bits per second. The gap between those two numbers is the territory this course explores.

Every moment of your waking life, your brain is processing an enormous quantity of sensory, emotional, and cognitive information. The vast majority of this processing never reaches consciousness. It happens in parallel, automatically, continuously — regulating your body, scanning for threat and opportunity, integrating past experience with present circumstance, generating the emotional tone that colours your interpretation of everything.

What reaches conscious awareness is a curated summary — a small fraction of the total processing, selected and compressed for the limited bandwidth of the conscious mind. The thoughts you are aware of having are not the full story of what your mind is doing. They are the headline of a much longer article.

The Iceberg Metaphor

Freud's iceberg metaphor — conscious mind as the tip above water, unconscious as the vast mass below — has been criticised on various grounds but remains useful as a structural image. Modern neuroscience supports a version of it: the proportion of mental processing that reaches conscious awareness is genuinely small relative to the whole. Learning to access more of what is below the surface is the central project of this course.

Your conscious mind is the part of the orchestra you can hear from the audience. Below the stage, an enormous ensemble is playing — and it shapes everything you think you are deciding freely.
Scenario · Where does this path lead?

You make a decision that feels completely rational and conscious — the result of weighing evidence and arriving at a considered conclusion.

Later you learn that much of the decision was made by subconscious processes before your conscious mind was involved, and the reasoning was constructed after the fact.

Page 03 / 06 · The Access

The deeper mind is not locked. It is unaddressed.

You have not failed to unlock the untapped mind. You have simply not yet learned the language it speaks — or turned your attention in its direction with enough consistency to establish communication.

The subconscious and deeper layers of mind are not locked behind a door that requires a special key. They are communicating constantly — through dreams, through body sensations, through the sudden insight that arrives in the shower, through the quality of feeling that tells you something is right or wrong before you know why. The problem is not access — it is attention. Most people have learned to direct their attention primarily at the surface.

The education system is oriented toward conscious, linear, verbal reasoning. The professional world rewards the kind of thinking that can be articulated, documented, and defended. This is not wrong — it is genuinely useful. But it trains a very narrow bandwidth of mental activity while leaving the rest largely uncultivated. The deeper capacities are not absent. They are simply not practiced.

Learning the Language

The deeper mind communicates in a different language than conscious thought. Its vocabulary includes imagery, metaphor, sensation, emotion, dream logic, synchronicity, and the pre-verbal knowing that precedes articulation. Learning to access more of your mind means learning to be fluent in this language — to take the non-linear communications seriously as information rather than dismissing them as noise.

The part of you that already knows the answer is speaking. You have simply been trained to listen to a different channel.
Scenario · Where does this path lead?

You have a strong intuitive sense about a situation — a feeling of rightness or wrongness that you cannot fully articulate or defend with evidence.

The conscious mind dismisses it as irrational. But the feeling is persistent and specific in a way that vague emotion usually is not.

Page 04 / 06 · The Blocks

What prevents access is not lack of ability — it is what you have been taught to dismiss.

The capacity for deeper mental access is not rare or special. It is the default human endowment. What is variable is the degree to which it has been trained, respected, and used.

In most educational and professional environments, certain forms of knowing are valued and others are dismissed. Logical analysis, evidence-based reasoning, and verbal articulation are rewarded. Intuitive knowing, somatic intelligence, creative leaps, and non-linear insight are treated as unreliable, soft, or irrelevant. People learn to discount these forms of intelligence even when they are producing accurate and useful information.

The result is not the absence of these capacities but their underdevelopment — and a trained habit of dismissing their outputs as noise. The block to accessing your deeper mind is not a lack of capability. It is a conditioned habit of directing attention away from the forms of knowing that deeper access requires.

What Gets Dismissed

What typically gets dismissed: the feeling that something is not right even when the evidence says it should be fine. The creative solution that arrived without a logical process. The dream that contained an accurate picture of an unresolved situation. The sense of knowing something about another person that no one told you. The persistent image or phrase that returns until you pay attention to it. These are not superstition or wishful thinking. They are the communications of a deeper intelligence that most people have learned to ignore.

You were not born without access to the deeper mind. You were trained out of listening to it. The training can be reversed.
Scenario · Where does this path lead?

A creative solution to a problem arrives fully formed — not through analysis, but suddenly, when you were doing something unrelated.

The solution is good. Better, in some ways, than what hours of deliberate analysis produced.

Page 05 / 06 · The Invitation

This course is an expedition into unmapped territory.

What follows is not a set of techniques for hacking your brain. It is an invitation to develop a working relationship with the full depth of your own mind — including the parts that do not speak in language you already know.

The mind you are about to explore is not someone else's territory. It is yours — it has been yours since before you were born. You are not gaining access to something external. You are re-establishing contact with something internal that has been there all along, communicating in ways you have not always been equipped to receive.

What this course can offer is a set of maps and a set of practices. The maps describe the terrain — the layers of mind, the nature of belief, the architecture of fear, the mechanisms by which consciousness can be deliberately expanded. The practices give you ways to enter the terrain yourself. But the territory is yours, and the expedition is conducted in your own experience, not in theory.

The Three Principles

Everything that follows in this course rests on three principles. First: the mind is deeper than the thoughts you are aware of having. Second: access to that depth is a learnable skill, not a fixed endowment. Third: what you find there does not contradict who you are — it completes it.

You are not trying to become something other than yourself. You are trying to become all of yourself. That is a more demanding project and a more rewarding one.
Scenario · Where does this path lead?

You are about to begin a genuine inquiry into the deeper layers of your own mind — not as an intellectual project but as an experiential one.

This means moving beyond what you can currently articulate, beyond the known territory of your conscious self-understanding.
