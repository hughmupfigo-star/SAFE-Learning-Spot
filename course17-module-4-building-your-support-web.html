Page 01 / 04 · One Trusted Adult

You only need one to start.

If you need help right now

Call or text 988 (Suicide & Crisis Lifeline) · text HOME to 741741 (Crisis Text Line) · call 911 if you’re in immediate danger. Free, confidential, 24/7.

A support web doesn't need to be big. Research on young people who get through hard times keeps finding the same thing: one reliable, caring adult makes an enormous difference. You're looking for one, not ten.

With school counselors stretched thin, your trusted adult might be someone else: a teacher you click with, a coach, an aunt or older cousin, a friend's parent, a faith or community leader, a school nurse, a librarian. The qualities that matter: they listen without freaking out, they take you seriously, and they keep your trust. You probably already have someone in mind — that flicker of ‘maybe them’ is worth following.

You don't have to hand this person everything. Starting can be as small as ‘Hey, can I talk to you about something that's been weighing on me?’ A good trusted adult doesn't need you to have it figured out or said perfectly. They just need you to start, and they'll meet you there.

If the first person isn't right

Sometimes the first adult you try doesn't respond well — they get awkward, minimise it, or make it about them. That's disappointing, and it is not a verdict on you or proof that no one will help. It just means they weren't the right one. Try another. The right person is out there, and finding them is worth a couple of tries.

You don't need a village to start. You need one person who listens and takes you seriously. Just one.

Page 02 / 04 · Asking For Help

The words to actually say.

A huge barrier to getting help is simply not knowing how to start the sentence. So here are real, borrowable words — because ‘I don't know how to bring it up’ keeps too many people stuck and silent.

Keep it simple and true. To a trusted adult: ‘Can I talk to you about something? I've been having a really hard time and I don't totally know what to do.’ To a parent: ‘I think I need some help with how I've been feeling — can we talk?’ To a doctor: ‘I've been feeling down/anxious a lot and I want help with it.’ You don't have to explain everything or justify it. Naming that you're struggling and want support is a complete, valid request.

It's normal for your voice to shake or for it to come out clumsy. The words don't have to be smooth to work. Most people, once you start, are relieved you trusted them. And if writing is easier than speaking, a text or a note absolutely counts — ‘I've been struggling and didn't know how to say it out loud’ is a brave, real way in.

Naming what you want

It helps to say what you're hoping for, even roughly: ‘I just need someone to listen,’ or ‘I think I might need to see someone,’ or ‘I don't know what I need, I just know I can't keep doing this alone.’ Any of those gives the other person a way to help. ‘I don't know what I need’ is itself a perfectly good thing to say — you don't have to arrive with the answer.

‘I've been struggling and I need some help’ is a complete sentence. You don't owe anyone a polished explanation to deserve support.

Page 03 / 04 · Free & Low-Cost Care

Real care, even without school or money.

Professional help in the US can feel like it's only for people with great insurance and lots of money. It isn't. There's a real network of free and low-cost options — you just have to know the doors.

Start here: SAMHSA's National Helpline, 1-800-662-4357 — free, confidential, 24/7, and it connects you to local treatment including sliding-scale and free options. findtreatment.gov lets you search by location and filter for low-cost care. Community Mental Health Centers and Federally Qualified Health Centers (FQHCs) exist across the country and charge on a sliding scale based on what you can pay — sometimes nothing. Many therapists also offer reduced ‘sliding scale’ fees if you ask.

More doors: if you're in college, your campus counseling center is usually free and a great first stop. Many areas have telehealth therapy that's cheaper and easier to reach. NAMI (1-800-950-6264) offers free support and can point you to local groups. And your school counselor or school psychologist, even stretched, can often still help you find services — it's worth asking what's available.

Money should never be the wall

If cost ever feels like the thing stopping you, say so out loud to whoever you reach — ‘I need help but I can't pay much.’ It's a normal thing to say, and the people staffing these lines deal with it every day; pointing you to affordable care is literally their job. The system is patchy and imperfect, but free and low-cost help genuinely exists, and you're allowed to use it.

‘I can't afford it’ is a sentence to say out loud to a helpline — not a reason to go without care. Sliding-scale and free options are real.
