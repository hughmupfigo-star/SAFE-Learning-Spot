Electric Food

The healing tradition this course is built upon made a distinction that modern science is slowly arriving at: there is food that the body recognises as alive, and food that the body does not. The first feeds you. The second burdens you.

Natural, original foods — fruits, vegetables, seeds, roots, and herbs that grow as nature designed them — carry minerals, enzymes, and living compounds in a form the body was built to receive. These are sometimes called electric foods: foods that carry genuine life force and transfer it to those who eat them.

Hybrid, processed, and chemically altered foods are something different. They may look like food. They may taste like food. But the body's capacity to receive full nourishment from them is compromised — because they were not made by the intelligence that made the body. The further a food drifts from its natural form, the further it drifts from being able to truly feed you.

02 / 09

The Question of Hybrid Foods

Not all food that appears natural is original. Hybridisation — the crossing of plant varieties to produce new traits — has created many of the staple foods we now consider normal. But normal is not the same as natural.

Seedless grapes, seedless watermelons, most varieties of commercial corn, most wheat, and many popular fruits are hybrids that do not exist in nature without human intervention. They cannot reproduce on their own — the absence of seeds is itself a sign of manipulation. Hybrid foods are often higher in sugar and starch than their natural ancestors, and lower in the mineral content that makes a food genuinely nourishing.

The healing tradition taught that hybrid foods — because they exist outside nature's original design — tend to be acid-forming in the body, contributing to the acidic internal environment that is associated with disease. Returning to original, non-hybrid foods is part of returning to alignment with what the body was designed to receive.

03 / 09

Reading the Ingredient List

The ingredient list on a food package is a map of how far that food has travelled from nature. Learning to read it is one of the most practical skills in nutritional literacy.

A real food has no ingredient list, or a very short one. An apple is an apple. A handful of walnuts is walnuts. When a food requires a list of thirty ingredients to describe what it is, it is no longer a food in the original sense — it is a product designed in a laboratory to replicate the experience of eating while delivering very little of the nourishment.

The simple rule from the healing tradition: if you cannot imagine it growing from the earth, or if your great-grandmother would not have recognised it as food, approach it with caution. Ingredients you cannot pronounce, numbers in place of names, and substances that exist only in chemistry laboratories are signs that what you are holding is man-made — assembled, not grown.

04 / 09

What Refining Removes

Refining takes something whole and strips it down. What is left may look clean, may taste mild, may last longer on a shelf — but the most important parts have been removed.

White flour begins as a whole grain containing the bran — rich in fibre and B vitamins — and the germ — rich in minerals, healthy fats, and vitamin E. Refining removes both, leaving only the starchy endosperm. The result is a product that raises blood sugar rapidly, provides almost no nutrients, and has had its living intelligence extracted. Manufacturers sometimes add synthetic vitamins back, but a supplement pressed into depleted flour is not the same as the whole food.

The same process applies to white rice, refined sugar cane, and white salt. Nature packages nutrients together for a reason — they work synergistically, each supporting the absorption and function of the others. Refining breaks that package apart and discards the parts industry finds inconvenient.

05 / 09

Whole Food vs. Isolated Nutrients

Nature does not make isolated nutrients. It makes whole foods — complex, interconnected systems of compounds that work together in ways that no laboratory has yet fully mapped.

The vitamin C in an orange does not arrive alone. It comes embedded in a matrix of bioflavonoids, enzymes, and co-factors that govern how it is absorbed and used. An ascorbic acid tablet contains one isolated compound, synthesised in a factory. The body recognises and uses the two differently.

This principle — called food synergy — is why the healing tradition has always pointed toward whole food rather than supplements. A mango does not just contain beta-carotene; it contains hundreds of compounds that interact with each other and with the body in ways that a capsule cannot replicate. Eat the mango. Eat the herb. Eat the food. The intelligence is in the whole, not in the isolated part.

06 / 09

What Artificial Additives Do

Preservatives, artificial colours, synthetic flavour enhancers, emulsifiers — these chemicals exist in food products because they serve the manufacturer, not the person eating them. The body was not designed to process them.

Artificial additives extend shelf life, intensify flavour, improve appearance, and reduce production costs. They are extraordinarily profitable. What they are not is food. When the body encounters substances it does not recognise as food, it mounts a response — often inflammatory — to manage their presence. Over time, chronic exposure to multiple artificial compounds creates a cumulative burden that manifests as gut disruption, skin conditions, hormonal imbalance, and systemic inflammation.

Children are particularly vulnerable. Their gut microbiomes are still developing, their livers are less equipped to process chemical loads, and their bodies are building at a rapid pace — from whatever materials, natural or synthetic, are available. Removing artificial additives from a child's diet often produces visible improvements in behaviour, focus, and immunity within weeks.

07 / 09

The Seed and the Soil

A natural food is only as good as the earth it grew in. Decades of industrial farming — heavy pesticide use, monoculture, synthetic fertilisers — have depleted the mineral content of agricultural soil. Even vegetables can now be nutritionally hollow compared to what they once were.

Studies comparing the mineral content of vegetables grown fifty years ago to those grown today show significant declines in iron, magnesium, calcium, and zinc. The plants grow — industrial fertilisers ensure that — but they grow large and fast, without the depth of mineral absorption that comes from rich, living soil. A tomato that grew in a week in nutrient-depleted soil is not the same food as a tomato that grew in a month in mineral-rich earth.

Organic growing, natural composting, and traditional farming methods maintain living soil. Food grown this way retains more of what the body needs. Where possible, choosing organically grown, naturally cultivated food — and supporting those who grow it — is an extension of the same principle: stay as close to what nature made as possible.

08 / 09

Recognising Real Food

The simplest test does not require a nutrition degree. It requires asking one question: did this exist before humans had factories?

A mango, a fig, a bunch of kale, a coconut, a handful of hemp seeds — these are real food. They grow. They contain seeds. They decay. Their ingredient list is themselves. The body has been receiving these foods for as long as humans have existed, and it knows precisely what to do with them.

A product in a box with a shelf life of two years, thirty ingredients, and a flavour that could not exist in nature is man-made. It has been engineered to taste irresistible and to bypass the body's satiety signals. It serves the company that makes it. It does not serve the body that eats it. The more you move your plate toward the first category and away from the second, the more your body begins to function as it was designed to. That shift does not require perfection. It requires direction.

  5. 05Soil health determines food quality. A vegetable grown in depleted soil is nutritionally different from the same vegetable grown in living, mineralised earth — regardless of how it looks.
  6. 06The simplest framework: closer to nature, closer to health. The question is not whether a food is organic or expensive — it is how many steps it has been through between the earth and your plate.
Practice this week

Read the ingredient list on five packaged foods you currently eat regularly. For each one, ask: would I recognise every item on this list as food? If an ingredient requires a chemistry background to identify, note it. You are not committing to change anything yet — you are learning to see what is actually there.

Monthly: identify one processed food you eat regularly and find its whole-food equivalent. Spend one month swapping them. Note any differences in how you feel — in energy, digestion, or satiety — in the two weeks after the swap. Not to prove a point, but to gather your own data.
