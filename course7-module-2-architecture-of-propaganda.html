Module 02 · Slide 01

What Propaganda Actually Is

The word propaganda conjures specific images: wartime posters, Soviet state media, totalitarian rallies. This is the first and most effective piece of propaganda about propaganda — the idea that it is something other people do, in other places, under obviously illegitimate regimes. Once you believe that, you stop looking for it where it actually operates most effectively: in ordinary life, in democratic societies, in the media you trust, in the assumptions you inherited.

Propaganda is not defined by its source or its political affiliation. It is defined by its method: the systematic effort to shape belief and behaviour through means that bypass rational evaluation — through repetition, emotion, tribal loyalty, and the management of what feels thinkable. By this definition, propaganda is not the exception in modern information environments. It is close to the rule.

Central observation
The most effective propaganda is the kind you are certain you are immune to — because that certainty is exactly what the propaganda produced.
Propaganda is not primarily about lying
A common misconception is that propaganda works by inserting false information. In practice, the most durable propaganda is built substantially from true facts — selectively chosen, strategically arranged, and emotionally framed to produce a predetermined conclusion. The facts are accurate. The picture they create is engineered. This makes propaganda far more robust than simple lying: the facts can be checked and confirmed, which makes the overall framing feel verified even though the framing is doing most of the work.
Propaganda requires no conspiracy
Propaganda does not require a room full of people consciously deceiving the public. It emerges from the accumulated effect of many individual decisions made by people operating within shared frameworks — editors who know what their readers want, journalists who have internalised what counts as a credible source, advertisers who fund coverage they find sympathetic. No individual decision is a lie. The system produces distortion without anyone in it having to intend deception. Understanding this prevents the error of thinking that if there is no conspiracy, there is no propaganda.
The propaganda of normalcy
Some of the most powerful propaganda is not produced by any actor trying to shape opinion — it is produced by the accumulated effect of what is treated as normal, unremarkable, and not requiring explanation. The presence of military recruitment advertisements in entertainment, the routine coverage of share prices as public interest information, the framing of GDP growth as a measure of national wellbeing — these are not obviously propaganda. They are so thoroughly normalised that calling them so seems eccentric. That normalcy is itself a propagandistic achievement.

Module 02 · Slide 02

Repetition: Truth by Volume

The relationship between repetition and perceived truth is one of the most thoroughly documented findings in cognitive psychology. Statements that are repeated become more familiar. Familiarity is processed as a signal of truth. The result — called the illusory truth effect — means that a claim heard many times is rated as more likely to be true than a claim heard for the first time, even when people are explicitly told that the familiar claim might be false, and even when they already know the claim is incorrect.

This is not a vulnerability of poorly educated or credulous people. It operates across educational levels, across political affiliations, and across degrees of media literacy. Repetition affects belief at a level below conscious evaluation. It is the most reliable, most scalable, and most thoroughly exploited mechanic in the propaganda toolkit.

Central observation
You do not need to convince someone a claim is true. You need to ensure they encounter it often enough that it begins to feel true — and then the mind does the rest, generating explanations and evidence that feel like independent confirmation.
The political slogan as repetition technology
Political slogans are not primarily arguments. They are repetition technologies: short, memorable phrases designed to be heard and repeated until they achieve the familiarity that registers as truth. "Take back control." "Make America great again." "Strong and stable." These phrases do not make claims that can be evaluated — they produce associations and feelings through repetition. The content is less important than the volume. The volume is what builds the felt sense that this is obviously, self-evidently true.
The media echo as amplification
When a claim is repeated across multiple sources — different newspapers, different broadcasters, different online platforms — the repetition across sources produces an additional effect: it feels like independent confirmation. Ten outlets reporting the same thing feels like ten independent verifications. If those outlets share the same source, the same wire service, or the same fundamental framing assumptions, the ten accounts are not independent. But they are experienced as ten separate data points converging on the same conclusion. The apparent consensus is manufactured by repetition, not produced by independent investigation.
Repetition and the direction of attention
Repetition does not only make specific claims feel true — it determines what counts as a problem worth thinking about. The topics that are covered repeatedly become the issues that matter; the topics that receive little coverage cease to feel urgent regardless of their actual significance. This agenda-setting function of repetition is arguably more powerful than any specific claim: it shapes the landscape of what is real, what is concerning, and what deserves public attention — without ever making a single falsifiable assertion.
I noticed I believed something about a political figure I had never actually looked into. When I tried to find where the belief came from, I couldn't find a source — just a vague sense that everyone knew this about them. That sense was the product of thousands of repetitions I couldn't locate individually.

Module 02 · Slide 03

Tribal Identity: The Loyalty Mechanic

Human beings are profoundly social animals. The need to belong — to be part of a group, to be recognised as a member in good standing, to share identity with others — is not a weakness or an irrationality. It is one of the deepest drivers of human behaviour. Propaganda does not exploit this need by accident. It is specifically engineered to activate it.

Once a narrative is successfully tied to group identity, it becomes almost impervious to factual challenge. Pointing out that the narrative is inaccurate does not produce a revision of the narrative — it produces a defence of the group. The person is not defending a claim. They are defending who they are and where they belong.

Central observation
When belonging to a group becomes contingent on holding that group's narrative, changing your mind stops being an intellectual act and becomes a social one — an act of leaving rather than updating.
Identity capture
The process by which a narrative becomes identity — rather than just a held belief — is gradual and usually invisible. It begins with shared language and symbols, moves through shared enemies and shared victories, and completes when the narrative is so fused with self-concept that questioning it feels like self-erasure. At this stage, the narrative requires no external enforcement. The person enforces it themselves — through internal guilt when doubt arises, through social policing of those in the group who waver, and through increasing hostility to outside perspectives.
Belief as loyalty signal
Within any identity group, expressing the group's narrative functions as a loyalty signal — proof of membership, of commitment, of trustworthiness. This creates pressure to express the narrative regardless of private doubts, which in turn creates the appearance of consensus, which makes the narrative feel even more obviously true. The group produces what looks like collective conviction, but is substantially collective performance of conviction. The performance eventually shapes the belief, because humans are not reliably able to distinguish between what they say they believe and what they actually believe.
The cost of group departure
Leaving a group narrative — changing your mind about something the group holds as definitional — typically means losing belonging, relationships, and identity. This cost is not abstract. For many people it means family conflict, friendship loss, professional consequences, and the disorienting experience of having no community. The propaganda of group identity is so effective precisely because the cost of leaving it is real and significant, not imaginary. The narrative is enforced not by force but by the genuine pain of exclusion.

Module 02 · Slide 04

Enemy Creation

Every propaganda system requires an enemy. The enemy performs several essential functions simultaneously: it unifies the in-group around a shared threat; it explains all failures and frustrations (the enemy's fault); it directs anger outward rather than inward; and it makes critique of the in-group feel like collaboration with the enemy. Without an enemy, the propaganda architecture loses most of its emotional force.

The enemy does not need to be fabricated whole cloth. In most effective propaganda, the enemy is real — a real group, a real government, a real set of interests. What is fabricated is not their existence but their total explanatory power: the claim that they are the primary or sufficient cause of the problems attributed to them, and that defeating or excluding them would resolve those problems.

Central observation
The enemy is not a description. It is a function. Understanding the function tells you more about the propaganda system than understanding the enemy's actual characteristics.
The enemy as explanation
One of the enemy's primary narrative functions is to explain problems that might otherwise require structural analysis. Economic insecurity, cultural change, political dysfunction — these are complex phenomena with multiple interacting causes. The propaganda enemy collapses this complexity: one group becomes the explanation for everything, and removing or suppressing that group becomes the solution to everything. This is enormously appealing because it is simple, emotionally satisfying, and requires no uncomfortable examination of systems in which the audience is also implicated.
The enemy as in-group glue
A group with a clear, vivid enemy is more cohesive, more motivated, and easier to mobilise than a group without one. This is why propaganda systems produce enemies even when no external threat exists — and why they maintain enemy narratives even when those enemies have been defeated or diminished. The enemy is not primarily about the enemy. It is about the in-group — about maintaining the emotional intensity and collective identity that the enemy narrative generates. When the old enemy disappears, a new one is located.
Internal dissent as enemy collaboration
The most important function of the enemy narrative for propaganda purposes is what it does to internal dissent. Once an enemy has been established as an existential threat, anyone who questions the in-group's narrative about that enemy can be framed as a naive dupe, a fellow traveller, or an active collaborator. This closes the feedback loop that allows groups to update their beliefs. Critique from outside is dismissed because it comes from the enemy. Critique from inside becomes evidence of infiltration or betrayal. The enemy narrative makes the propaganda system self-sealing.
The question that cuts through
When a group presents a single enemy as the explanation for a complex set of problems, ask: what would need to be true for removing this enemy to actually solve the problems attributed to them? What structural factors would remain? Whose interests are served by this explanation? The answers to these questions usually reveal what the enemy narrative is designed to make invisible.

Module 02 · Slide 05

The Big Lie

In Mein Kampf, Adolf Hitler described a technique he called the "big lie" — the observation that people are more likely to believe a massive falsehood than a small one, because they cannot imagine anyone having the audacity to so completely distort the truth. The small lie is checked against experience and found wanting. The big lie is so far outside the normal range of human dishonesty that it short-circuits the evaluation process.

The big lie has evolved since Hitler described it. Contemporary versions rely less on a single massive falsehood and more on a strategy of confident, total assertion — claims delivered with such certainty, at such scale, and with such frequency that the demand for evidence begins to feel like the unusual position. In a high-volume information environment, the firehose of confident falsehood can overwhelm the fact-checker simply through volume.

Central observation
The big lie does not succeed by being believed. It succeeds by making truth feel uncertain — by producing enough noise that distinguishing signal from interference becomes exhausting, and exhausted people default to the simplest available story.
Confidence as credibility substitute
In normal epistemic environments, confidence and evidence travel together: people are most confident about things they have good reason to believe. Propaganda disrupts this by producing confidence without evidence — and relying on the fact that most people, most of the time, use confidence as a proxy for credibility. The politician or commentator who speaks with absolute certainty about complex matters is not displaying knowledge. They are displaying propaganda technique. The certainty is the product, not the result of the evidence.
The firehose of falsehood
The modern evolution of the big lie is the strategy of producing so many claims — some false, some misleading, some true — at such volume and speed that comprehensive fact-checking becomes impossible. The goal is not to be believed on each individual claim. It is to produce a general state of epistemic confusion in which nothing feels definitively verifiable, and the audience retreats to trusting whoever feels most familiar or most aligned with their existing views. Confusion benefits those who produce it, not those who consume it.
The partial truth as delivery vehicle
The most effective big lies are carried by partial truths. A claim that is ten percent accurate can survive fact-checking of that ten percent while the ninety percent of misleading framing goes unchallenged. When a fact-checker confirms the accurate element, the confirmation bleeds across to the surrounding falsehood. The accurate detail is not incidental — it is structural. It is what allows the lie to travel through environments that would otherwise filter it out.

Module 02 · Slide 06

Propaganda in Democracy

After the First World War, the success of propaganda in mobilising mass populations produced a crisis for democratic theorists. If ordinary people could be moved so reliably by propaganda, could democracy — which required an informed citizenry capable of genuine deliberation — actually function? The response, developed most explicitly by figures like Edward Bernays, was not to limit propaganda but to professionalise it: to develop the techniques of what Bernays called "the engineering of consent" and place them in the service of democratic institutions and commercial interests.

Bernays was Sigmund Freud's nephew. He took his uncle's insight about the irrational foundations of human behaviour and applied it to the problem of managing public opinion at scale. The result was modern public relations — an industry that now shapes almost every significant communication between institutions and the public, and which operates using every mechanic examined in this module.

Central observation
The difference between propaganda and public relations is primarily one of legitimacy, not of method. Both use repetition, emotion, identity, and the management of what feels thinkable. One is done by governments we disapprove of. The other is done by organisations we are accustomed to trusting.
Advertising as continuous propaganda
Modern advertising is propaganda in the precise technical sense: it uses repetition, emotional association, tribal identity, and aspirational narrative to shape belief and behaviour through means that bypass rational evaluation. The claim that a particular car conveys success, or that a particular drink is associated with authentic freedom, or that a particular cosmetic product addresses a need the advertisement itself creates — these are not informational claims subject to fact-checking. They are narrative injections that produce associations without making propositions.
Think tanks and manufactured expertise
The think tank model — organisations that produce research and commentary aligned with their funders' interests while presenting themselves as independent experts — is a sophisticated propaganda infrastructure operating within democratic legitimacy norms. The research they produce can be technically accurate. The questions they choose to study, the frameworks they apply, the conclusions they emphasise, and the media relationships that amplify their work are all shaped by the interests of whoever funds them. The appearance of independent expertise is the product being sold.
Political messaging as emotional engineering
Modern political campaigns are built on psychological profiling, emotional trigger mapping, and message testing at scale. The message delivered to each voter is increasingly calibrated to their specific psychological profile — their particular fears, aspirations, tribal identities, and emotional vulnerabilities. This is not persuasion in the sense of argument and evidence. It is emotional manipulation conducted with the sophistication of large budgets, extensive data, and teams of psychologists. It operates within democratic norms while being structurally incompatible with the deliberative ideal those norms are supposed to serve.
Scenario · Slide 06
A public relations firm is hired to improve public perception of a mining company's environmental record. They commission independent-seeming research showing the company's practices compare favourably to industry averages, place sympathetic stories in local media, and fund community events. No individual claim is false. Is this propaganda?
Module 02 · Slide 07

These Mechanics Work on Everyone

One of the most consistent findings in the psychology of propaganda and persuasion is that believing yourself to be immune significantly increases your susceptibility. The person who is certain they can spot manipulation is less vigilant, not more — because their confidence has replaced the ongoing attention that actual immunity requires. This applies regardless of education, intelligence, political affiliation, or media literacy training.

This module exists not to produce certainty that you can now identify propaganda, but to make the mechanics visible enough that you can notice when they are running — including when they are running on you, in narratives you find entirely convincing, from sources you trust completely.

Central observation
The mechanics of propaganda do not distinguish between people who know about them and people who do not. Knowing the name of a technique is not protection against it. Ongoing noticing is.
The third-person effect
Research consistently shows that people believe media influence affects others more than it affects themselves — a phenomenon called the third-person effect. People who score highest on this belief — most certain that they are unaffected — tend to show the strongest signs of influence in their subsequent behaviour. The belief in personal immunity is itself a propaganda product: it has been cultivated, because people who believe they are not being influenced stop scrutinising the influence they are receiving.
The propaganda you agree with
The propaganda mechanics examined in this module are most visible in narratives you distrust and least visible in narratives you find convincing. This is not because the mechanics are absent from the convincing narratives. It is because alignment between your existing views and the narrative's conclusions makes the mechanics invisible — the repetition feels like confirmation, the emotional resonance feels like recognition of truth, the tribal identity feels like genuine community. The most important application of these mechanics is to the content you find most obviously, naturally true.
Immunity as practice, not achievement
The goal of this course is not to produce people who have successfully identified all propaganda and can now consume media without being shaped by it. That is not a reachable state, and believing you have reached it is the most dangerous position available. The goal is to develop a practice: a set of questions applied consistently, a habit of noticing the mechanics as they operate, and enough humility about your own susceptibility to keep the practice active rather than replacing it with confidence that you no longer need it.

Module 02 · Slide 08

Recognising the Mechanics in Real Time

Knowing the names of propaganda mechanics and being able to spot them as they operate are different capacities. This slide is about the second: the specific signals that indicate each mechanic is running, in the moment, in ordinary media consumption and conversation.

These signals are not proof of propaganda — they are reasons to slow down and look more carefully. The mechanics can operate in both accurate and inaccurate content. Their presence does not determine truth or falsehood. It indicates that something other than evidence is doing some of the work of conviction.

Central observation
The signal that a propaganda mechanic is running is almost never the content of what is being said. It is the quality of the experience of receiving it — the speed, the certainty, the emotional intensity, the social weight that arrives attached to the claim.
Repetition signal: familiarity without source
When something feels obviously true and you cannot locate where you first encountered it, repetition has likely been the mechanism. The test: can you trace this belief to a specific source, a specific argument, a specific piece of evidence? If not — if it simply feels like something everyone knows — ask when you first formed this view and what it was based on. The inability to locate a source for a strongly held belief is one of the clearest signals that repetition, not evidence, produced the conviction.
Tribal identity signal: critique feels like betrayal
When engaging with a challenge to a belief makes you feel disloyal rather than curious — when the emotion that arrives is anxiety about the group rather than interest in the question — tribal identity capture is running. The test: can you engage with the strongest version of the opposing argument without it feeling like a threat to your sense of belonging? If not, the belief is doing social work as well as intellectual work, and the social work is likely doing most of the heavy lifting.
Enemy narrative signal: one explanation for everything
When a single group or force is consistently offered as the explanation for a wide range of otherwise unrelated problems — economic, cultural, security, social — the enemy narrative mechanic is likely running. The test: what would need to be true about this group for them to be the primary cause of all the problems attributed to them? What structural factors would remain if they were removed? If the enemy's removal would clearly not solve the problems attributed to them, the enemy is functioning as a narrative device rather than an accurate analysis.

✓

Module 02 complete.

You have examined the five core mechanics of propaganda — what it actually is (not just government lying), how repetition produces false familiarity, how tribal identity makes narratives impervious to challenge, how enemy creation serves the in-group rather than describing reality, how the big lie overwhelms rather than convinces, and how these mechanics operate inside democratic societies and inside the content you trust most. And you have encountered the hardest truth: knowing all of this does not make you immune. Ongoing noticing is the only thing that does.

What you carry forward
  1. 01Propaganda is not state lying — it is any systematic use of communication to produce specific beliefs and behaviours by bypassing rational evaluation. It operates in democracies, in trusted media, and in content that feels like analysis.
  2. 02Repetition produces familiarity, and familiarity produces the sensation of truth. A claim encountered enough times begins to feel known rather than asserted — the mechanism requires no deception and no conspiracy to operate.
  3. 03Tribal identity makes narratives impervious to evidence. A claim that serves in-group identity will be believed not because it is true but because believing it is a membership requirement.
  4. 04Enemy creation does not describe a real threat — it manufactures internal cohesion by designating an out-group whose hostility justifies whatever the in-group needs to do.
  5. 05The big lie works by overwhelming. A claim of sufficient scale produces the sensation of significance and the reluctance to disbelieve something so consequential.
  6. 06Knowing the mechanics does not make you immune. You must notice them operating, in each specific case, while they are operating. The knowledge is the beginning; the noticing is the practice.
Practice this week

Identify one claim you believe with high confidence that also serves your in-group identity — a claim whose falseness would cost you standing in your community. Then ask: what would the evidence need to look like to genuinely change my mind about this? If you cannot answer the question concretely, that difficulty is the propaganda architecture operating from inside.

Monthly: track one ongoing narrative — political, cultural, or social — across thirty days. Note each repetition. Observe when familiarity begins to produce the sensation of confirmation rather than assessment. The tracking is the counter-mechanism: it makes the repetition visible rather than cumulative.
