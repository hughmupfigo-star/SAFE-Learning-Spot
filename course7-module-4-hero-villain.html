Module 04 · Slide 01

The Narrative Role System

Stories need characters with functions. The hero acts and is morally elevated. The villain threatens and is morally condemned. The victim suffers and generates sympathy. The rescuer arrives and is authorised. These are not descriptions of people — they are narrative slots, roles that stories assign to people in order to produce a coherent emotional and moral structure.

In fiction, we understand this. We know the villain in a film is a character constructed to serve narrative purposes, not a complete human being. In news, political messaging, and social media, the same role system operates — but we forget that it is operating. The politician, the immigrant, the whistleblower, the corporation, the activist are assigned roles before we have assessed them, and those roles determine what we believe about them before any evidence arrives.

Central observation
The role a person or group is assigned in a narrative is not a description of who they are. It is a decision about what function they will serve in the story — and that decision is made by whoever controls the narrative, in service of their interests.
Roles precede facts
In any established narrative, the role assignment often precedes the specific facts being reported. When a government has been cast as villain in the dominant narrative about a conflict, individual events are interpreted through that casting: their actions confirm villainy, their statements are treated as lies, their victims are foregrounded and their justifications are dismissed. The same events, occurring within a narrative where the government is cast as hero, would be interpreted entirely differently. The role determines what the facts mean — which is why changing the narrative role is often more powerful than changing the facts.
Roles collapse complexity
Real people and real institutions are irreducibly complex — they contain contradictory qualities, act from mixed motives, produce both harmful and beneficial outcomes, and change over time. Narrative roles cannot accommodate this complexity. The villain cannot have genuine grievances; the hero cannot be genuinely self-interested; the victim cannot have contributed to their situation. The role system flattens human complexity into functional simplicity, which is emotionally satisfying and analytically destructive.
Role assignment is contested
The most significant conflicts in public discourse are often not about facts — they are about role assignment. Who is the victim and who is the aggressor. Who is protecting freedom and who is threatening it. Who deserves sympathy and who deserves condemnation. These contests over roles are more fundamental than contests over facts because they determine how facts will be interpreted. Controlling the role assignment in a narrative is more powerful than controlling any individual piece of information within it.

Module 04 · Slide 02

The Hero Slot

Every narrative has a protagonist — a perspective that structures the account, whose interests define what the problem is, and whose wellbeing determines what counts as a good outcome. In news and political narrative, this protagonist slot is almost never made explicit. It does not need to be. It is built into whose suffering is rendered visible, whose account is taken seriously, whose experts are quoted, and whose frame determines the questions being asked.

Understanding who occupies the hero slot in any narrative tells you whose interests the narrative is designed to serve — because the hero's goals are what the story is moving toward. Everything else is context, obstacle, or threat to the hero's purpose.

Central observation
The hero slot is the most powerful position in any narrative — not because it is the most visible, but because it is the most invisible. We see the story through the hero's eyes without noticing that we have been given the hero's eyes to see with.
The default protagonist in economic news
In most mainstream economic journalism, the default protagonist is the investor, the market, or "the economy" as an abstraction. When markets fall, the news reports concern and anxiety; when they rise, relief and optimism. The suffering of workers during the same periods is reported as context, as data, as consequence — not as the primary story. The feelings attributed to the market are foregrounded; the feelings of the people the market affects are backgrounded. This is not neutral. It is a protagonist assignment that systematically centres one set of interests above all others.
The hero's goals as natural goals
Once a protagonist slot is established, the hero's objectives become the natural objectives of the story. If the hero is the government managing a crisis, the natural question is "how is the government managing?" If the hero is a community under threat, the natural question is "how is the community responding?" The objectives of whoever is not in the hero slot — the crisis's underlying causes, the government's own role in creating the threat — become secondary considerations, obstacles, or complications rather than the point. The hero's goals are the story's goals.
Humanisation and the hero slot
People in the hero slot are humanised: their internal lives, their feelings, their individual stories are presented. People outside the hero slot are often presented as categories — statistics, representatives of groups, functions rather than full people. This humanisation differential is one of the most powerful effects of the role system. It determines whose suffering generates empathy and whose generates policy debate. Whose deaths produce grief and whose produce concern. Whose children are individual children and whose are a social phenomenon.

Module 04 · Slide 03

The Villain Slot

The villain slot is defined by three narrative operations applied to a person, group, or institution: dehumanisation (removal of interior life, complexity, and legitimate grievance), total agency attribution (the villain is fully responsible for the problems assigned to them, unaffected by structural conditions), and context removal (the villain's actions are presented without the historical, political, or social conditions that produced them).

These operations are not descriptions of a genuinely evil actor. They are the narrative technology used to construct one — to produce a figure whose function in the story is to be hated, blamed, and defeated. A real person who does genuinely harmful things is not the same as a narrative villain. The difference matters because the narrative villain cannot be negotiated with, cannot have legitimate interests, and cannot be the subject of policies short of defeat. The construction of villains forecloses options as well as understanding.

Central observation
The villain slot is not a description of a person's character. It is a set of narrative operations that remove everything about a person that would complicate the story — their humanity, their context, their reasons, their legitimate interests. What remains is a function: the thing to be defeated.
Dehumanisation as narrative technology
Dehumanisation in narrative does not require overt slurs or explicit denial of humanity. It operates through omission: not showing the villain's interior life, not reporting on their families, their fears, their histories, their ordinary experiences. The hero's interior life is rendered; the villain's is not. This asymmetry is the technical mechanism of dehumanisation in journalism and political narrative — and it produces the same psychological effect as explicit dehumanisation, more efficiently and with greater deniability.
Context removal and manufactured monstrosity
Every harmful action has a context — historical, political, social, psychological — that does not excuse the action but is necessary for understanding it. The villain slot strips this context away. The terrorist's actions are reported without the colonial history that shaped their political formation. The criminal's actions are reported without the poverty, trauma, or structural failure that shaped their life. This does not mean the context excuses the harm — it means the harm cannot be understood, and therefore cannot be addressed, without it. Context removal produces villains that are satisfying to hate and useless to learn from.
The villain's legitimate interests
Perhaps the most consequential effect of the villain slot is that it makes it narratively impossible to acknowledge that the villain has legitimate interests. This forecloses negotiated resolutions, makes policy options that would address those interests invisible, and ensures that conflicts continue because the conditions producing them cannot be acknowledged. The history of successful conflict resolution almost always involves the discovery — awkward, politically costly, narratively disruptive — that the designated villain had interests that were real, and that addressing them was the only path to ending the conflict.
When I finally read about the history from the other side — not to agree with it, just to understand it — I realised I had been given a cartoon. A cartoon designed to make certain responses feel obvious. The actual situation was nothing like the cartoon.
— workshop participant, former military intelligence analyst

Module 04 · Slide 04

The Victim Slot

The victim slot generates the emotional engine of any narrative. It is the figure whose suffering makes the hero's action necessary, the villain's action condemnable, and the audience's emotional response legitimate. The victim is not simply the person who was harmed — they are the person whose harm is made visible, rendered humanly, and placed at the centre of the story in a way that produces the specific emotional response the narrative requires.

The victim slot is among the most contested positions in public discourse, because it determines whose suffering counts — whose pain generates policy responses, media attention, public grief, and political will. And because it is contested, it is among the most strategically deployed. The assignment of victimhood is always a political act, even when it appears to be a humanitarian one.

Central observation
Whose suffering is made visible and whose is not is one of the most consequential editorial decisions in any media environment — and it is almost never presented as a decision at all. It presents itself as obvious: of course these are the victims. That obviousness is the politics.
The hierarchy of grievability
Not all deaths, not all suffering, not all displacement generate equal media coverage or equivalent emotional responses. The deaths of people from wealthy, Western, culturally proximate countries receive extensive coverage; the deaths of comparable numbers of people from poorer, more distant, less culturally familiar places receive little. This differential is not about the objective significance of the suffering — it is about the hierarchy of who counts as a full victim, whose suffering is treated as fully human and therefore fully grievable. The hierarchy is not stated. It is performed, every day, through coverage decisions.
The victim who cannot also be an agent
The victim slot, like the villain slot, collapses complexity. A person assigned the victim role in a narrative cannot simultaneously be an agent with choices, a person with complicated history, or a participant in conditions that contributed to their situation. The pure victim — innocent, passive, fully deserving of sympathy — is narratively useful but analytically limiting. Real people who have been genuinely harmed are also agents, also have complex histories, and also exist within conditions they have shaped as well as been shaped by. Acknowledging this is not victim-blaming. It is what understanding requires.
Strategic victimhood claims
Because the victim slot generates sympathy, resources, and political permission, it is actively competed for by actors who have interests in occupying it. Governments frame their military actions as responses to victimisation. Corporations frame regulation as persecution. Dominant cultural groups frame demographic change as cultural victimisation. These claims are not always false — genuine victimhood is real. But the competition for the victim slot means it cannot be accepted uncritically. The question is always: what follows from this victimhood claim, and whose interests does the assignment serve?

Module 04 · Slide 05

You Are Always Being Cast

The role system does not only assign parts to politicians, corporations, and foreign governments. It assigns parts to you — the audience. Every narrative positions its audience somewhere in the story: as members of the threatened in-group (your way of life is under attack), as righteous opponents of the villain (your anger is justified), as potential heroes (your action is needed), or as witnesses whose sympathy is being recruited for a cause.

Accepting the role assigned to you in a narrative is not a neutral act. It shapes how you interpret subsequent events, which responses feel obvious, which solutions feel impossible, and whose interests you find yourself serving through your emotional and political responses. The casting happens before you have evaluated the narrative — and its effects persist even after you have noticed what happened.

Central observation
The question to ask of any narrative is not only "who is the hero and who is the villain?" It is "what role is this story assigning to me — and what does accepting that role commit me to?"
The threatened in-group
The most common audience role in political and media narrative is membership of a threatened in-group: your community, your culture, your way of life, your economic security is under threat from the designated villain. This role assignment is effective because the threat activates genuine concern for things that matter — community, culture, security are real values. The role does not create these values; it hijacks them, directing the concern they generate toward the villain assignment rather than toward a full analysis of what the actual threat is and where it comes from.
The righteous opponent
Once a villain has been assigned, the audience is often positioned as the righteous opponent — the person whose anger at the villain is morally justified, politically necessary, and socially approved. This role is emotionally satisfying because it converts frustration into legitimated outrage and individual feeling into collective purpose. It is also analytically damaging: righteous opposition to a designated villain does not require understanding the situation, because the villain's culpability is already established. The role provides purpose without requiring thought.
The witness whose sympathy is recruited
Humanitarian and advocacy narratives often position the audience as witnesses to suffering — people whose sympathy for the assigned victim is being recruited in service of a specific response. This role generates genuine compassion for genuine suffering. It also guides that compassion toward pre-determined conclusions: this is the response that will help, this is the actor that will rescue, this is the solution that the suffering requires. The compassion is real; its direction has been shaped before it arrived.
Scenario · Slide 05
A political advertisement shows images of crime and disorder, then states: "Your family is not safe. [Candidate] will restore order." What role is being assigned to the viewer, and what does accepting that role commit them to?
Module 04 · Slide 06

The Rescuer Slot

Every narrative that assigns a victim needs a rescuer — the figure whose intervention will resolve the threat, end the suffering, and restore order. The rescuer slot is one of the most politically consequential positions in the narrative system, because it authorises specific actors to act on behalf of the victim and specific responses as the appropriate solution.

The rescuer is not simply the person who helps. They are the person the narrative has pre-authorised as the legitimate agent of resolution — which means that other potential responses, other potential actors, and other potential paths to resolution are positioned as inadequate, misguided, or threatening. The rescuer slot determines what counts as help and what counts as interference.

Central observation
The rescuer slot is where the narrative's politics become most explicit — because it names who has the right to act, in what way, on whose behalf. Whoever controls the rescuer casting controls the solution space.
Military intervention as rescue narrative
The humanitarian intervention narrative follows a precise template: a victim population (rendered as helpless, sympathetic, fully innocent), a villain (rendered as purely malevolent, unresponsive to negotiation), and a rescuer (the intervening power, positioned as acting from pure humanitarian concern). This narrative structure pre-authorises military action by casting it as rescue rather than as intervention with its own interests and likely consequences. The template has been used to justify interventions whose actual effects on the victim population were deeply harmful — because the narrative's function was to authorise the action, not to accurately predict its consequences.
The expert as designated rescuer
In technical and policy narratives, the rescuer slot is often occupied by the designated expert — the economist, the medical authority, the security specialist — whose intervention is framed as the appropriate and sufficient response to the problem. This positioning forecloses lay participation, community-based solutions, and approaches from outside the expert's framework. The expert's authority is real and often legitimate. The narrative function of the rescuer slot is to make that authority seem not just useful but exclusively appropriate — and to make other approaches seem dangerously amateurish.
Corporate rescue narratives
Commercial entities regularly occupy the rescuer slot in narratives about social problems. Technology companies are positioned as rescuers from inefficiency and disconnection. Pharmaceutical companies are positioned as rescuers from disease. Financial companies are positioned as rescuers from poverty and exclusion. These rescue narratives serve the commercial interests of the rescuer while positioning structural solutions — regulation, public provision, redistribution — as threats to the rescue operation. The rescuer's business model becomes the solution, and anything that interferes with the business model becomes the obstacle.

Module 04 · Slide 07

When Good People Are Cast as Villains

The villain slot is not reserved for people who do genuinely harmful things. It is a narrative tool available to whoever controls the story, and it has been used systematically against people who threaten power rather than people who exercise it harmfully: whistleblowers, investigative journalists, dissidents, reformers, union organisers, inconvenient scientists, community activists.

The same three operations apply regardless of whether the target is genuinely harmful or genuinely threatening to power. Dehumanisation: their personal failings, real or invented, are foregrounded. Context removal: their actions are stripped of the institutional failures, abuses, or injustices they were responding to. Total agency: they are positioned as the problem, rather than as people responding to a problem. The result is the same whether the target deserves the slot or not — which is why recognising the operations is more important than trusting the assignment.

Central observation
The villain slot is assigned most reliably not to the most harmful actors but to the most threatening ones — and threatening power and threatening people are different things. Learning to distinguish them is one of the most important things media literacy can offer.
The whistleblower as villain
Whistleblowers who expose institutional wrongdoing are almost invariably assigned the villain slot by the institutions they expose. Their personal history is excavated for failings. Their motives are questioned. Their character is attacked. The wrongdoing they revealed is backgrounded while their own actions are foregrounded. The media often participates in this construction — because institutions are better-resourced sources than individuals, because the institutional narrative is more convenient, and because the whistleblower's story requires complexity that is harder to tell than the character-attack story the institution provides.
The dissident as unstable or dangerous
People who challenge dominant narratives — political dissidents, counter-cultural voices, people who question official accounts — are routinely assigned villain-adjacent roles: the conspiracy theorist, the radical, the troublemaker. This role assignment performs a specific function: it removes the content of their challenge from serious consideration by relocating the problem from what they are saying to who they are. The question of whether their claims are accurate or their analysis is sound is replaced by a question about their character, sanity, or motives. The challenge is neutralised without being engaged.
The reverse villain slot: heroes of convenience
The hero slot can be assigned equally strategically. People who serve power are elevated to hero status — scientists who provide convenient findings, journalists who amplify preferred narratives, dissidents from enemy states whose criticism serves geopolitical purposes. The hero slot, like the villain slot, is assigned based on narrative function, not character assessment. Someone can be assigned the hero slot by one narrative and the villain slot by another covering the same actions — depending entirely on whose interests the assignment serves in each case.

Module 04 · Slide 08

Reading the Casting

The practical skill this module is building is the ability to identify role assignments in narratives before accepting them — to see the casting decisions as decisions, rather than as obvious descriptions of reality. This does not mean refusing to have moral responses to events. It means ensuring those responses are produced by genuine assessment rather than by narrative positioning that happened before assessment began.

Reading the casting requires a specific set of questions applied to any narrative you encounter. The questions are not complex. They are just consistently avoided, because the roles feel like facts, and questioning facts feels like either naivety or bad faith. The discomfort of asking them is a feature, not a problem — it is the feeling of refusing to accept a part without reading the script.

Central observation
You are not obligated to play the role a narrative assigns you. Noticing the assignment — seeing that a role has been offered, and pausing before accepting it — is the first and most important act of narrative independence.
The four casting questions
Four questions, applied to any narrative, begin to make the casting visible. First: who is being humanised and who is being presented as a category? Second: whose suffering is being rendered visible and whose is absent? Third: what role am I being offered in this story, and what does accepting it commit me to? Fourth: who controls this narrative, and whose interests does the role assignment serve? These questions do not produce alternative facts. They produce awareness of the choices that produced the story you are inside.
Humanising the villain as analysis, not excuse
One of the most reliable ways to test a villain construction is to deliberately seek out the villain's own account — their stated reasons, their historical context, their legitimate grievances. This is not the same as accepting their account as accurate or treating their actions as justified. It is the analytic practice of restoring the complexity that the villain slot removed. A person whose actions you understand — contextually, historically, motivationally — is someone you can think about accurately. A pure villain is someone you can only react to. Understanding is more useful than reaction in almost every practical context.
The role you were assigned in your own story
The most personal application of this module is the examination of the role you have been assigned in the narratives about your own life — your family's story, your community's story, your political identity's story. Who is the villain of the story you were raised inside? Who is the hero? What role were you given, and what does that role commit you to emotionally and politically? These are the most durable castings, the ones that feel least like narratives and most like reality — and therefore the ones that most deserve the questions this module has been practising.

✓

Module 04 complete.

You have examined the narrative role system and how it operates in news, political messaging, and the stories we live inside: the hero slot and how it determines whose interests the story serves; the villain slot and the three operations that construct it; the victim slot and the politics of whose suffering counts; the rescuer slot and how it pre-authorises specific solutions; how you are always being cast; how good people are assigned villain roles to protect power; and the four questions that let you read the casting before accepting the part.

What you carry forward
  1. 01The hero slot determines whose interests the story serves. Whoever occupies it receives the narrative’s moral energy: their suffering counts, their agency is foregrounded, their goals structure the resolution.
  2. 02The villain slot is constructed through three operations: attribution of intention, decontextualisation of action, and denial of legitimate grievance. All three are rhetorical moves, not factual findings.
  3. 03The victim slot is political. Whose suffering counts, whose trauma receives the narrative’s attention, whose loss authorises a response — these are editorial decisions that reflect power, not simply proportion.
  4. 04The rescuer slot pre-authorises a specific solution. Whoever is cast as rescuer arrives with the solution already implied by their role. The narrative decides what help looks like before the problem is examined.
  5. 05You are always being cast. The role assigned to you by a narrative determines whether your actions are read as heroism or villainy, regardless of their content.
  6. 06Good people are assigned villain roles to protect power. The role assignment is a structural move that requires no malice and no conspiracy — only that the person challenges something the narrative has designated as legitimate.
Practice this week

Take one political or social story you hold strong views on. Write down who occupies each role slot in the version you believe — hero, villain, victim, rescuer. Then write the same story with the hero and villain slots swapped. You do not have to believe the swap. You have to write it seriously enough that it holds together. Note what becomes visible that was invisible before.

Monthly: for one recurring conflict you follow — political, cultural, or personal — identify which slot you habitually occupy in your own telling of it. Ask what the story looks like from the slot you do not occupy. The answer is not that you are wrong. It is that the story is larger than your position inside it.
