Page 01 / 04 · Youth as Assets

From risk to resource.

Curricula on youth violence usually end with ‘what to do with offenders’. This one ends elsewhere: with the evidence that young people are not merely the problem to be managed, but the most powerful agents of their own communities' recovery.

Positive Youth Development (PYD) reframes young people from a bundle of risks to be reduced into a set of assets to be developed. Rather than asking only ‘how do we stop them offending?’, it asks ‘how do we build their competence, confidence, connection, character, and contribution?’ In post-conflict and high-violence settings, PYD-informed work treats youth as partners and leaders — and finds that engaging their agency produces better outcomes than treating them purely as targets of control.

This is not naïve optimism; it is a structural claim. Young people have unmatched access to, and credibility within, the very networks where violence spreads — which makes them uniquely effective as interrupters, mentors, and norm-changers. The same peer influence that can transmit violence (Module 1) can transmit its opposite. Mobilising youth leadership turns the contagion's own vector toward peace.

Why agency matters

Interventions done to young people tend to underperform interventions done with them. Ownership generates legitimacy and durability: a peace a community's youth helped build is one they have reason to defend. The shift from object to agent is therefore not just ethical but effective — it recruits the population's own capacity into the solution, at the levels where outside actors have least reach.

The peer networks that carry violence are the same ones that can carry its cure. The question is who is leading them — and the most credible leaders are young.

Page 02 / 04 · Reintegration

Bringing young people back.

For youth who have been part of armed groups, the decisive challenge is reintegration. The international framework — disarmament, demobilisation, and reintegration (DDR) — succeeds or fails on whether the ‘R’ is taken seriously.

DDR programmes aim to move former combatants out of armed groups and back into civilian life. The first two steps — collecting weapons and formally disbanding fighters — are comparatively straightforward. The hard, decisive part is reintegration: rebuilding a viable civilian identity, livelihood, and place in the community. Where reintegration is underfunded or rushed, demobilised youth are left with the same vacuum that drew them in — no income, no status, no belonging — and re-recruitment becomes the rational option once again, exactly as Module 2 predicts.

Reintegration is hardest precisely because of stigma. Communities may fear or reject returning youth, and that rejection is itself a documented driver of re-recruitment — it confirms that the armed group is the only place that will have them. Effective reintegration therefore works on two sides at once: equipping the young person with livelihood, education, and psychosocial support, and preparing the community to receive them, often through the restorative processes covered next.

Identity, not just employment

As with recruitment, reintegration that offers only a job underperforms, because the armed group supplied identity and meaning too. Durable reintegration must answer ‘who am I now?’ as well as ‘how do I earn?’ — providing a respected civilian identity to replace the combatant one. This is why reintegration is fundamentally a social and psychological project, not merely an economic transaction.

Disarming a young person is an afternoon's work. Giving them a self worth more than the one the group offered is the whole task — and the only thing that lasts.

Page 03 / 04 · Restorative Systems

Accountability without disposal.

The course closes where punitive systems end and a different logic begins: restorative justice, which pursues accountability and repair through community-led process rather than through mass incarceration and exclusion.

Restorative justice asks a different set of questions than the punitive system. Not only ‘what rule was broken and what punishment applies?’ but ‘who was harmed, what do they need, and whose obligation is it to repair it?’ It brings together those affected — including, where appropriate, the person responsible — in a structured, facilitated process aimed at acknowledgement, repair, and reintegration. Accountability is central, but it is accountability oriented toward repair and changed behaviour rather than toward exclusion.

This matters for youth violence specifically because the dominant alternative — mass incarceration — performs poorly on the course's own terms. It removes individuals while leaving every structural driver intact, exposes young people to further violence and criminal networks, and attaches a stigma that forecloses the legitimate pathways whose absence drove the behaviour in the first place. It is, in the ecological language, an intervention that often worsens the very conditions it claims to address. Restorative models aim instead to repair harm while keeping young people connected to the community that can change them.

The thread that ties the course together

Restorative justice is the natural endpoint of everything before it. If violence is produced by structural conditions, transmitted through exposure, and rooted in unmet needs for belonging and dignity, then a response built on exclusion and disposal works against the grain of the evidence. A response built on repair, reintegration, and changed conditions works with it. The whole arc — from public health to political economy to gender, digital systems, environment, and peacebuilding — converges here: build the conditions for non-violence, and respond to harm in ways that restore rather than discard.

Punishment asks which rule was broken. Restoration asks who was harmed and how to repair it. Only the second question can also prevent the next harm.
